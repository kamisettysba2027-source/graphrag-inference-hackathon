[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
Resistant hypertension (RH) affects approximately 10–15% of treated hypertensive adults and up to 50% of individuals with chronic kidney disease (CKD). Defined by persistent elevation of blood pressure despite multidrug therapy, RH reflects inadequate therapeutic control and is associated with accelerated renal function decline, increased cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, and more than US$10 billion in excess annual healthcare costs. Growing evidence indicates that, beyond sodium retention and activation of vasoconstrictor pathways, chronic endothelial dysfunction and maladaptive microvascular remodelling – particularly in CKD-associated RH – are increasingly recognized contributors to disease progression. The vascular endothelium regulates arterial tone through three major vasodilatory systems: nitric oxide (NO), prostacyclin (PGI 2 ), and endothelium-dependent hyperpolarization (EDH). Pharmacologic agents targeting individual pathways have demonstrated short-term vasodilatory effects but have not consistently translated into durable endothelial recovery or microvascular repair in clinical practice. Therapeutic strategies capable of coordinated engagement of all three pathways may therefore be required to modify the vascular abnormalities underlying RH. Human tissue kallikrein-1 (KLK1) and its recombinant analogue, rinvecalinase alfa (DM199), represent a promising approach. In cardiovascular and renal disease models, KLK1 supplementation restores physiological generation of kinin peptides, which, by binding vascular and renal B 2  receptors, stimulate NO, PGI 2 , and EDH signalling pathways, thereby promoting vasorelaxation and natriuresis. Moreover, sustained KLK1 therapy has been associated with improved microvascular integrity and angiogenic signalling through both kinin-dependent and independent mechanisms. Emerging Phase II clinical evidence with DM199 in CKD populations demonstrates blood pressure reduction together with stabilization of renal function and reductions in albuminuria, suggesting potential downstream effects on microvascular structure and function. Importantly, these effects are achieved with a favourable electrolyte and safety profile, supporting investigation in CKD-associated RH where standard therapies often fail. This review integrates contemporary treatment perspectives with mechanistic and clinical evidence supporting KLK1 augmentation as a chronic, endothelial-supportive strategy targeting microvascular remodelling in CKD-associated RH.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Resistant hypertension (RH), defined as blood pressure (BP) remaining above target despite treatment with ≥3 antihypertensive agents of different classes, including a diuretic, affects approximately 10–15% of treated hypertensive adults. Importantly, RH represents a heterogeneous clinical syndrome rather than a single disease entity, encompassing essential, neurogenic, and volume-dependent forms, as well as renal-driven hypertension [ 1 , 2 ]. Among these phenotypes, chronic kidney disease (CKD)–associated RH constitutes one of the most prevalent and highest-risk subgroups, associated with disproportionate cardiovascular burden and substantial healthcare utilization and costs [ 3 , 4 ].
Resistance to antihypertensive therapy reflects the convergence of multiple pathogenic mechanisms, including heightened sympathetic activity, aldosterone excess, and renal sodium and volume retention; in CKD-associated RH, these processes are further amplified by uremic oxidative stress, blunted pressure–natriuresis response, and tubulointerstitial inflammation [ 5 ]. In addition, a key and incompletely addressed contributor in CKD-associated RH is endothelial dysfunction, characterized by reduced nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability, diminished prostacyclin (PGI 2 ) production, and impaired endothelium-dependent hyperpolarization (EDH) [ 6 ]. These abnormalities impair vasorelaxation and promote progressive microvascular remodelling in the kidney and heart, characterized by capillary rarefaction, weakened endothelial–pericyte interactions, defective angiogenic signalling, and dysregulated extracellular matrix turnover [ 7 , 8 ]. Capillary dropout, particularly within the renal microcirculation where vascular integrity is essential for pressure–natriuresis coupling, contributes to progressive kidney and cardiovascular deterioration [ 9 ].
Current guideline-based therapies – including renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics – as well as emerging approaches such as renal denervation and angiotensinogen-targeting siRNA, primarily target neurohormonal activation and volume-dependent mechanisms [ 10 – 12 ]. While effective in many patients, these interventions do not directly target endothelial dysfunction or the microvascular abnormalities that underlie RH, and their use in CKD patients may be limited by declining renal reserve and the risk of hyperkalaemia [ 13 – 15 ].
Against this backdrop, this review illustrates a triple-pathway endothelial modulation strategy based on the coordinated enhancement of NO, PGI 2 , and EDH signalling. Within this framework, human tissue kallikrein-1 (KLK1) – and its recombinant analogue, rinvecalinase alfa (DM199) – is discussed here as a candidate biological strategy. By restoring physiologic generation of kinin peptides, which may be reduced in CKD-associated RH, this naturally occurring enzyme activates receptors expressed in vascular and renal tissues, thereby stimulating all three vasorelaxant pathways simultaneously [ 16 ]. In addition, preclinical evidence suggests that KLK1 delivery supports microvascular stabilization through mechanisms that extend beyond acute vasorelaxation, including kinin-dependent and -independent pathways implicated in extracellular matrix remodelling and angiogenic processes [ 17 ].
Following a concise overview of evolving therapeutic strategies in RH, the review places KLK1 augmentation within this emerging landscape, examining the biological rationale and mechanistic basis alongside preclinical and early clinical evidence relevant to CKD-associated RH.

NOVEL TRENDS IN PHARMACOTHERAPY FOR RESISTANT HYPERTENSION: TOWARD ORGAN PROTECTION:
Following decades of therapeutic stagnation, several new drug classes have emerged for the treatment of RH across diverse aetiologies and its cardiorenal complications. Recent advances reflect a shift away from broad suppression of vasoconstrictor pathways toward more precise, mechanism-guided strategies to protect vascular and renal structure and function. This evolving paradigm includes nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (nsMRAs) such as finerenone, aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs), endothelin pathway modulators, and sodium–glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors. Collectively, these developments underscore a transition from pressure-centric management to organ-protective therapy, emphasizing restoration of vascular and renal homeostasis rather than simply lowering systemic resistance [ 12 ].

ALDOSTERONE-DRIVEN MECHANISMS IN RESISTANT HYPERTENSION:
Among the mechanisms perpetuating RH, excessive aldosterone signalling remains particularly influential. Beyond its classical actions on sodium retention and potassium excretion, aldosterone accelerates inflammation, fibrosis, and vascular remodelling through both mineralocorticoid receptor (MR)-dependent and nongenomic pathways [ 18 ]. Elevated urinary aldosterone independently predicts cardiorenal–metabolic events, with excretion rates rising across body mass index quartiles, further supporting its mechanistic importance in the increasingly prevalent obese population [ 19 ]. Despite treatment with angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), aldosterone breakthrough frequently occurs and contributes to persistent hypertension and residual vascular and renal risk [ 20 ].

NONSTEROIDAL MINERALOCORTICOID RECEPTOR ANTAGONISTS:
nsMRAs selectively inhibit MR coactivator binding, offering anti-inflammatory and antifibrotic benefits with a lower risk of hyperkalaemia than spironolactone [ 21 ]. Finerenone, approved in 2021, provided significant cardiorenal protection in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes in the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials, with modest BP reductions but meaningful decreases in albuminuria and slower decline in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) [ 22 , 23 ]. Esaxerenone, approved in Japan, lowered systolic and diastolic BP in a dose-dependent manner, with powerful nocturnal effects [ 24 ]. In contrast, ocedurenone did not meet its primary endpoint in the Phase 3 CLARION-CKD trial, highlighting variability in antihypertensive efficacy within the nsMRA class [ 25 ].

ALDOSTERONE SYNTHASE INHIBITORS:
ASIs directly suppress aldosterone production by selectively blocking CYP11B2, aiming to reduce BP while avoiding MR antagonist–related side effects [ 26 ]. Lorundrostat and baxdrostat are the leading candidates. In the Target-HTN trial, lorundrostat produced systolic BP reductions of 7.8–9.6 mmHg vs. placebo in adults with uncontrolled hypertension on at least two antihypertensive medications, with a low incidence of hyperkalemia (~5%) [ 27 , 28 ]. Baxdrostat demonstrated approximately 100 : 1 selectivity for CYP11B2 over cortisol-synthesizing enzymes. In a multicentre placebo-controlled trial in RH, baxdrostat reduced systolic BP by 8 and 11 mmHg (1 mg and 2 mg doses) with good tolerability and limited electrolyte disturbances; hyperkalemia ≥6.0 mmol/l occurred in a small number of patients, resolved with withdrawal, and did not recur upon rechallenge [ 29 ]. Phase 3 results confirmed these antihypertensive effects, with relative reductions of −8.7 and −9.8 mmHg for the 1 mg and 2 mg doses, respectively [ 30 ].
Despite these promising results, ASIs are not yet established for patients with advanced CKD-associated RH, because patients with advanced CKD and those at high risk of hyperkalemia were largely excluded from clinical trials. Safety concerns and short trial durations, primarily focused on surrogate endpoints such as BP and albuminuria, have contributed to limiting regulatory approval.

ENDOTHELIN RECEPTOR ANTAGONISTS:
Aprocitentan, a dual ETA/ETB receptor antagonist, demonstrated durable BP lowering in the PRECISION Phase 3 study of patients with RH on triple therapy [ 31 ]. The drug was generally well tolerated, with dose-dependent oedema as the most frequent side effect. Sparsentan, a dual endothelin and angiotensin II receptor blocker, significantly reduced proteinuria and kidney inflammation in immunoglobulin A (IgA) nephropathy and focal segmental glomerulosclerosis, leading to U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval in 2023 [ 32 ].

SODIUM–GLUCOSE COTRANSPORTER-2 INHIBITORS:
SGLT2 inhibitors have emerged as cornerstone therapies for cardiorenal protection, extending well beyond glycaemic control. Agents such as dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, and canagliflozin reduce plasma volume, arterial stiffness, and sympathetic activity, resulting in modest (3–5 mmHg) but sustained BP reductions in patients with or without diabetes [ 33 – 38 ]. The DAPA-CKD and EMPA-KIDNEY trials demonstrated slowed CKD progression, reduced heart failure hospitalizations, and benefits independent of glucose lowering. Mechanistically, SGLT2 inhibition restores tubuloglomerular feedback, lowers intraglomerular pressure, and reduces inflammation and oxidative stress within the renal microvasculature [ 35 ]. These agents also counteract aldosterone overactivation and enhance responsiveness to RAAS inhibitors, making them particularly attractive in CKD-associated RH.

TRANSITIONING TO A MECHANISTIC SOLUTION:
Collectively, these therapeutic advances reflect a shift toward mechanism-based and organ-protective management of RH. Nevertheless, a substantial proportion of patients with CKD-associated RH remain inadequately controlled despite optimized therapy, underscoring a persistent therapeutic gap. This unmet need has stimulated growing interest in strategies aimed at restoring endothelial signalling and microvascular function – an area that remains only partially addressed by current pharmacologic approaches. Therefore, the following section examines a complementary biological framework centred on coordinated modulation of endogenous vasodilatory pathways.

PHARMACOLOGICAL MODULATION OF VASORELAXATION IN RESISTANT HYPERTENSION:
Endothelial regulation of arterial tone relies on three principal vasodilator pathways. NO activates soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC), generating cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP) that promotes smooth-muscle relaxation. PGI 2  stimulates cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)-dependent pathways and inhibits platelet aggregation, while EDH spreads electrical hyperpolarization through calcium-activated potassium (KCa) channels to induce vasorelaxation.
In RH, particularly when accompanied by CKD, this vasodilatory equilibrium deteriorates. Vasoconstrictor pathways are upregulated, whereas endothelial vasodilator activity is markedly impaired. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenge NO, blunt sGC sensitivity, and disrupt KCa-channel signalling [ 39 – 41 ]. At the same time, endothelial prostacyclin (PGI 2 ) bioavailability is reduced as a consequence of both diminished constitutive COX-1–dependent PGI 2  synthesis and a relative shift toward inducible COX-2–derived vasoconstrictor prostanoids, including thromboxane A 2 , particularly under conditions of CKD-related inflammation and oxidative stress [ 42 ]. The resulting loss of vascular homeostasis sustains elevated peripheral vascular resistance, promotes sodium retention, and accelerates target-organ injury.
Despite this mechanistic rationale, vasodilators remain underutilized. In the American College of Cardiology PINNACLE registry, only 13.9% of patients with RH were prescribed a vasodilator, and these agents were absent from all commonly used 3-, 4-, or 5-drug regimens [ 43 ]. These findings suggest potential underuse of vasodilatory strategies in clinical practice.
Several pharmacologic agents exert direct vasorelaxant effects but are constrained by safety or practicality. Sodium nitroprusside – the prototypical intravenous (i.v.) NO donor – is restricted to hypertensive emergencies due to the risk of cyanide toxicity, particularly in patients with renal or hepatic impairment. Organic nitrates such as nitroglycerin and isosorbide dinitrate are primarily indicated for angina and have limited applicability to systemic hypertension. More recently, attention has shifted downstream to NO signalling. sGC stimulators (e.g., vericiguat, riociguat) enhance NO–cGMP pathways and demonstrate vasodilatory and antifibrotic effects in experimental hypertension, although clinical data in RH remain limited [ 44 – 46 ]. PGI 2  analogues – including epoprostenol and treprostinil – promote cAMP-mediated vasodilation but are approved only for pulmonary arterial hypertension because of their short half-life and systemic adverse effects [ 47 ]. Hydralazine – a phthalazine-derived, direct-acting arterial vasodilator – has been considered a potential adjunctive therapy. However, its requirement for three-times-daily dosing and relatively modest antihypertensive efficacy limit its practicality and overall effectiveness in achieving BP control.
The potassium-channel opener minoxidil has regained interest as an adjunct in RH. By inducing membrane hyperpolarization, it produces potent arteriolar vasodilation but may cause reflex tachycardia, volume expansion, and RAAS activation, necessitating concurrent β-blocker and diuretic therapy [ 48 , 49 ]. Experimental microperfusion studies show that minoxidil enhances tubular cotransporter activity in the loop of Henle, increasing Na + , Cl − , and K +  reabsorption and contributing to sodium retention [ 50 ]. Reports of pericardial effusion and acute kidney injury – particularly in patients with CKD or malignant hypertension – underscore the need for careful selection and monitoring [ 48 ].
Beyond currently available agents, soluble epoxide hydrolase (sEH) inhibitors, which stabilize epoxyeicosatrienoic acids (EETs) and enhance EDH-mediated signalling, have improved microvascular function and renal perfusion in preclinical hypertension models [ 51 ]. However, these compounds remain investigational and are not yet approved for RH.
Importantly, while the above agents lower vascular tone with varying potency, there is currently no strong clinical evidence that they induce sustained reversal of microvascular remodelling or restore endothelial structure in RH, particularly in CKD-associated disease. TOWARD COORDINATED MODULATION OF ENDOTHELIAL VASODILATOR PATHWAYS:

A more integrated strategy aimed at restoring vascular responsiveness and renal microcirculatory function may enhance BP control while preserving autoregulatory capacity. Even when advanced endothelial dysfunction constrains acute responsiveness, sustained modulation of complementary vasorelaxant signalling may help re-establish functional equilibrium over time. Organization and regulation of the kallikrein–kinin system underlying endothelial triple-pathway signalling:
Translating coordinated endothelial pathway modulation into durable clinical benefit requires careful consideration of how and where endogenous regulatory systems operate in vivo. In RH, particularly in the setting of CKD, disruption of local vasodilator signalling within the renal and microvascular compartments underscores the importance of understanding tissue-level organization.
One pivotal mechanism in this context is the kallikrein–kinin system (KKS), which functions predominantly through autocrine and paracrine signalling [ 52 ]. KLK1 is a secreted serine protease that promotes local generation of bioactive kinins – primarily bradykinin (BK) and kallidin (Lys-BK) – by cleaving low-molecular-weight kininogen, thereby acting as an enzymatic amplifier of endogenous kinin signalling. Physiological kinin activity reflects the balance between local KLK1 expression and rapid degradation by kininases, including angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) and aminopeptidase P. BK acts predominantly through the constitutively expressed bradykinin B 2  receptor (B2R), activating downstream endothelial signalling through calcium-dependent mechanisms, endothelial hyperpolarization, and smooth-muscle relaxation [ 53 ]. This restricted organization to sites of generation confers tissue specificity to KLK1-dependent signalling.
The existence of an intrinsic vascular KKS is supported by studies demonstrating local expression of KLK1, kininogen, and B 2  receptors within endothelial and vascular smooth muscle cells. Moreover, experimental evidence indicates that inhibition or genetic disruption of this pathway alters endothelial vasodilatory signalling, angiogenesis, and microvascular integrity independent of circulating kinins [ 54 , 55 ]. In the kidney, KLK1 is expressed in distal nephron segments adjacent to the RAAS, supporting a functional counter-regulatory relationship that favours vasodilation and natriuresis [ 56 , 57 ]. Luminal kinins inhibit sodium transport through effects on sodium chloride cotransporter (NCC) and the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC), independently of aldosterone. In addition, interstitial kinins influence medullary blood flow, glomerular hemodynamics, and oxygen delivery [ 58 – 60 ]. Kinin signalling also contributes to podocyte integrity and glomerular barrier function [ 61 ]. Taken together, these compartmentalized vascular and renal actions position the endogenous KKS as an integrative regulator of endothelial and glomerular-tubular homeostasis, providing a mechanistic framework for therapeutic strategies aimed at restoring coordinated vasodilatory signalling in CKD-associated RH.

Inducers and depressors of endogenous human tissue kallikrein-1:
Endogenous KLK1 expression varies widely due to genetic and environmental factors. Urinary KLK1 excretion, reflecting renal synthesis, is heritable and influenced by dietary potassium intake and genetic polymorphisms [ 62 – 64 ]. Specific promoter variants and coding mutations have been associated with reduced KLK1 activity, increased salt sensitivity, and an augmented risk of hypertension and kidney disease in some populations [ 65 – 67 ]. In CKD, KLK1 expression declines with nephron loss and correlates with higher BP and disease progression [ 68 ]. Potassium-induced upregulation of KLK1 is attenuated in individuals carrying reduced-function variants [ 68 – 70 ].

Rationale for human tissue kallikrein-1 augmentation and difference from angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibition:
KLK1 possesses several properties that support its consideration as an adjunctive approach to achieve both short-term and sustained therapeutic outcomes. Exogenous KLK1 supplementation enhances local kinin generation, thereby engaging coordinated vasodilatory signalling. Notably, the EDH-dependent component of this response is particularly relevant in resistance vessels, where kinin-mediated vasodilation remains relatively preserved even when eNOS signalling is downregulated or uncoupled, as frequently observed in CKD-associated RH [ 71 , 72 ]. Sustained activation of these pathways has been associated in preclinical models with improved microvascular stability and reduced fibrotic signalling. Moreover, KLK1 possesses reparative angiogenesis actions that are mediated by the activation of a proteolytic cascade apparently independent of kinin signalling [ 17 ].
There are similarities, but also substantial differences that distinguish KLK1 augmentation from ACE inhibition. ACE inhibitors not only prevent Angiotensin II formation/action in hypertension, but also promote active vasorelaxation primarily by limiting kinin degradation; as a result, their vascular and renal effects are largely dependent on intact endogenous kinin production [ 73 , 74 ], which is reportedly reduced in CKD and salt-sensitive hypertension [ 75 ]. Consistent with this concept, early clinical studies have reported that hypertensive patients with low urinary KLK1 excretion exhibit a diminished BP response to ACE inhibition [ 76 ]. By restoring local kinin generation, exogenous KLK1 supplementation may help overcome this limitation. In addition, the long-term microvascular effects of ACE inhibitors appear to be strictly kinin-dependent [ 77 ].
Selected in vitro studies suggest that KLK1 may induce direct B 2 R activation and redistribution even under kininogen-free conditions, raising the possibility of receptor-level interactions beyond classical kinin signalling [ 78 , 79 ]. In line with this interpretation, experimental studies in kininogen-deficient Brown Norway Katholiek rats subjected to acute coronary artery ligation have reported that local tissue kallikrein administration to the infarct border zone was associated with reduced infarct size, apoptosis, and inflammation, as well as improved postinfarction cardiac function [ 80 ]. The physiological relevance of this mechanism in humans remains uncertain, and the prevailing view is that kininogen-derived kinins continue to represent the principal endogenous activators of the B 2 R.

Preclinical evidence of therapeutic efficacy:
Over the past three decades,  KLK1  gene therapy has been explored in a broad range of cardiovascular, renal, and ischemic models. The earliest studies used naked plasmid delivery of  KLK1  in spontaneously hypertensive rats, demonstrating sustained reductions in BP after a single systemic injection and reversal of the effect by KLK1 inhibition, thereby establishing proof of principle for somatic gene transfer in hypertension [ 81 ]. Subsequent work with adenovirus (Ad) and adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors extended these findings to multiple disease contexts, including salt-sensitive and renovascular hypertension, DOCA-salt models, fructose-induced metabolic hypertension, Goldblatt renovascular hypertension, and chronic renal failure, consistently showing BP lowering together with attenuation of renal injury, cardiac hypertrophy, vascular remodelling, and fibrosis [ 82 – 91 ].
Beyond BP control,  KLK1  gene transfer has also been investigated as an angiogenic and tissue-protective strategy in peripheral limb ischemia, myocardial infarction, and cerebral ischemia–reperfusion. Local or intramuscular delivery of adenoviral  KLK1  constructs enhances neovascularization, restores blood flow, limits infarct size, and improves functional recovery, primarily through B 2 R-dependent activation of Akt, eNOS, and downstream antiapoptotic and pro-angiogenic pathways [ 92 – 96 ]. Mechanistic studies have further clarified the central role of KLK1 in vascular repair and progenitor-cell biology. KLK1 is essential for the invasive and proangiogenic function of circulating progenitor cells, linking KLK1-dependent signalling to endothelial migration, matrix remodelling, and microvascular growth [ 97 ]. More recent work has focused on KLK1-modified endothelial progenitor cells, which combine cell-based repair with KLK1 overexpression and provide augmented protection against ischemic injury [ 98 ].
Taken together, these findings support the concept that KLK1-based strategies may complement conventional antihypertensive therapy by targeting microvascular biology rather than blood pressure reduction alone. Clinical experience with human tissue kallikrein-1 crude preparations:
Porcine-derived tissue kallikrein has been used clinically in Asia for cerebrovascular and renal indications, although much of the supporting evidence originates from regional studies. Across randomized trials, phase IV investigations, and observational cohorts, several thousand patients have been formally evaluated, most commonly in acute ischemic stroke, with reported evidence of improved neurological outcomes in selected reports [ 99 – 102 ]. Broader real-world exposure has likely occurred through routine clinical practice; however, the overall number of treated patients remains uncertain and available safety data are largely limited to short-term follow-up. Heterogeneity in study design, reporting standards, and endpoints precluded definitive conclusions regarding efficacy or long-term risk. Nevertheless, these early clinical observations provided proof of biological activity and supported the subsequent development of standardized recombinant KLK1 formulations.

A step forward toward standardized therapy: DM199:
To address variability and scalability limitations of native preparations, a recombinant form of KLK1, DM199 (rinvecalinase alfa), was developed (Table  4 ). Clinical development has progressed into multiple human studies using intravenous (i.v.) and subcutaneous (s.c.) administration, initially to assess pharmacokinetics (PK). Both administration modes produced predictable, dose-proportional exposure with low interindividual variability and no evidence of accumulation [ 103 ]. i.v. administration produces a rapid rise in circulating KLK1 levels and immediate systemic exposure, with a relatively short distribution half-life, making it useful when prompt vasodilation is desired. s.c. administration provides slower absorption and more prolonged exposure, yielding smooth, sustained plasma concentrations with reduced peak-to-trough fluctuation—an ideal profile for chronic therapy. Clearance appears to occur mainly via proteolytic pathways rather than renal filtration. The PK profile of DM199 results in predictable pharmacodynamic activation of endothelial vasodilator pathways, providing a pharmacokinetic rationale for sustained engagement of endothelial signalling pathways.

DM199 clinical trials in acute ischaemic stroke:
The clinical development of DM199 has advanced into cerebrovascular trials assessing safety, tolerability, and initial signals of efficacy. The Phase II randomized study ReMEDy1 ( NCT03290560 ) enrolled 92 patients with acute ischemic stroke (AIS) with National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) scores of 6–25 within 24 h of onset [ 104 ]. Patients were randomized 1 : 1 to DM199 (1 μg/kg i.v. followed by 3 μg/kg s.c. every 3 days for 22 days) or placebo on top of standard therapy, except for ACE inhibitors, which were a criterion of patient exclusion from the trial. Serious adverse events occurred in 20/47 (43.5%) DM199-treated patients and in 14/45 (31.1%) placebo-treated patients; common adverse events in the DM199 cohort included constipation (60.9%), oral candidiasis (23.9%), and nausea (17.4%). The trial was not designed to test BP outcomes; hence, no quantitative data are available on this parameter. Interestingly, by Day 90, there were no cases of stroke-in-evolution in the DM199 arm versus 6/45 (13.3%) in the placebo arm (including four deaths). Moreover, although functional outcome differences (NIHSS, modified Rankin Scale, Barthel Index) in the global cohort did not reach statistical significance, a post hoc subgroup analysis excluding patients undergoing endovascular therapy showed that 36% of DM199 recipients achieved an NIHSS score of 0–1, compared with 14% in the placebo group. These data can be treated as a hypothesis-generating signal that DM199 might reduce early recurrent ischemia/progression, possibly via reperfusion through collateral vessels.
Building on these data, the Phase 2/3 adaptive seamless trial ReMEDy2 ( NCT05065216 ) was initiated to enrol 728 AIS patients within 24 h of symptom onset, excluding those eligible for mechanical thrombectomy or those with large-vessel occlusion. Despite a temporary FDA clinical hold in July 2022 14 due to three cases of transient hypotension, the trial resumed with adjusted dosing, and DM199 was granted Fast Track designation for AIS. This trial is expected to clarify the role of KLK1 supplementation in enhancing collateral perfusion via vasodilatory collateral recruitment.

DM199 clinical trials in chronic kidney disease:
The REDUX Phase II programme ( NCT04123613 ) evaluated DM199 across several CKD populations, including African American hypertensive nondiabetic adults, patients with IgA nephropathy, and individuals with type 2 diabetes and hypertension. DM199 was administered s.c. at 2.0 or 5.0 μg/kg twice weekly for 95 days. ACE inhibitors were explicitly excluded, whereas ARBs could be continued as part of standard care.
Although full results have not yet been published in peer-reviewed journals, available data indicate a favourable safety profile, with no drug-related serious adverse events [ 105 , 106 ]. Two patients experienced hypotension, three had injection site pain, and one had erythema. Reported renal benefits include 30–35% reductions in UACR in the IgA nephropathy cohort, stabilization of eGFR across all groups, and reductions in both office and ambulatory BP in hypertensive subjects.
These findings suggest that DM199 provides renal and BP benefits beyond background therapy, including ARBs, and is broadly compatible with standard CKD management. However, the results were not stratified by ARB use, and the study lacked an “ARB-alone” comparator arm. The rationale for combining an ARB with DM199 is supported by a series of Chinese clinical studies in diabetic kidney disease that added pancreatic-derived kallikrein to standard ARB therapy. Across multiple randomized and observational trials using valsartan, losartan, or irbesartan as the RAAS blocker, adjunctive pancreatic kallikrein consistently produced greater reductions in albuminuria and urinary protein excretion, along with improvements in endothelial and inflammatory markers, compared with ARB monotherapy [ 107 ]. Although these studies used porcine pancreatic KLK1 rather than recombinant human KLK1, they provide clinical proof-of-concept that dual RAAS–KKS modulation yields renal benefits beyond RAAS blockade alone. This body of evidence offers a biologically and clinically relevant foundation for evaluating ARB + DM199 as a next-generation combination strategy in CKD.

Safety considerations and potential harms:
Clinical experience with DM199 remains relatively limited: across early-phase trials conducted before the ongoing ReMEDy2 programme, approximately 150–250 individuals have been exposed, including about 45 healthy volunteers, 30–35 patients with acute ischemic stroke, and roughly 80–100 patients with CKD enrolled in REDUX and related renal studies. Although the overall human safety database is still modest compared with more established therapeutic classes, available data indicate a generally favourable tolerability profile.
Across Phase 1 and Phase 2 trials, DM199 has been well tolerated, with most adverse events classified as mild to moderate. Commonly reported events – such as headache, flushing, and gastrointestinal symptoms – are consistent with activation of vasodilatory, kinin-dependent pathways. Importantly, no drug-related serious adverse events have been confirmed in completed programmes, and s.c. administration has not been associated with clinically meaningful hypotension, electrolyte disturbances, cough, or angioedema. In contrast, intravenous dosing in the acute stroke setting identified transient, exposure-related hypotension that resolved with infusion interruption, underscoring the relevance of dose, route of administration, and clinical context.
When viewed alongside extensive clinical experience with ACE inhibitors, the emerging safety profile of DM199 appears broadly comparable and potentially favourable in some respects. Whereas ACE inhibition increases kinin levels by blocking degradation – contributing to cough, oedema, hypotension, and rare angioedema – recombinant KLK1 supplementation aims to restore upstream kinin generation in a controlled, pharmacokinetically guided manner. This raises the hypothesis that KLK1 therapy could allow more physiologic and compartmentalized modulation of kinin signalling rather than indiscriminate systemic amplification; however, such interpretations remain provisional given the limited scale and duration of existing studies.
Definitive evaluation of long-term safety will require larger and longer trials with focused monitoring for kinin-related effects, including hypotension, fluid shifts, and vascular permeability changes. Future strategies, such as assessing baseline urinary KLK1 excretion to guide dosing or patient selection in CKD-associated RH, are conceptually attractive but remain exploratory and require prospective validation.
Taken together, current evidence suggests that DM199 has an acceptable early-phase safety profile, particularly with s.c. administration, but the relatively small exposure base and limited long-term data warrant cautious interpretation. For its potential application in RH among patients with CKD – a population already vulnerable to hemodynamic instability – careful dose titration, route optimization, and rigorous cardiovascular and renal safety monitoring will be essential as clinical development advances.

CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVE:
Experimental and early clinical observations provide biological support for a triple-pathway approach, exemplified by KLK1 and its recombinant form DM199, as a potential adjunctive strategy for treating CKD-associated RH. By engaging multiple vasorelaxant mechanisms, DM199 broadens the mechanistic scope of current antihypertensive therapy and targets endothelial and microvascular pathways that are incompletely addressed by existing agents. In this context, emerging approaches such as angiotensinogen-targeting siRNA therapies and renal denervation – offering durable RAAS suppression and sympathetic modulation, respectively – should be viewed as potentially complementary rather than competitive. Because these modalities act through largely nonoverlapping physiological pathways, their combined use may ultimately enable more comprehensive protection of target organs. Likewise, combination strategies integrating KLK1 supplementation with therapies that directly target endothelial redox balance, eNOS coupling, or ion-channel signalling may warrant future evaluation, particularly in advanced endothelial dysfunction.
It should be emphasized that the supporting evidence for DM199 derives from early-phase studies with limited sample sizes. The magnitude, durability, and long-term safety of its effects on BP and renal outcomes remain to be established in larger, adequately powered clinical trials. In addition, pharmacokinetic data in advanced CKD remain incomplete, and dose optimization in this population will require dedicated investigation.
A further opportunity – and challenge – lies in the development of more personalized KLK1-based therapeutic strategies. As outlined in Table  7 , several candidate biomarkers may help identify patients with genetically or functionally reduced endogenous KLK1 activity. Such markers could enrich trial populations, refine pharmacodynamic interpretation, and inform precision dosing. Nevertheless, these biomarkers remain exploratory, and their analytic validity, predictive value, and clinical utility will need to be confirmed in prospectively designed, biomarker-enabled studies.
Taken together, the available data support further clinical evaluation of DM199 within the more biologically defined subgroup of CKD-associated RH, where endothelial dysfunction, microvascular injury, and reduced endogenous kallikrein activity may be particularly relevant. A Phase 2 proof-of-concept study enriched for this subpopulation could be considered based on existing safety and pharmacokinetic observations. Such an investigation would benefit from standardized optimization of background therapy, careful phenotyping to distinguish renal-driven RH from other heterogeneous presentations, and close safety surveillance. Evaluation of ambulatory BP alongside renal functional measures, albuminuria, and endothelial biomarkers may help clarify both therapeutic signals and underlying mechanisms within this clinical context. Whether KLK1 augmentation can translate into meaningful benefit for CKD-associated RH remains to be established in adequately powered trials.
