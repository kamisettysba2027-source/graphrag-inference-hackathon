[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Prediabetes (impaired fasting glucose [IFG] and/or impaired glucose tolerance [IGT]) is a frequent condition with increased risk of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and cardiovascular disease (CVD) [ 1 – 3 ]. Both IFG and IGT are exhibiting a precipitous increase worldwide [ 4 ]. The cornerstone of management is lifestyle modifications and weight loss, while metformin may be used in selected individuals [ 2 ]. Current approaches support prompt prediabetes management as an effective strategy to reduce the global burden of T2DM [ 5 ].
Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) are established agents for T2DM and are also used in obesity [ 6 ,  7 ]. Interestingly, limited evidence points to beneficial effects in carefully selected individuals with type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) [ 8 ]. Currently, particular emphasis is placed on prevention or delay of T2DM manifestation [ 9 ]. Impaired glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) action has been shown in prediabetes [ 10 ,  11 ].
Therefore, the aim of this review was to summarise available experimental and clinical evidence on GLP-1RAs in prediabetes. Evidence on tirzepatide (LY3298176), the double glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor (GIPR), and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonist was also considered [ 12 ]. This review is based on previously conducted studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors.

Search Strategy:
An electronic search was carried out in Scopus, PubMed/MEDLINE and Google Scholar databases until 1 December 2025 using combinations of the following key words: ‘prediabetes’ and ‘glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists.’ All types of articles (clinical trials, meta-analyses, case–control studies, observational studies, cross-sectional studies, prospective or retrospective studies, cohort studies, comparative studies, randomised trials, experimental studies) were included. Only articles in English were considered.

Experimental Studies:
Almost 3 decades ago, GLP-1 was shown to promote glucose-dependent insulin secretion in prediabetic non-obese diabetic (NOD) mice [ 13 ]. Nevertheless, this model was defined on the basis of the presence of insulitis and fasting normoglycaemia, pointing to T1DM [ 13 ].
Tourrel et al. [ 14 ] studied GLP-1 and exendin-4 in a model of prediabetes. They included Goto-Kakizaki (GK) rats treated with either GLP-1, exendin-4 or without treatment [ 14 ]. GK rats are regarded as a lean T2DM model that develops at 8 weeks of age [ 15 ]. Wistar rats were included in the control arm [ 14 ]. On day 7, increased pancreatic insulin content and stimulated β-cell regeneration were seen with both treatments [ 14 ]. At 2 months, decreased plasma glucose and slightly improved glucose-stimulated insulin secretion both in vivo (following intravenous glucose load) and in vitro (in isolated perfused pancreas) were observed [ 14 ].
More recently, 12-week liraglutide versus vehicle or saline was evaluated in 2-week old prediabetic Otsuka-Long-Evans-Tokushima rats [ 16 ]. Liraglutide led to dose-dependent significant reductions in food intake (without reaching similar levels to controls) and body weight [ 16 ]. The beneficial effects of liraglutide were sustained [ 16 ]. IGT was not present until week 24 [ 16 ]. Liraglutide significantly reduced fasting glucose, 2 h-post-prandial glucose and the glucose area under the curve (area under the curve [AUC] 0–120 min), to levels similar to controls [ 16 ]. It also improved fasting serum insulin and homeostasis model assessment of insulin resistance (HOMA-IR), insulin sensitivity index (ISI), homeostasis model assessment of β-cell function (HOMA-β) along with ratio of insulin incremental to glucose incremental at 30 min [ 16 ]. At week 12, progression to T2DM was seen in 7 out of 8 (87.5%) vehicle-treated rats, whereas 9 out of 21 (42.9%) rats on liraglutide achieved normoglycaemia and none of them progressed to T2DM [ 16 ]. Liraglutide reduced total cholesterol, but not triglycerides (TGs) and interleukin (IL)-6 and tumour necrosis factor (TNF)-α, high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP) and plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) after 12 weeks [ 16 ]. Moreover, pathology showed restored islet size [ 16 ].
A recent study has demonstrated that liver dysfunction could be improved with exendin-4 in fructose-exposed male Wistar rats with prediabetes [ 17 ]. Exendin-4 reduced TGs, insulin, HOMA-IR and HOMA-β indices, liver fructokinase, adenosine monophosphate (AMP)-deaminase and glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G-6-P DH) [ 17 ].

Clinical Studies on GLP-1RAs:
Le Roux et al. [ 18 ] evaluated liraglutide-based treatment versus placebo for subjects with prediabetes at a dose of 3 mg for a study period of 160 weeks. T2DM diagnosis was met for 2% of subjects on liraglutide versus 6% in the control arm, and for 3% and 11% following statistical adjustment, respectively [ 18 ].
The interval to DM diagnosis was 2.7-times longer for subjects on liraglutide versus controls (95% confidence interval [CI] 1.9–3.9,  p  < 0.0001) with a hazard ratio [HR] of 0.21 (95% CI 0.13–0.34,  p  < 0.0001) [ 18 ] (Table  1 ).

Moreover, 66% of subjects on liraglutide achieved normoglycaemia versus 36% in the control arm, corresponding to a number needed to treat [NNT] of 3 (odds ratio [OR]: 3.6, 95% CI 3.0–4.4,  p  < 0.0001) at week 160 [ 18 ]. At week 172, regression to prediabetes was documented [ 18 ]. Nonetheless, normoglycaemia persisted in 50% of subjects on liraglutide versus 36% in the control arm (HR: 1.9, 95% CI 1.6–2.3,  p  < 0.0001) [ 18 ]. Liraglutide reduced glycated haemoglobin (HbA 1c ) by an estimated treatment difference of 0.21% (95% CI −0.24 to −0.18,  p  < 0.0001, fasting glucose by 0.41 (mmol/L) (95% CI −0.46 to −0.36,  p  < 0.0001) and fasting insulin by 10.1% (95% CI −14.3 to −5.6,  p  < 0.001) versus controls [ 18 ]. For weight loss, estimated treatment difference was −4.3% (95% CI −4.9 to −3.7,  p  < 0.0001) at week 160 and −3.2% (95% CI −4.3 to −2.2,  p  < 0.0001) at week 172 [ 18 ]. Liraglutide led to similar outcomes in weight management and HbA 1c , independently of body-mass index (BMI) [ 18 ]. The efficacy of liraglutide in terms of weight management was lower in those eventually being diagnosed with T2DM [ 18 ].
In the same study, reduced fasting glucose and fasting insulin were seen on week 160 [ 18 ]. Reduced systolic blood pressure (SBP) and C-reactive protein (CRP) were also found [ 18 ]. Adverse events, particularly gastrointestinal were mild-to-moderate and were more frequently reported for subjects on liragutide (8% versus 2%) [ 18 ]. Other adverse events included pancreatitis, neoplasms and resting tachycardia (increase by 2 bpm) [ 18 ]. Women with neoplasms had a weight loss above the average weight loss assessed in the study [ 18 ]. Prespecified cardiovascular events occurred more frequently in the control arm (15.1 events per 100 person-years of observation [PYO] versus 12.1 events per 100 PYO) [ 18 ]. Prevalence of major adverse cardiovascular events [MACEs] (cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction and/or non-fatal stroke) was similar in both groups (0.20 in the control arm versus 0.19 events per 100 PYO for the treatment arm) [ 18 ]. Overall, higher mean scores on the Short Form Health Survey (SF)-36 physical component summary score and the Impact of Weight on Quality of Life total score were seen with liraglutide [ 18 ].
In a post-hoc analysis of 62 subjects with prediabetes or early T2DM, several benefits were documented for those randomised to receive liraglutide or lifestyle modification [ 19 ]. Visceral adipose tissue (VAT) was significantly reduced versus placebo ( p  = 0.028), without any effect on subcutaneous adipose tissue (SAT) [ 19 ]. Significantly improved β-index was seen on liraglutide ( p  = 0.021) [ 19 ]. Insulin-like growth factor (IGF)-II was significantly increased ( p  = 0.024) [ 19 ].
A randomised controlled trial examined 88 subjects either treated with liraglutide or sitagliptin, or adhering to a caloric restriction (CR) diet [ 20 ]. A weight loss exceeding 5% was present in 44% of subjects on CR diet, 22% of those on liraglutide and 5% of those on sitagliptin (CR versus liraglutide: −2.8 kg, 95% CI; −1.2 to −4.8,  p  = 0.01; CR versus sitagliptin: −3.5 kg, 95% CI −0.1 to −6.9,  p  = 0.006; liraglutide versus sitagliptin: −1.7 kg, 95% CI −0.3 to −3.7,  p  = 0.01) [ 20 ]. In terms of fat loss, CR diet and liraglutide treatment yielded the most promising outcomes, with significant fat loss versus sitagliptin ( p  = 0.01) [ 20 ]. Lean mass loss occurred significantly more frequently with liraglutide ( p  = 0.07) [ 20 ]. Subjects on CR exhibited the greatest decrease in fat-to-lean mass ratio (difference CR versus liraglutide: −0.05, 95% CI −0.01 to −0.09,  p  = 0.04; CR versus sitagliptin: −0.07, 95% CI −0.01 to −0.12,  p  = 0.006; liraglutide versus sitagliptin: −0.02, 95% CI −0.02 to 0.07,  p  = 0.40) [ 20 ]. In subjects on liraglutide, fat-to-lean mass ratio decrease was significantly correlated with reduced truncal fat, reduced proportion of android to gynoid fat, and reduced visceral fat ( r  = 0.72,  p  < 0.001;  r  = 0.71,  p  < 0.001;  r  = 0.43,  p  = 0.02), respectively [ 20 ]. Similar results were seen with CR, as shown by the reduced truncal fat ( r  = 0.77,  p  = 0.005) [ 20 ]. The change in percentage of fat in the truncal region was significantly different among the three arms [ 20 ]. HOMA-IR was decreased by 35.4% in the CR group ( p  = 0.02) and by 30.8% in the liraglutide group ( p  = 0.03 [ 20 ]. Liraglutide resulted in significantly diminished feeling of fullness ( p  = 0.003) and willingness for food consumption ( p  = 0.02) [ 20 ]. Greater hunger was reported by subjects on CR and sitagliptin, but not on liraglutide [ 20 ]. Subjects on CR reduced their total dietary sugar intake: CR versus liraglutide: −29.5 g, 95% CI −5.0 to −54.8,  p  = 0.02; CR versus sitagliptin: −25.1 g, 95% CI −25.9 to −75.2,  p  = 0.02 and liraglutide versus sitagliptin: −9.9 g, 95% CI −14.4 to 34.2,  p  = 0.42. For added dietary sugars, the following differences were seen: CR versus liraglutide: −31.1 g, 95% CI −9.7 to −52.5,  p  = 0.005; CR versus sitagliptin: −23.0 g, 95% CI −67.4 to −21.4,  p  = 0.005; liraglutide versus sitagliptin: −5.6 g, 95% CI −42.9 to 31.6,  p  = 0.43) [ 20 ].
The same research group evaluated insulin sensitivity, following an identical study design [ 21 ]. Sitagliptin did not result in significant weight loss versus liraglutide (at week 14) and hypocaloric diet (at week 2 and 14) [ 21 ]. Liraglutide significantly reduced fasting glucose, glucagon and HOMA-IR at both weeks 2 and 14, as well as fasting insulin and homeostasis model assessment 2 (HOMA2) at week 2. No effect on C-peptide and glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) was seen [ 21 ]. Liraglutide reduced post-prandial glucose, AUC glucose and peak insulin C-peptide at both week 2 and 14 and AUC insulin at week 2, as well as AUC C-peptide at week 14. The same held true for increased Matsuda index at both week 2 and 14 [ 21 ]. Finally, the antagonist exendin 9–39 increased post-prandial insulin and C-peptide and AUC and decreased Matsuda index in subjects on liraglutide [ 21 ].
A randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicentre phase 3 trial (STEP10) examined the efficacy of semaglutide in subjects with prediabetes and obesity [ 22 ]. Only subjects with baseline HbA 1c  between 6.0 and 6.4% and fasting glucose between 5.5 and 6.9 mmol/L were included [ 22 ]. These were randomised to receive once-weekly subcutaneous 2.4 mg semaglutide or placebo with concomitant dietary and exercise recommendations for 52 weeks, followed by a 28-week off-treatment period [ 22 ]. At week 80, semaglutide reduced mean body weight by 7.9% versus 1.3% in the control arm [ 22 ]. Partial weight regain was found during the off-treatment period, but no participant regained all lost weight [ 22 ]. Significantly more subjects receiving semaglutide exhibited normal glucose control at week 52 versus control arm (81% versus 14%, OR: 19.8, 95% CI 8.7–45.2,  p  < 0.001) [ 22 ]. This effect was ameliorated at week 80 (44% versus 18%) [ 22 ]. Regression to prediabetes after achieving normoglycaemia was observed in 45% in the treatment arm and 57% in the control arm at week 80 for subjects who achieved normoglycemia at week 52 [ 22 ]. At week 80, 3% of subjects on semaglutide progressed to T2DM compared with 8% in the control arm [ 22 ]. At week 52, semaglutide significantly reduced HbA 1c  (−0.5, 95% CI −0.5 to −0.4,  p  < 0.0001), fasting glucose (−0.6, 95% CI −0.8 to −0.4,  p  < 0.0001), waist circumference (−8.3, 95% CI −10.4 to −6.2,  p  < 0.0001) and SBP (−7.8, 95% CI −11.3 to −4.3,  p  < 0.0001) [ 22 ]. These effects were less pronounced at week 80 [ 22 ].
Total cholesterol, very-low density lipoprotein cholesterol (VLDLc) and TGs were reduced at week 52 [ 22 ]. A similar trend for low density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDLc), VLDLc and TGs was noted at week 80[ 22 ]. At week 80, high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDLc) and total cholesterol returned to baseline levels [ 22 ]. Serious adverse events were reported by 9% in both arms, with gastrointestinal adverse events being reported only in semaglutide users [ 22 ]. Other adverse events included: neoplasms in four subjects, acute pancreatitis in one subject, malignant neoplasms in two subjects and acute gallbladder disease in one subject [ 22 ]. Treatment discontinuation was mainly attributed to gastrointestinal adverse events: 5% with semaglutide versus 1% in the control arm [ 22 ]. Finally, semaglutide improved health-related quality of life (HRQoL) measured by European Quality of Life 5 Dimensions 3 Level Version (EQ-5D-3L) [ 22 ].

Clinical Studies on Tirzepatide:
Various studies conducted secondary analyses of SURMOUNT-1 trial with a particular focus on prediabetes [ 23 ]. In a post-hoc analysis, Hankosky et al. [ 24 ] evaluated the clinical utility of tirzepatide in 987 subjects with prediabetes and 1552 subjects without prediabetes. The 10-year predicted risk of T2DM at baseline, week 24 and week 72 was calculated according to Cardiometabolic Disease Staging risk engine [ 24 ]. At baseline, the 10-year risk for T2DM development was similar between subjects on tirzeptide (22.9%) and controls (24.3%) [ 24 ]. After 24 weeks, mean risk scores ranged between 11.0% on 15 mg tirzepatide and 12.5% on 5 mg tirzepatide versus 21.4% on placebo [ 24 ]. Mean risk score reductions from baseline to week 24 ranged between 11.2% on 5 mg tirzepatide and 12.7% on 15 mg tirzepatide versus 2.3% with placebo [ 24 ]. Significant risk reduction versus placebo ( p  < 0.001) was found overall on 5 mg tirzepatide by 8.9%, on 10 mg tirzepatide by 10.1% and on 15 mg tirzepatide by 10.4%, [ 24 ]. At week 72, mean risk scores were between, 9.0% on 15 mg and 11.4% on 5 with tirzepatide and 23.0% with placebo [ 24 ]. Similarly, the most pronounced mean risk score reduction from baseline to week 72 was found for subjects on 15 mg tirzepatide (14.7%) versus those on 5 mg tirzepatide (12.4%) and versus 0.7% on placebo. A significant risk reduction was found on 5 mg by 11.7%, on 10 mg by 13.7% and on 15 mg by 14.0% ( p  < 0.001) [ 24 ]. Median relative risk decreased by 60.3% to 60.9% on tirzepatide versus 10.8% on placebo [ 24 ] (Table  2 ).

Recently, the same research group published another post-hoc analysis of SURMOUNT-1, dedicated at cardiovascular benefits in prediabetes [ 25 ]. They included 235 subjects treated with 5 mg tirzepatide, 251 subjects treated with 10 mg tirzepatide, 242 subjects treated with 15 mg tirzepatide and 257 in the control arm [ 25 ]. At baseline, participants were considered mostly of low-risk for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) (73.4%) based on American College of Cardiology (ACC)/American Heart Association (AHA) risk equation: 14.2% belonged to the borderline group, 11.1% to the intermediate group and 1.3% in the high-risk group for ASCVD [ 25 ]. At week 176, a worse risk category was seen in 23.8% of subjects in the control arm versus 11.8% of those on tirzepatide [ 25 ]. ACC/AHA scores were between 1.8% and 2.2% for participants on tirzepatide versus 3.7% for controls [ 25 ]. The odds ratio of improved ASCVD risk category was 2.4 for tirzepatide (95% CI 1.4–4.0,  p  < 0.001) [ 25 ].
Moreover, tirzepatide reduced cardiovascular risk on the basis of the Predicting Risk of Cardiovascular Disease EVENTs (PREVENT) equation: deteriorating risk was more frequent in the control arm (14.6%) versus treatment arm (3.5%) [ 25 ]. At week 176, PREVENT scores were comparable across the different study arms. For ASCVD risk, the scores ranged between 1.5% and 2.0% on tirzepatide versus 2.9% in the control arm. For heart failure (HF), corresponding scores were between 1.3% and 1.5% on tirzepatide versus 2.4% in the control arm [ 25 ]. The odds ratio of improved ASCVD risk category was 3.5 for tirzepatide (95% CI 1.6–7.4,  p  = 0.001) [ 25 ].
At week 176, the composite risk score for ASCVD and HF was between 2.5% and 3.2% for subjects on tirzepatide versus 4.3% in the control arm [ 25 ]. Overall, least square mean percent change of the 10-year predicted risk of total CVD was significantly higher for tirzepatide users (1.3%, 0.7% and 2.0% on 5 mg, 10 mg and 15 mg tirzepatide, respectively) compared with increased risk in the control arm (+43.8%) ( p  < 0.0001) from baseline to week 176 [ 25 ].
Tirzepatide was associated with a significantly greater decrease in T2DM risk based on cardiometabolic disease staging at all doses versus the control arm (5 mg: −17.0%; 10 mg: −19.6%; 15 mg: −19.5%) versus placebo (−4.3%),  p  < 0.0001) [ 25 ]. Mean 10-year T2DM prediction scores were 8.9%, 6.3% and 6.4% on 5 mg, 10 mg and 15 mg tirzepatide, respectively versus 21.9% on placebo [ 25 ]. Mean percent change for T2DM prediction score was significantly greater with tirzepatide (17.0%, 19.9% and 19.5% on 5 mg, 10 mg and 15 mg tirzepatide, respectively, versus increased risk in the control arm (+4.3%) ( p  < 0.0001) [ 25 ].
The recent additional post-hoc analysis of SURMOUNT-1 [ 26 ] examined the effects of tirzepatide on insulin sensitivity for 72 weeks. After week 12, reduced HbA 1c , glucose and insulin were found in tirzepatide users versus controls [ 26 ]. Reduced Homeostatic Model Assessment 2—Insulin Resistance (HOMA2-IR) reached a stable level at week 36 [ 26 ]. Restored HOMA2-IR similar to that of subjects with normoglycaemia was found at week 72 [ 26 ]. At week 72, significantly decreased glucose and insulin AUC during oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) were documented [ 26 ]. Glucose AUC decreases were more pronounced in subjects with prediabetes, versus those with normal glycaemic control [ 26 ]. Significantly improved β-cell glucose sensitivity was observed among tirzepatide recipients ( p  < 0.001) [ 26 ]. Changes in insulin secretion rate were found: at 5.4 mmol/L glucose, insulin rate was promoted in a dose-dependent manner for tirzepatide users, the effect being particularly present in subjects with prediabetes, but not in those with normoglycaemia [ 26 ].
At week 72, total insulin secretion (TIS) was significantly diminished in subjects treated with tirzepatide versus placebo, irrespective of baseline glycaemic status [ 26 ]. Significantly more pronounced increases in the potentiation ratio at 2 h after OGTT were found across most tirzepatide groups versus controls, and these were almost independent of glycaemic status [ 26 ]. Treatment with 10 mg and 15 mg tirzepatide (but not with 5 mg) increased 30-min insulinogenic index (IGI) in all participants (including those with normoglycaemia at baseline) versus controls [ 26 ]. Nevertheless, no such effect was observed for subjects with prediabetes at baseline irrespective of tirzepatide dose [ 26 ].
Subjects with prediabetes and normoglycaemia at baseline who were allocated to tirzepatide exhibited improved insulin sensitivity, according to Matsuda index [ 27 ] or oral glucose insulin sensitivity (OGIS) [ 26 ]. Tirzepatide-induced weight loss mediated changes in Matsuda index and in OGIS. These changes were significantly inversely correlated to body weight reduction ( p  < 0.001) [ 26 ]. After adjustment for weight reduction, OGIS changes were less pronounced in subjects with prediabetes versus subjects with normoglycaemia [ 26 ]. In multivariate analysis, no significant correlation was established between prediabetes status and changes in the Matsuda index [ 26 ]. A significant correlation between tirzepatide and percentage body change was noted for OGIS ( p  = 0.039) but not for Matsuda index [ 26 ]. Beta-cell specific changes were associated with tirzepatide, but not with body weight change [ 26 ]. In multivariate regression analysis, improved insulin sensitivity was associated with weight reduction and only partly with tirzepatide treatment, whereas improved β-cell function was attributable to tirzepatide [ 26 ].
The post-hoc analysis of the SURMOUNT-CN trial indicated that tirzepatide-based treatment response may vary in Chinese individuals [ 28 ]. Mean predicted T2DM risk decreased from 5.3% to 1.3% on 10 mg tirzepatide, from 4.9% to 1.1% on 15 mg tirzepatide and from 5.8% to 4.2% in the control arm [ 28 ]. Significantly greater predicted T2DM risk changes were observed on 10 mg tirzepatide (mean risk change −2.8%, −3.7%, −2.0%) and on 15 mg tirzepatide (−3.1%, −3.9%, −2.2%) versus controls [ 28 ]. Mean percent risk reduction was 76% on 10 mg tirzepatide and 80% on 15 mg tirzepatide versus 22% in controls [ 28 ]. The effect of tirzepatide was significantly greater, independent of baseline prediabetes and of BMI ( p  < 0.05) [ 28 ]. The difference in mean risk change to week 52 was significant on 10 mg tirzepatide (−3.2%, 95% CI −4.2% to −2.2%) and on 15 mg tirzepatide (−3.4%, 95% CI −4.4% to −2.4%) versus controls [ 28 ]. At week 52, mean risk score was 1.2% on 10 mg tirzepatide, 1.0% on 15 mg tirzepatide versus 4.5% in the control arm [ 28 ]. Compared with baseline, mean risk reduction ranged from 77% on 10 mg tirzepatide to 82% on 15 mg tirzepatide versus 17% for the control arm [ 28 ].
Among subjects with prediabetes, differences in mean change in risk scores were −4.7% (95% CI −6.3% to −3.1%) at week 24 and −4.9% (95% CI −7.3% to −2.4%) at week 52 for subjects on 10 mg tirzepatide versus controls [ 28 ]. They were −5.3% (95% CI −6.9% to −3.8%) at week 24 and −5.5% (95% CI −7.9% to −3.0%) at week 52 on 15 mg versus controls [ 28 ]. Direct comparison with subjects without prediabetes showed that the differences in mean change in risk scores were: −1.6% (95% CI −2.4% to −0.8%) at week 24 and −1.9% (95% CI −2.7% to −1.1%) at week 52 on 10 mg tirzepatide; −1.7% (95% CI −2.5% to −0.9%) at week 24 and −2.1% (95% CI −2.9% to −1.3%) at week 52 on 15 mg tirzepatide versus controls [ 28 ].
A recent phase 3, double-blind, randomised, controlled trial examined the efficacy of tirzepatide in preventing progression to T2DM [ 29 ]. Overall, 1092 subjects were randomised to three tirzepatide doses (5 mg, 10 mg and 15 mg) or placebo according to a 1:1:1:1 ratio [ 29 ]. Tirzepatide achieved significantly increased body weight loss versus placebo: the loss ranged between 12.3%/12.4 kg and 19.7%/21.4 kg versus 1.3%/0.9 kg [ 29 ]. Estimated treatment difference on 15 mg tirzepatide was −18.4% (95% CI −22.2 to −14.7,  p  < 0.001) [ 29 ]. Less than 5 subjects on tirzepatide across the different doses (between 0.4% and 2.0%) developed T2DM versus 36 subjects on placebo (13.3%) ( p  < 0.001) [ 29 ]. Almost all subjects on tirzepatide achieved normoglycaemia versus 58.9% on placebo [ 29 ]. Post-hoc mediation analysis showed that 38.9% (average treatment effect versus placebo for all subjects having completed randomisation process) of T2DM risk was mediated by % reduction in body weight [ 29 ].
Normoglycaemia was more frequent in participants experiencing body-weight reductions of at least 5% and at least 10% at week 176 on tirzepatide versus controls: 486 (96%) and 440 (97%), respectively, of subjects on tirzepatide and in 39 (83%) and 17 (81%), respectively, of those on placebo [ 29 ]. Similar outcomes were observed for subjects with a documented weight loss of at least 15%, at least 20% or at least 25% at week 176: normoglycaemia was seen in 360 (98%), 271 (99%), and 184 (99%), respectively, of subjects on tirzepatide and in 11 (100%), 6 (100%), and 4 (100%), respectively, of those in the control arm [ 29 ]. In the subgroup failing to achieve > 5% weight loss: 38 (81%) of tirzepatide users and 47 (49%) of subjects on placebo exhibited normal glucose at week 176 [ 29 ]. At the end of the 17-week off-treatment period, 2.4% of tirzepatide users developed T2DM versus 13.7% in the control arm: hazard ratio [pooled tirzepatide versus placebo], 0.12; 95% CI 0.1–0.2,  p  < 0.001) [ 29 ]. Normoglycaemia was seen in 182 (73.7%) subjects on 5 mg tirzepatide, 215 (82.1%) subjects on 10 mg tirzepatide and 196 (77.5%) subjects on 15 mg tirzepatide [ 29 ]. Prediabetes was seen in 58 (23.5%) subjects on 5 mg tirzepatide, 39 (14.9%) subjects on 10 mg tirzepatide and 54 (21.3%) subjects on 15 mg tirzepatide [ 29 ]. T2DM was seen in 7 (2.8%) subjects on 5 mg tirzepatide, 8 (3.1%) subjects on 10 mg tirzepatide and 3 (1.2%) subjects on 15 mg tirzepatide [ 29 ]. Finally, tirzepatide reduced waist circumference, systolic and diastolic blood pressure and serum lipids [ 29 ].
Mild-to-moderate gastrointestinal adverse events (nausea, constipation and diarrhoea) were reported [ 29 ]. Nausea and diarrhoea were transient and mostly documented during the dose-escalation period [ 29 ]. Some adverse events (1.6%) were attributed to coronavirus disease-19 (COVID-19) [ 29 ]. The overall beneficial effects were reflected in health-related quality of life (HRQoL) of the study participants, as shown by the higher score by 2.6 points with tirzepatide compared with placebo from baseline in SF-36 physical-function domain score [ 29 ]. Similar beneficial effects were noted in other SF-36 domain scores, including mental health, role-emotional, role-physical, bodily pain and general health perception [ 29 ].
A retrospective cohort study presented data from TriNetXGlobal Collaborative Network regarding 5100 subjects on tirzepatide and 5100 subjects on metformin [ 30 ]. Subjects with T1DM, T2DM prior to study initiation or any prior treatment with GLP-1RAs were excluded [ 30 ]. T2DM development was significantly less frequent on tirzepatide (4.7%) versus metformin (12.5%) (risk ratio [RR]: 0.37; 95% CI 0.29–0.48,  p  < 0.001) [ 30 ]. Subjects on tirzepatide lost 14.7 pounds and 9.1 pounds and the corresponding values on metformin were 6.5 and 3.4 pounds [ 30 ]. Slight reduction was documented in HbA 1c  by 0.2% and 0.1% for tirzepatide and metformin users, respectively [ 30 ].
To date, a single study has compared semaglutide, liraglutide and tirzepatide in 115 subjects, including 36 subjects with prediabetes [ 31 ]. Participants were not equally divided into semaglutide, liraglutide and tirzepatide [ 31 ]. There was an average weight reduction by 8.78%, 8.27% and 7.03% on tirzepatide, semaglutide and liraglutide, respectively [ 31 ]. A weight reduction over 15% was found in 36.7% of subjects on tirzepatide, followed by 27.5% on semaglutide [ 31 ]. Fat mass was reduced by 15.46%, 13.86% and 12.53% on tirzepatide, liraglutide and semaglutide, respectively [ 31 ]. Overall, significant improvement in cholesterol, LDLc, TGs, HbA 1c , alanine transaminase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) were found for all study participants [ 31 ]. These benefits were largely reflected in 36 subjects with prediabetes: significantly reduced HbA 1c  by 0.46% ( p  < 0.001), cholesterol by 16.24 mg/dL ( p  = 0.003), weight loss of 7.52 kg ( p  < 0.001) and fat mass by 5.43 kg ( p  < 0.001) were seen [ 31 ]. Importantly, 80.6% of subjects with prediabetes achieved normoglycaemia [ 31 ].

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
This review summarises evidence on the promising effects of several GLP-1RAs and the double GLP-1R and GIPR receptor agonist tirzepatide in subjects with prediabetes. Liraglutide has been the first agonist extensively investigated [ 18 – 21 ,  31 ], followed by semaglutide [ 22 ,  31 ]. Tirzepatide is now being increasingly explored [ 24 – 26 ,  28 – 31 ].
A key endpoint was proportion of participants achieving normoglycaemia: 66% of subjects on liraglutide and 81% of those on semaglutide had optimal glucose versus 36% and 14% of subjects in the control arm, respectively [ 18 ,  22 ]. Following the off-treatment period, normoglycaemia persisted in 50% of subjects on liraglutide and 44% of subjects on semaglutide versus 36% and 18% of those in the control arm, respectively [ 18 ,  22 ]. Tirzepatide yielded even more pronounced outcomes: 89.9–93.3% of tirzepatide users across the different doses achieved normoglycaemia versus 58.9% on placebo [ 29 ]. Slightly decreased HbA 1c  and reduced fasting glucose were observed [ 18 ,  21 ,  22 ,  26 ,  30 ,  31 ]. The former did not persist following the off-treatment period [ 18 ,  22 ]. At the end of the 17-week off-treatment period, 73.7–82.1% of tirzepatide users sustained optimal glucose [ 29 ]. Liraglutide over 3 years resulted in a NNT of only 3 in terms of achieving normoglycaemia [ 18 ]. The STEP1 extension trial provided evidence regarding the glycaemic and weight profile of subjects with obesity and without concomitant DM after semaglutide withdrawal [ 32 ]. At week 68, semaglutide treatment was discontinued and participants were monitored until week 120 [ 32 ]. HbA 1c  was decreased versus baseline. However, subjects on semaglutide experienced a more pronounced increase in HbA 1c  versus controls [ 32 ]. The trial focused on subjects with prediabetes as well [ 32 ]. At week 68, 93.6% of semaglutide users achieved normoglycaemia compared with 41.5% of subjects in the control arm [ 32 ]. Following semaglutide discontinuation, glucose control was aggravated, particularly in subjects on semaglutide [ 32 ]. Overall, a relative benefit was maintained for subjects on semaglutide versus controls: nomoglycaemia was found in 43.3% versus 34.0%, respectively [ 32 ]. This is the only trial addressing long-term effects of GLP-1RAs following treatment discontinuation. The off-treatment period of 52 weeks in this trial is far longer than the corresponding intervals used in other works (12–28 weeks) [ 18 ,  21 ,  29 ].
GLP-1RAs are as weight-lowering agents [ 33 ,  34 ]. Liraglutide resulted in reduced weight by 6.1% versus 1.9% in the control arm [ 18 ]. A small study showed that liraglutide was not as beneficial as caloric restriction, but was superior to sitagliptin [ 20 ]. Semaglutide led to a more pronounced weight loss by 7.9% versus 1.3% in the control arm [ 18 ]. Nonetheless, the effect was not sustained, and partial weight regain was reported [ 18 ]. Tirzepatide achieved the greatest weight loss ranging from 12.3% (12.4 kg) to 19.7% (21.4 kg) versus 1.3% (0.9 kg) on placebo [ 30 ]. These changes were reflected in reduced waist circumference [ 22 ,  29 ]. A weight loss of 7.52 kg corresponded to a fat mass loss of 5.43 kg [ 31 ]. Other works showed that subjects with prediabetes lost mainly VAT, without any effect on SAT [ 19 ,  20 ]. This is consistent with decreased truncal fat and decreased android to gynoid fat [ 20 ]. Lean mass loss was reported in single studies [ 19 ,  20 ]. Changing eating patterns with reduced fullness feeling threshold and food consumption eagerness have also been reported, and these were likely attributable to direct effects on hypothalamic circuits [ 20 ,  35 ]. Regarding sustained weight loss, the STEP 1 extension trial showed that weight loss of 17.3% (achieved at week 68) was diminished owing to weight regain of 11.6% following treatment discontinuation. This resulted in a long-term net weight loss of 5.6% [ 32 ].
The safety profile of GLP-1RAs across the various studies was acceptable with gastrointestinal effects of mild-to-moderate severity [ 18 ,  22 ]. Choice of individual GLP-1RAs may be influenced by the safety profile of each agent [ 36 ,  37 ]. Furthermore, minor dietary adaptations and adequate hydration could at least partly relieve adverse events [ 38 ,  39 ].
T2DM was finally diagnosed in 3% of subjects on liraglutide and semaglutide versus 11% and 8% for individuals in the control arm, respectively [ 18 ,  22 ]. Tirzepatide achieved more pronounced results: only 0.4–2% of tirzepatide recipients across the different doses finally developed T2DM versus 13.3% in the control arm [ 29 ]. This effect persisted following the off-treatment period: T2DM diagnosis was reported for 2.4% versus 13.7%, respectively [ 29 ]. Another work showed median risk reduction on tirzepatide by 60.3–60.9% versus 10.8% in the control arm [ 24 ]. Liraglutide delayed T2DM diagnosis by 99 weeks versus 87 weeks in the control arm [ 18 ]. It significantly prolonged time to T2DM diagnosis by 2.7 times [ 18 ].
Accumulating evidence suggests that these beneficial effects were reflected in insulin sensitivity and resistance [ 18 – 21 ,  26 ]. GLP-1RAs and tirzepatide in prediabetes improved insulin sensitivity and reduced insulin resistance, mostly evaluated by Matsuda index [ 21 ,  26 ] and HOMA-based tools [ 18 ,  20 ,  21 ,  26 ], respectively. Nevertheless, despite its wide application, HOMA-based tools do not suffice for predicting prediabetes [ 40 ,  41 ]. Improved insulin sensitivity was observed in experimental studies, as well [ 16 ,  17 ].
Weight loss in tirzepatide users mediated about 38.9% of overall benefit in T2DM risk reduction [ 29 ]. Certainly, future trials including individuals with prediabetes without concomitant obesity or overweight are needed to delineate the exact benefit of GLP-1RAs in insulin resistance beyond mere weight loss. Nevertheless, weight loss and increased insulin sensitivity are strongly associated with each other and may concurrently drive the efficacy of GLP-1RAs and particularly tirzepatide [ 42 ].
Tirzepatide was linked to reduced cardiovascular risk in a post-hoc analysis [ 25 ]. Cardiovascular risk was calculated on the basis of various scores e.g. ACC/AHA score or PREVENT equation [ 43 – 45 ]. The composite risk for ASCVD and HF was also significantly reduced [ 25 ]. T2DM risk was reduced following assessment with Cardiometabolic Disease Staging [ 25 ]. Liraglutide was also considered safe from a cardiovascular perspective [ 18 ].
Moreover, trials have reported improved lipid management [ 22 ,  29 ,  31 ] and SBP [ 18 ,  22 ,  29 ]. CRP was also reduced, pointing to anti-inflammatory properties of GLP-1RAs [ 18 ]. Similar anti-inflammatory effects were observed in subjects with prediabetes on liraglutide based on reduced macrophage markers (notably CD163) and IL-6 [ 46 ,  47 ].
For years, management of prediabetes has been based on lifestyle interventions [ 9 ]. A combination of dietary restrictions with regular exercise is strongly encouraged, even after manifestation of T2DM [ 48 ]. This combined approach has yielded promising outcomes in several studies [ 49 ,  50 ]. Lifestyle interventions, as described in the Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) have shown greater potential than metformin [ 51 ]. Similar outcomes were observed in subsequent analyses [ 52 ]. The value of lifestyle intervention has been confirmed in recent large meta-analyses [ 53 ,  54 ]. However, tailored approaches are increasingly needed for subjects with isolated IFG [ 53 ,  54 ]. The same holds true for subjects with increased HbA 1c  [ 54 ]. This subgroup of subjects with prediabetes could experience the greatest benefit from pharmacological approaches, including GLP-1RAs, combined with lifestyle intervention [ 54 ,  55 ]. Overall, the efficacy of GLP-1RAs may be increased by dietary and exercise recommendations. Currently, practical nutritional recommendations are available for subjects treated with GLP-1RAs [ 56 ,  57 ]. An 8-week combined dietary (low calorie diet) and exercise (either 150 min/week of moderate-intensity or 75 min/week of intense aerobic exercise) intervention along with GLP-1RAs treatment has been shown to potentiate the beneficial effects of GLP-1RAs [ 58 ]. A study concluded that weight loss persisted at 1 year after liraglutide discontinuation [ 59 ]. Another work concluded reduced appetite after meals and decrease in sedentary time by 41 min/day beyond mere maintenance of weight loss with combination of liraglutide and exercise [ 60 ]. Nevertheless, exercise was sufficient as a single intervention in maintaining weight loss despite the increased appetite similar to the control arm [ 60 ]. This study enrolled subjects with obesity and not specifically with prediabetes, but it offers interesting comparisons on the efficacy of GLP-1RAs versus lifestyle modification.
A favourable key aspect for future use of these agents was improvement in self-perceived HRQoL, as reported by tirzepatide recipients in various studies [ 18 ,  22 ,  29 ]. This improvement was reflected in various validated assessment tools, including SF-36, Impact of Weight on Quality of Life–Lite total score and EQ-5D-3L [ 18 ,  22 ,  29 ]. Several diabetic complications manifest as early as in the prediabetic state, rendering prediabetes a non-innocent condition [ 61 – 63 ]. Thus, prompt management is recommended. Emerging diabetes-associated conditions also require special attention: 42.4% of individuals with prediabetes have concomitant metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) [ 64 ]. A significant proportion of them face an elevated risk for advanced cirrhosis and fibrosis [ 64 ]. Semaglutide and tirzepatide have shown positive results for MASLD management [ 65 ,  66 ]. Experimental studies showed reduced liver dysfunction in the prediabetic state with exendin-4 [ 17 ]. Consequently, there may be an additional role for GLP-1RAs in prevention of diabetic complications and of other diabetes-associated conditions, but much more information is needed.
So far, metformin has been used in selected individuals with prediabetes [ 67 ]. A relatively recent trial in Chinese individuals showed that metformin could reduce the risk for DM progression by 17%, when combined with lifestyle intervention, than the exclusive use of the latter [ 68 ]. Nevertheless, the use of metformin remains controversial [ 69 ,  70 ]. To date, only a single study has compared metformin with tirzepatide [ 30 ]. Impressively, 4.7% of subjects on tirzepatide versus 12.5% on metformin developed T2DM [ 30 ]. These earliest outcomes would enhance the use of GLP-1RAs in prediabetes compared with the only currently available therapeutic option and this conclusion certainly merits further investigation [ 30 ]. Early findings on metformin and exenatide showed no greater benefit for the latter in terms of reduced microvascular endothelial markers, inflammation and oxidative stress [ 71 ]. In prediabetes, lifestyle modification or pharmacological intervention with metformin may be inadequate, while GLP-1RAs yield more favourable outcomes. Nevertheless, future research should answer the question whether individuals facing a higher risk of T2DM development should be administered GLP-1RAs, whereas those at lower risk should receive metformin and/or lifestyle modification.
Poor response to GLP-1RAs-based treatment in prediabetes is another meaningful aspect. A subject failing to achieve a weight loss of 5% on semaglutide progressed to T2DM [ 22 ]. Similarly, subjects on tirzepatide failing to achieve the 5% weight loss achieved normoglycaemia but to a less pronounced effect than those with greater weight loss on tirzepatide [ 29 ]. Based on these outcomes, poor weight loss in subjects with prediabetes on GLP-1RAs may be associated with less pronounced glycaemic effects. Certainly, this is an important issue requiring further investigation.
The therapeutic armamentarium of GLP-1RAs is constantly expanding [ 72 ]. Double GLP-1R/glucagon receptor (GCGR) agonists, such as cotadutide and mazdutide, have been introduced in clinical studies [ 73 ,  74 ]. Retatrutide, a triple GCGR, GIPR and GLP-1R agonist has been evaluated in several studies [ 75 ,  76 ]. The phase 2 study on retatrutide showed prediabetes remission for 72% of retatrutide users compared with only 22% in the control arm [ 76 ]. Retatrutide may hold the greatest potential in prediabetes remission and certainly this outcome should be further explored in randomised trials.
Nevertheless, cost is an important issue, given the immense financial burden of GLP-1RAs (approximately $71 billion in the USA in 2023) [ 77 ]. GLP-1RAs may be considered first-line agents in T2DM, but their price needs to be decreased by 70% to ensure their cost-effectiveness [ 78 ]. Of note, the patent of liraglutide expired in 2024, and so the introduction of generic liraglutide might reduce financial burden [ 79 ]. This is relevant, granted that data on prediabetes is largely based on liraglutide [ 79 ]. Thereby, the goal of 70% reduction to increase cost-effectiveness could be achieved. Consequently, a balanced cost-effective administration of GLP-1RAs in this large population group merits careful consideration.
The strength of this review is discussion of experimental and clinical evidence on the emerging role of GLP-1RAs and tirzepatide in prediabetes. In this evidence, ethnic diversity has also been considered, based on Black/African American [ 24 ,  25 ] and Chinese participants [ 28 ]. Nevertheless, there are several limitations. The first relates to the variable number of participants across studies and to the huge variability in study duration (including duration of off-treatment periods) and the great heterogeneity in study endpoints. Moreover, several studies were post-hoc analyses and thus did not include original data and focused almost exclusively on subjects that were overweight or had obesity. Thirdly, real-world data is also extremely limited. Therefore, large randomised controlled trials are needed to ascertain the exact benefits conferred by GLP-1RAs in prediabetes. Future research should explore the long-term effects of GLP-1RAs treatment in prediabetes and particularly the durability of their effects after treatment withdrawal. Weight regain following treatment discontinuation is commonly encountered. Essentially, a net weight loss is often maintained according to earliest studies, but drug withdrawal may reduce the additional benefits in subjects with prediabetes, including the improvement in insulin resistance. Current prevention programmes focusing on lifestyle modification may potentiate the effects of GLP-1RAs. Scarce evidence from clinical trials comparing GLP-1RAs with metformin has confirmed the superiority of the former. Nevertheless, a potential synergistic effect of metformin and GLP-1RAs should be also examined. Researchers should elucidate which subgroups of individuals with prediabetes will most benefit from GLP-1RAs [ 80 ]. So far, clinical studies have focused on the effects of GLP-1RAs in subjects with prediabetes and overweight/obesity. The latter are, therefore, more likely to derive the greatest benefit. Given the absence of studies looking into pathophysiology, knowledge of the mechanisms (decreased appetite, increased insulin secretion, or both) underlying the effects of GLP-1RAs in prediabetes is limited. Further research into molecular mechanisms may unravel novel therapeutic targets or signalling pathways potentiating the effects of GLP-1RAs and allow tailored therapeutic strategies, ensuring treatment response to GLP-1RAs in carefully selected subjects with prediabetes. Additional trials should include all groups of subjects with prediabetes in order to provide more comprehensive evidence on individual characteristics suggesting better treatment response. Finally, more experience on untoward effects in everyday clinical reality is desirable.

[SECTION: CONCLUSIONS]
Prediabetes is a frequent condition with increased risk of T2DM. Accordingly, early and prompt management of prediabetes is becoming important. Liraglutide, semaglutide and, more recently, tirzepatide have improved weight management and insulin sensitivity in subjects with prediabetes. Many participants achieved normoglycaemia, and this effect was greater with tirzepatide, even following agent withdrawal. Tirzepatide has demonstrated the most pronounced efficacy in prevention of progression to T2DM. Of note, it has also reduced the increased cardiovascular risk in subjects with prediabetes and has exerted beneficial effects in lipid profile. Future large randomised controlled trials are now needed to ascertain the exact role of GLP-1RAs in prediabetes and to identify which subgroups are likely to experience the greatest benefit.
