Title: Use of Oral Semaglutide and Associated Clinical Outcomes in Thai Patients With Type 2 Diabetes: Real‐World Evidence From the REALISED Study
Year: 2022
Type: observational_study
Drugs: metformin, semaglutide

[SECTION: RESULTS]
Oral semaglutide was predominantly used as add‐on therapy (190/195 [97.4%]). Mean HbA1c decreased by −0.7% (95% CI: −0.9 to −0.5;  p  < 0.0001), and the mean body weight decreased by −4.3 kg (95% CI: −5.5 to −3.2;  p  < 0.0001). At the EoS, 75.9% (117/154) achieved an HbA1c < 7%, and 57.7% (71/123) met the composite endpoint. No severe hypoglycaemia occurred. Dose escalation from 3 mg to 14 mg was observed in 53.2% by Week 26 and 70.4% by Week 52.

[SECTION: CONCLUSIONS]
The REALISED study is the first to report real‐world outcomes of oral semaglutide use in Southeast Asia, demonstrating its clinical benefits in routine care as an add‐on therapy for Thai patients with T2D. Significant reductions in HbA1c and body weight were observed across diverse profiles, including those with longstanding diabetes and obesity. These findings support its use in adults needing glycaemic and weight control. Further long‐term and controlled studies are needed to clarify its role in regional diabetes management.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Diabetes is a growing global health crisis, causing rising morbidity, mortality, and healthcare burden [ 1 ,  2 ]. In 2022, 828 million adults were affected with an increase of 630 million since 1990, primarily in low‐ and middle‐income countries [ 1 ]. Type 2 diabetes (T2D) made up 93.5% of cases, driven by aging, urbanization, and unhealthy lifestyles [ 2 ].
Thailand ranks among Southeast Asian countries with the highest T2D prevalence, with 8200 cases per 100 000 population in 2019, and incidence continues to rise [ 3 ]. Despite proactive screening programmes, the true burden is likely underestimated due to undiagnosed cases and patients not receiving treatment [ 4 ].
Poor glycaemic control accelerates microvascular and macrovascular complications, including chronic kidney disease (CKD) and cardiovascular disease (CVD) [ 5 ,  6 ], highlighting the need for effective prevention and management. Since 2011, Thailand's National Health Security Office (NHSO) Diabetes Clinical Practice Guidelines adopted HbA1c levels to assess glycaemic control, targeting < 7% (53 mmol/mol) for non‐pregnant adults. This target is consistently endorsed by national and international guidelines [ 7 ,  8 ,  9 ,  10 ]. However, large real‐world data (2011–2018) indicate that only 33%–35% of Thai patients achieve this target, underscoring gaps in diabetes care [ 11 ].
Glycaemic control in T2D is challenged by complex pathophysiology requiring combination therapy, poor patient adherence due to treatment burden and socioeconomic factors, and clinical inertia or delayed treatment driven by concerns over side effects and patient‐related delays in treatment [ 12 ,  13 ,  14 ].
Oral semaglutide has demonstrated superior glycaemic and weight reductions versus placebo and standard oral therapies in a recently published clinical trial, achieving HbA1c reductions up to 1.2% with a low risk of hypoglycaemia due to its glucose‐dependent mechanism [ 15 ]. As the first oral glucagon‐like peptide‐1 receptor agonist (GLP‐1 RA), it offers a non‐injectable alternative with efficacy comparable to injectable GLP‐1 RAs and remains effective as monotherapy [ 15 ,  16 ], potentially improving adherence and glycaemic outcomes.
Despite positive trial results, real‐world evidence on oral semaglutide is scarce, mostly from Europe and North America. In Asia, data are largely from Japan, where the PIONEER REAL study reported HbA1c and weight reductions of 0.7%‐points and 2.8 kg over 40 weeks, with no new safety findings [ 17 ]. These results support its effectiveness and tolerability in routine practice. The applicability of outcomes from Japan and Western countries to Thai or other Asian populations remains uncertain due to differences in clinical characteristics and diabetes management.
To address limited real‐world data on oral semaglutide in Southeast Asia, this study evaluates clinical outcomes in Thai patients with T2D, providing the first evidence from Thailand. Such evidence is vital for guiding diabetes care strategies aimed at reducing complications, mortality, and healthcare burdens across Southeast Asian countries.

Study Design:
This retrospective, single‐arm, multi‐centre cohort study was conducted using routine clinical records from four university hospitals in Bangkok, Pathum Thani, and Chiang Mai, Thailand.
Ethical approvals were obtained from Central Research Ethics Committee (Reference number: COA‐CREC075/2024), Siriraj Institutional Review Board (approved on 22 June 2024), Human Research Ethics Committee, Faculty of Medicine, Thammasat University (Reference number: 176/2024), Research Ethics Committee, Faculty of Medicine, Chiang Mai University (Reference number: 285/2024), and Ethics Committee of Chulabhorn Research Institute, Chulabhorn Hospital (Reference number: EC092/2567). The study complied with the Declaration of Helsinki and ICH‐GCP guidelines. Given its retrospective design, informed consent was waived, and all data were anonymised to maintain confidentiality.

Study Population:
Eligible participants were Thai adults (≥ 18 years) with confirmed T2D [ 7 ] and an HbA1c result within 12 weeks before starting oral semaglutide (April 2022–December 2023). Eligible patients must not have received prior injectable glucose‐lowering therapy (insulin and GLP‐1 receptor agonists) and were required to complete at least 6 months of continuous oral semaglutide treatment, allowing for temporary discontinuation of up to 2 weeks to reflect real‐world practice. Exclusions included type 1 diabetes or prior study participation. Full criteria are summarised in Table  S1 .

Study Procedures:
This retrospective, non‐interventional study used assessment timing from routine clinical care. For descriptive purposes, visits were grouped into predefined time windows. Baseline (V1) was defined as the date of oral semaglutide initiation. Baseline HbA1c was the most recent value within 12 weeks before initiation, and baseline body weight and other clinical variables were taken as the measurement closest to initiation (within 52 weeks, where applicable). Intermediate visits (V2) included routine follow‐up from Day 2 to Week 20. The end‐of‐study (EoS) assessment was defined as the HbA1c measurement closest to Week 26 (±6 weeks), consistent with standard 6‐month follow‐up. An optional extended observation period (Weeks 33–62) included patients who continued therapy per routine care, without additional eligibility criteria.
Collected data included patient demographics (age, sex), duration of T2D, smoking status, comorbidities, presence of microvascular complications, laboratory parameters (HbA1c, eGFR), details of anti‐diabetic medications, oral semaglutide dosage, and the clinical rationale for its initiation. Observed HbA1c and body weight were summarised descriptively within visit windows. Estimates of change from baseline were derived separately using mixed models for repeated measures (MMRM) based on the actual timing of assessments relative to treatment initiation.

Study Endpoints:
The primary endpoint was the change in HbA1c (%‐points) from baseline to EoS (Week 26). The secondary endpoints included absolute (kg) and relative (%) changes in body weight (BW), as well as the relative change in HbA1c and the proportion of participants who achieved an HbA1c level < 7% over the same period. A post hoc endpoint assessed the proportion of participants who met a composite target of HbA1c < 7% combined with a ≥ 3% reduction in BW from baseline [ 18 ]. The exploratory endpoints included absolute and relative changes in HbA1c and BW from baseline to Week 52, assessed in the overall population and stratified by CKD stage. The prescription patterns of oral semaglutide and changes in the prescribed dosage over time were described. Concomitant of non‐semaglutide glucose‐lowering medications were described at baseline and EoS. The occurrence of severe hypoglycaemia events, if any, was recorded from treatment initiation to V5.

Definitions:
Obesity in Asian adults is defined as a body mass index (BMI) ≥ 25.0 kg/m 2 , based on the lowered cutoff recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO) and adopted in Thailand [ 19 ,  20 ]. Similarly, CKD is defined as estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) < 60 mL/min/1.73 m 2 , indicating moderate kidney impairment to kidney failure (Stages 3 or higher), in accordance with international Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) criteria, with eGFR ≥ 60 mL/min/1.73 m 2  indicating normal kidney function in the Thai context [ 21 ,  22 ,  23 ].

Statistical Analyses:
The sample size ensured 90% power to obtain a 95% confidence interval for a mean HbA1c reduction of 0.46%‐points, assuming a standard deviation of 1.7 based on prior studies. This required 146 patients with EoS HbA1c data, accounting for 70% data availability; 209 patients were targeted for treatment initiation.
The full analysis set (FAS) comprised all eligible patients. The primary (‘in‐study’) analysis included all available endpoint data from the FAS (regardless of treatment status). The ‘on‐treatment’ analysis, a subset of the in‐study data, included only measurements taken during periods of active treatment. Sensitivity analyses were conducted in study ‘completers’, patients with EoS data ( n  = 154 for HbA1c;  n  = 141 for body weight).
Changes in HbA1c and body weight were analysed using MMRM with restricted maximum likelihood, including all post‐baseline values. Fixed effects included time since initiation (linear and quadratic), time‐varying dose (0, 3, 7, and 14 mg), and baseline HbA1c (or baseline body weight for body weight outcome) [ 24 ,  25 ]. Random intercepts for study site and patient, and random slopes for time accounted for clustering and individual variability, with an unstructured covariance matrix to accommodate irregular intervals. Missing post‐baseline data were implicitly handled under the missing‐at‐random assumption. For sensitivity analyses with reduced sample sizes (EoS completers, baseline HbA1c ≥ 7%, baseline BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2 , stable background anti‐diabetic medications, and CKD subgroups), the fully adjusted MMRM did not converge; therefore, a reduced MMRM including time since semaglutide initiation only was applied.
All statistical analyses were performed using Stata Statistical Software: Stata 18.5 (StataCorp, TX, USA) and R 4.4.0 (R Core Team, 2024).

Study Populations and Baseline Characteristics:
Between April 2022 and December 2023, 222 participants initiating oral semaglutide were screened. Twenty‐seven were excluded for missing data, treatment interruptions, prior injectables, or protocol deviations (Figure  1 ). The remaining 195 participants comprised the FAS. Of all participants, 154 (79.0%) had HbA1c data at EoS (Week 26) and were classified as “completers”, while 72 (36.9%) had available HbA1c data at Week 52 (V5) during the extended follow‐up period. Eight patients (4.1%) discontinued treatment after EoS, mainly due to changes in treatment strategy or other unspecified reasons.
Table  1  presents the baseline characteristics of the study participants; the mean age was 56.5 years (SD 11.9), and 58.5% were female. Among 160 participants with available duration of T2D data, the mean diabetes duration (SD) was 8.8 (7.3) years, with 40.6% having T2D for less than 5 years and 40.6% having T2D for 10 or more years. Of all participants, mean baseline HbA1c (SD) was 7.4 (1.2) %; 63.1% of participants had HbA1c ≥ 7%. Mean BMI (SD) was 30.9 (5.9) kg/m 2 , with 84.6% classified as obese (BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2 ). Diabetic nephropathy (14.4%) was the most common microvascular complication. Hypertension (74.4%) and dyslipidaemia (77.9%) were prevalent, and 10.8% had CVD. Of the 188 participants with baseline renal function data, mean eGFR (SD) was 89.1 (22.3) mL/min/1.73m 2 ; 25 (13.3%) had CKD stage 3 or higher. Only 2% were current smokers.
Semaglutide was primarily initiated for glycaemic control (91.0%) and weight reduction (83.1%), with 15.3% for cardiovascular risk management. It was prescribed as an add‐on therapy in 97.4% of participants and as a first‐ or second‐line therapy for the rest (data not shown). Common concomitant agents were metformin (65.6%), SGLT‐2 inhibitors (49.2%), and sulphonylureas (35.4%); none received insulin or other GLP‐1 receptor agonists. Over half (107; 54.9%) were treated with ≥ 2 glucose‐lowering agents (Table  S2 ).

Change in Glycated Haemoglobin:
In the FAS ( n  = 195), the estimated mean reduction in HbA1c from baseline to EoS was −0.7 percentage points (95% CI: −0.9 to −0.5;  p  < 0.0001), and a relative decrease of −9.8% (95% CI: −12.0 to −7.5;  p  < 0.0001) (Table  2 , Figure  2A ). The observed change in HbA1c was −1.0 (SD: 1.2) percentage points (Tables  2  and  S3 ). A similar reduction was observed in the on‐treatment analysis, with an estimated change of −0.7 percentage points (95% CI: −0.9 to −0.5;  p  < 0.0001) (Table  S4 ). Comparable HbA1c reductions were observed across sensitivity analyses restricted to the EoS completers ( n  = 154), patients with baseline HbA1c ≥ 7% ( n  = 123), and those with stable background anti‐diabetic medications ( n  = 70) (Table  S4 ).
Participants with normal or mildly decreased kidney function (baseline eGFR ≥ 60 mL/min/1.73 m 2 ;  n  = 163) experienced an average HbA1c reduction of −0.9 percentage points (95% CI: −1.0 to −0.8;  p  < 0.0001). Similarly, those with CKD (Stage 3 or higher;  n  = 25) experienced a comparable reduction of −0.8 percentage points (95% CI: −1.1 to −0.6;  p  < 0.0001) at the EoS. The relative reductions were −12.2% and −11.3%, respectively.

Change in Body Weight:
In the FAS ( n  = 194), the estimated mean reduction in body weight from baseline to EoS was −4.4 kg (95% CI: −5.5 to −3.3;  p  < 0.0001), corresponding to a relative decrease of −5.3% (95% CI: −6.5% to −4.0%;  p  < 0.0001). Comparable reductions were observed in the on‐treatment analysis, with a mean change of −4.6 kg (95% CI: −5.7 to −3.5;  p  < 0.0001) and a relative reduction of −5.5% (95% CI: −6.8% to −4.2%;  p  < 0.0001) (Table  2 , Figure  2B ). Results were consistent across sensitivity analyses, including EoS completers ( n  = 141), patients with baseline BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2  ( n  = 159), and those with stable background anti‐diabetic medications ( n  = 70), all showing significant results (Table  S5 ).
Participants with normal or mildly decreased kidney function experienced an unadjusted mean body weight reduction of −4.3 kg (95% CI: −5.2 to −3.5;  p  < 0.0001) at EoS, corresponding to a relative decrease of −5.2% (95% CI: −6.1% to −4.2%;  p  < 0.0001). Those with CKD stage 3 or higher showed a slightly less reduction of −3.7 kg (95% CI: −6.9 to −0.5;  p  = 0.024) and −4.8% (95% CI: −8.8% to −0.8%;  p  = 0.020), respectively.

HbA1c < 7% Achievement and Composite Benefits (HbA1c < 7% Combined With a BW Reduction of ≥ 3%):
Of the 154 participants who completed the EoS, 117 (75.9%) achieved an HbA1c level below 7%; notably, 44.4% (52/117) had baseline HbA1c ≥ 7%. Within the subgroup of 123 participants with complete HbA1c and body weight data at baseline and EoS, 71 (57.7%) achieved the composite outcome of HbA1c < 7% and ≥ 3% weight reduction. Among those with suboptimal baseline metabolic profiles (HbA1c ≥ 7% and BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2 ), 30 of 61 (49.2%) met this composite target. Notably, 74.4% (32 of 43) of participants who were obese (BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2 ) at baseline but had adequate glycaemic control (HbA1c < 7%) also achieved the composite outcome at EoS. The distribution of composite outcomes stratified by baseline metabolic profile categories is shown in Figure  3 .

Treatment Patterns and Occurrence of Severe Hypoglycaemia Events:
Of 195 participants, 180 (92.3%) started on the lowest dose of oral semaglutide (3 mg), consistent with recommended titration protocols (Figure  S3 , Table  S6 ). By the EoS (Week 26), 59.6% of participants initiating at 3 mg and 81.8% initiating at 7 mg had escalated to 14 mg. All participants starting at 14 mg maintained this dose without reduction. Among those who attended Week 52 ( n  = 71), 28.2% of participants remained on 7 mg, while the rest maintained 14 mg; no participants continued on the 3 mg dose (Table  S6 ).
At the EoS, oral semaglutide was discontinued in eight patients. Of these, three were transitioned to subcutaneous semaglutide, while the subsequent treatment status of the remaining five patients was unknown.
No cases of severe hypoglycaemia were reported throughout the observation period, including up to Week 52.

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
The REALISED study provides the first real‐world evidence of oral semaglutide in Thai patients with T2D, demonstrating a mean HbA1c reduction of 0.7%‐points and ~4.4 kg weight loss over 26 weeks. Similar benefits were observed in patients with CKD, as semaglutide's pharmacokinetics are unaffected by renal function [ 26 ]. Glycaemic control was achieved in 75.9% of participants (HbA1c < 7%), and 57.7% met the composite endpoint of HbA1c < 7% with ≥ 3% weight reduction. Among those with baseline HbA1c ≥ 7% and BMI ≥ 25 kg/m 2 , nearly half (49.2%) met both targets.
Cross‐study comparisons should be cautious due to design and geographic differences. REALISED's 26‐week follow‐up is shorter than the 34–40 weeks in PIONEER REAL studies, yet HbA1c reduction aligns with findings from Japan, Italy, Sweden, and Switzerland (0.7%–0.9%‐points) [ 17 ,  27 ,  28 ,  29 ]. Larger reductions (1.1%–1.4%‐points) have been reported in Western cohorts such as Canada, the UK, the Netherlands, and Spain [ 30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ], while Japanese retrospective studies show a broader range (0.6%–1.2%‐point) [ 34 ,  35 ,  36 ].
Variations in glycaemic outcomes across studies may reflect differences in dosing practices influenced by baseline patient characteristics [ 37 ]. While 10%–20% of Western patients initiated oral semaglutide at 7 mg [ 30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ], nearly all participants in REALISED and PIONEER REAL Japan began at 3 mg [ 17 ], per label. Dose escalation to 14 mg occurred in 53.2% of patients in this study and only 11% in the Japan study, compared to up to 66% in Western cohorts [ 30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ], potentially due to tolerability, clinical inertia, or use for cardiovascular benefits in patients with good baseline glycaemic control [ 38 ]. Lower baseline HbA1c in REALISED (~7.4%) versus Western studies (~7.7%–8.6%) may have limited absolute HbA1c reduction [ 30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ,  37 ]. Nonetheless, the proportion achieving HbA1c < 7% aligns with PIONEER studies (~55%–77%) [ 17 ,  27 ,  28 ,  29 ,  30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ], supporting oral semaglutide use in diverse real‐world settings.
Weight loss is a key component of T2D management, addressing obesity‐related metabolic dysfunction and improving glycaemic control [ 8 ,  9 ,  39 ]. Oral semaglutide consistently shows meaningful weight reduction across populations. In REALISED, the observed loss (−4.3 kg at EoS; −4.7 kg at Week 52) falls within the range reported in PIONEER REAL studies (−3.8 to −5.8 kg) [ 17 ,  40 ]. Greater reductions in Western cohorts likely reflect higher baseline BMI (31–35 kg/m 2 ) [ 33 ,  40 ], while smaller changes in the Japanese study (BMI ~26.5 kg/m 2 ) align with lower starting weight [ 17 ]. The greater weight loss observed in this study may be partly attributed to the discontinuation of weight‐promoting antidiabetic agents such as sulphonylureas and thiazolidinediones [ 41 ]. Specifically, the proportion of patients using sulphonylureas decreased from 35% to 22%, and thiazolidinediones from 27% to 18% during the study period (Table  S2 ). In the PIONEER REAL studies, 20%–35% of participants achieved the dual target of ≥ 1% HbA1c reduction and ≥ 5% weight loss [ 33 ,  40 ]. Meanwhile, the REALISED study, which applied a slightly less stringent composite endpoint of HbA1c < 7% and ≥ 3% weight loss, found that 57.7% of patients met this goal. Although there are minor variations in endpoint definitions, both studies provide evidence that oral semaglutide delivers meaningful clinical outcomes.
Patients in this Thai study were managed similarly to those in the European PIONEER REAL studies, with most receiving metformin and/or other oral antidiabetic agents before starting semaglutide [ 28 ,  29 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ]. As all PIONEER REAL protocols enrolled individuals naïve to injectable therapies (i.e., no prior GLP‐1 RA or insulin use), comparisons with these studies provide useful context [ 28 ,  29 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ]. In Thailand, 97.4% of patients received oral semaglutide as an add‐on therapy, most commonly in combination with metformin (65.6%) and SGLT‐2 inhibitors (49.2%). This suggests semaglutide was often used as an adjunct therapy. Thai patients demonstrated a relatively well‐tolerated profile, with 4.1% discontinuing treatment at EoS and at Week 52, compared to 7%–12% in Japanese and Italian cohorts [ 17 ,  27 ]. No severe hypoglycaemia occurred in REALISED despite ~55% using sulphonylureas or insulin secretagogues, consistent with the glucose‐dependent action of GLP‐1 RAs and findings from other studies [ 42 ,  43 ].
It is important to consider the limitations of REALISED study, many of which are common to retrospective real‐world studies. First, its single‐arm design precludes causal inference, as improvements may reflect lifestyle or co‐medication effects that is, SGLT2 inhibitors, with most patients using oral semaglutide as add‐on therapy. Restriction to patients with complete data may introduce selection bias, partly mitigated by consecutive inclusion. Some residual confounding may persist, as not all potential confounders were adjusted for in the MMRM due to the limited sample size; thus, the estimated benefits of oral semaglutide should be interpreted with caution. Second, incomplete routine records and non‐standardised weight measurements may have introduced variability, reducing the precision of findings. This was mitigated by including site as a random effect in the statistical model. Adherence to semaglutide's dosing instructions (fasting intake with water only) could not be assessed, which may have influenced efficacy and tolerability. However, this reflects real‐world conditions where adherence varies. Safety outcomes assessed only severe hypoglycaemia, constraining conclusions on overall tolerability and safety. Third, the CKD subgroup analysis in this study was limited by a small sample size, therefore the findings should be interpreted cautiously. However, it offers initial insights into treatment outcomes for patients with renal impairment and highlights the need for validation in larger, prospective cohorts. Fourth, the follow‐up duration was relatively short (6 months for the primary endpoint, with a subset observed to 12 months). Although this timeframe is sufficient to assess medium‐term glycaemic and weight changes, it limits insight on long‐term durability. Fifth, the study was conducted in an urban tertiary care setting, where better access to specialist care and higher socioeconomic status may have contributed to more favourable outcomes than those seen in primary or secondary care setting, where T2D patients are typically managed. Nevertheless, it offers a valuable benchmark for future research in more diverse populations. Finally, the Thai cohort's lower baseline HbA 1 c and BMI compared to Western populations may affect applicability. Nonetheless, consistency with PIONEER REAL studies suggests broader relevance. To validate these findings, future research with extended follow‐up should assess adherence, dosing appropriateness, and safety to clarify glycaemic effects and durability of oral semaglutide benefits in routine practice.
A key strength of this study is its multi‐centre design involving four university hospitals across different regions of Thailand, which contributes to broader national relevance within the context of tertiary care. The findings are consistent with PIONEER REAL studies, reinforcing the benefits of oral semaglutide as an add‐on therapy in improving HbA1c and weight in routine practice.
