Title: Dapagliflozin improves hemoglobin and anemia in chronic kidney disease: a systematic review and meta-analysis
Year: 2026
Type: review
Drugs: empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, ertugliflozin
Keywords: Anemia of chronic disease, chronic kidney disease, dapagliflozin, erythropoiesis, hemoglobin, sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors
Domain: Type 2 Diabetes Drug Interactions
[END METADATA]

[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
Abstract Anemia becomes increasingly prevalent as kidney function declines. Current treatments carry multiple safety risks. Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2i), dapagliflozin, have emerged as background anemia modulator, a promising alternative, showing protective effects against anemia. This study synthesizes evidence on dapagliflozin’s potential to improve anemia in chronic kidney disease (CKD) patients. A systematic review and meta-analysis was performed according to Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines, including eight studies identified from six databases. Risk of bias was assessed using tools appropriate for each study design (RoB 2, NOS, JBI). Data were pooled using a random-effects model with RevMan 5.4. The primary analysis for hemoglobin demonstrated significant increase (MD = 4.37; 95% CI = 0.71–8.03;  p  = 0.02) and remained significant in the sensitivity analysis (MD = 4.11; 95% CI = 0.19–8.04;  p  = 0.04). A sensitivity analysis for hematocrit revealed a highly significant increase (MD = 2.15; 95% CI = 1.86–2.44;  p  < 0.00001). In the sensitivity analysis for adverse events dapagliflozin significantly reduced the overall risk (RR = 0.77; 95% CI = 0.59–0.99;  p  = 0.04). This finding was associated with significantly lower mortality (RR = 0.67;  p  = 0.02), while no significant differences were observed for cardiovascular ( p  = 0.22) or genitourinary events ( p  = 0.70). Dapagliflozin is associated with clinically meaningful improvements in hemoglobin and anemia outcomes in CKD. These findings suggest a potential erythropoietic benefit beyond glycemic control, although dedicated anemia-focused trials are needed to confirm clinical applicability.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Anemia is a common and serious complication in patients with kidney problems, particularly in those with advanced disease, defined by the Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) guideline as a hemoglobin level <130 g/L in men and <120 g/L in women [ 1 ]. Its prevalence and severity increase as glomerular filtration rate (GFR) declines below 60 mL/min/1.73 m 2 . While it affects up to 20% of patients in stage three Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD), this prevalence rises dramatically, with at least 90% of dialysis-dependent patients eventually developing anemia [ 1–3 ]. This condition primarily results from inadequate production of erythropoietin (EPO), a hormone produced by the kidneys that stimulates red blood cell formation in the bone marrow [ 4 ]. As kidney function declines, the peritubular fibroblasts responsible for EPO production become damaged or fibrotic, leading to insufficient synthesis. This deficiency, combined with chronic inflammation, iron dysregulation, and blood loss during dialysis, contributes significantly to the development of normocytic normochromic anemia in patients with kidney disease.
Current management focuses on two strategies to address this EPO deficiency. The first involves erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs), which act as exogenous (synthetic) EPO replacement therapy [ 5 ]. The other strategy focuses on hypoxia-inducible factors (HIF), utilizing HIF prolyl-hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) to stabilize these transcription factors and stimulate the body’s endogenous (natural) EPO production [ 6 ]. Beyond these primary interventions, comprehensive anemia management also includes iron therapy to ensure substrate availability, adjuvants like vitamin B12 and folate to support cellular maturation, and red blood cell (RBC) transfusions as a rescue intervention for acute or severe symptomatic cases [ 7 ]. However, both primary therapeutic classes carry a challenging safety landscape. ESAs are known to increase blood viscosity and reduce vasodilation, leading to a higher risk of ischemic stroke, myocardial infarction, and venous thromboembolism. While developed as an oral alternative, HIF-PHIs have not fully resolved these safety issues; clinical trials indicate they also carry significant risks of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) and thromboembolic events (including deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and vascular access thrombosis). Furthermore, both drug classes share theoretical concerns regarding the potential progression of tumorigenesis [ 8 ].
There is a clinical need for alternative or adjunctive therapies to serve as effective, safer background anemia modulators for long-term use. Landmark trials such as DAPA-CKD and EMPA-KIDNEY have demonstrated the pleiotropic effects of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor (SGLT2i) addressing this issue, including renal protection and cardiovascular benefits in diabetic and non-diabetic populations [ 8 ]. Dapagliflozin belongs to the class of SGLT2i, originally developed for type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) management. Dapagliflozin stands out among SGLT2i due to several key advantages. It has one of the longest bioavailability durations, approximately two hours, allowing for sustained therapeutic effects. Among its class, dapagliflozin is supported by the most extensive clinical trial data, making its pharmacological impact and efficacy the most comprehensively studied and understood. Notably, it is the only SGLT2i currently available in generic form, significantly reducing its cost and improving accessibility for the broader population [ 9 ]. Clinically, dapagliflozin is considered to have the strongest renoprotective effect, both in patients with T2DM and those with CKD.
The primary mechanism of this drug class is linked to the sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2), encoded by the SLC5A2 gene, plays a key role in renal glucose reabsorption. SGLT2 is the main transporter responsible for glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule of the nephron, accounting for 80–97% of this process and playing a crucial role in renal glucose homeostasis [ 10 , 11 ]. Inhibitors of SGLT2, such as canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, and ertugliflozin, are antihyperglycemic agents that function by preventing the reabsorption of filtered glucose from the tubular lumen [ 10 , 11 ]. These drugs not only improve blood sugar control in adults with T2DM but have also been consistently shown to reduce hospitalizations in patients with heart failure [ 5 , 8 ]. Furthermore, SGLT2i enhances natriuresis by a cross-reaction with the renal sodium hydrogen exchanger, promoting the excretion of electrolyte-free water  via  an osmotic effect. This mechanism helps to decongest the interstitium without causing significant changes in intravascular volume.
Beyond their initial indication for glycemic control and reducing heart failure hospitalization, emerging primary studies have reported that dapagliflozin may lead to increased hemoglobin levels in CKD patients [ 12 ]. However, no comprehensive meta-analysis and systematic review has focused specifically on the effect of dapagliflozin on anemia in CKD patients. A synthesized evaluation is necessary to determine the consistency and strength of evidence regarding dapagliflozin’s potential hematologic effects in this specific high-risk population.

Study design, data sources, and search strategies:
The reporting of this Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis adheres to the ‘Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analyses’ (PRISMA) 2020 statement ( Supplementary Table 1 ) [ 13 ]. The protocol was then registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO: CRD420251146999). A comprehensive search was performed across the following databases and platforms: PubMed, Scopus, ScienceDirect, the Cochrane Library, Wiley Online Library, and Taylor & Francis Online. Each Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) are provided in ( Supplementary Table 2 ). The search strategy targeted titles, abstracts, and keywords using the following related search terms: chronic kidney disease, dapagliflozin, and anemia.

Eligibility and selection criteria:
The review criteria were aligned with the population, intervention, comparison, and outcome (PICO) framework. Inclusion criteria were: (1) primary studies; (2) adult populations with CKD; (3) treatment with dapagliflozin; (4) articles published in English. Exclusion criteria were: (1) studies using other types of SGLT2i; (2) irrelevant anemia-related outcomes; (3) articles available only as abstracts, conference proceedings, or symposium publications.

Data extraction, data synthesis, and assessed outcome:
Data synthesis involved narrative qualitative synthesis to identify feasible outcomes, while the quantitative meta-analysis was restricted to data from Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), using statistical software Cochrane’s Review Manager 5.4 assessing mean and standard deviation (SD) with their respective 95% confidence interval (CI), random effect. For continuous results mean difference (MD) was used as the summary measure since all outcomes were reported on the same scale allowing for direct and clinically interpretable comparisons and for dichotomous such adverse events reported in risk ratio (RR) provides an intuitive and clinically meaningful measure [ 14 ]. Data were pooled using a random-effects model, chosen over a fixed-effect model due to significant anticipated heterogeneity in study populations (e.g., CKD with vs. without T2DM), study designs (e.g., parallel vs. crossover RCTs), and definition of anemia. This model accounts for such variance and provides a more conservative estimate of the average effect. The key assessed outcomes included hematologic measurements (hemoglobin and hematocrit changes) and adverse events, which were synthesized quantitatively and presented in forest plots, alongside a narrative review of potential anemia correction mechanisms.
For data synthesis, a random-effects meta-analysis was planned. Statistical heterogeneity was assessed using the chi-squared (Cochran’s Q) test, with its significance determined based on its degrees of freedom (df), calculated as the number of included studies minus one [ 15 ]. To quantify the impact of this heterogeneity, the I 2  statistic was used. This metric estimates the percentage of total variation across studies that is due to genuine between-study differences rather than sampling error. High I 2  values imply that the studies are estimating different underlying effects, thus justifying the random-effects model. Thresholds for I 2  values were defined at 25%, 50%, and 75% for low, moderate, and high heterogeneity, respectively [ 16 ]. The between-study variance was estimated by Tau-squared (τ 2 ). Finally, the overall pooled effect and its 95% CI were calculated, with its statistical significance determined by a Z-test ( p  < 0.05) [ 17 ].

Quality appraisal:
Study quality was assessed according to design by two independent reviewers, with any discrepancies resolved by a third reviewer. Post-hoc analyses of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and crossover trials were appraised using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2 (RoB 2) tool across five domains: randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, measurement of the outcome, and selection of the reported result [ 18 ]. Overall judgments were low risk, some concerns, or high risk of bias. Observational studies were evaluated with the Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (NOS) across three domains: selection (4 stars), comparability (2 stars), and outcome (3 stars), and categorized according to Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) standards [ 19 ]. Case reports were assessed with the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) Critical Appraisal Checklist, consisting of eight items covering demographics, history, chronology, diagnostics, intervention, outcomes, adverse events, and key lessons [ 20 ].

Study selection process:
The systematic search yielded a total of 1520 records. After removing 711 duplicates and 543 records marked as ineligible by automation tools, 266 unique records underwent initial screening. Of these, 217 records were excluded based on PICO criteria, leaving 49 reports to be sought for retrieval, seven of which could not be retrieved. Finally, 42 reports were assessed for eligibility, resulting in the exclusion of 34 due to ineligible study design, leaving 8 studies included in the systematic review and meta-analysis ( Figure 1 ).

Study and population characteristics:
Eight studies were included, comprising three post-hoc analyses of RCTs, one randomized crossover trial, three observational studies (prospective cohort, prospective, and retrospective), and one case report. The dosage used was 10 mg per day compared with a placebo, except for the prospective study and the case report. Across the included studies with defined sample sizes, a cumulative total of 4,498 adults with CKD were evaluated. The included studies collectively involved participants from 24 countries across multiple continents, encompassing regions in North and South America, Europe, Asia, and Africa. ( Supplementary Table 3 ).

Quality assessment of included study:
The methodological quality of the eight included studies was assessed using design-specific tools, with findings summarized in ( Supplementary Table 4 ) ( Figure 2 ). The four RCTs and post-hoc analyses demonstrated high methodological quality; three studies (Koshino 2023, Cherney 2020, and Stefansson 2020) were judged at low risk of bias across all domains, while one post-hoc study (Koshino(b) 2023) was rated as Some concerns due to potential bias in the selection of the reported result (D5) [ 21–24 ]. The observational studies showed varied quality: the prospective cohort by Elwaraky 2025 was rated at low risk, while the single-arm studies by Shibata 2023 and Osonoi 2023 were rated as moderate risk (some concerns), primarily due to a high risk of bias in the ‘Comparability’ domain (D2) [ 25–27 ]. The case report by Pasari 2024 was rated as some concerns, with high risk identified in the ‘diagnostic tests’ (D4) and ‘adverse events’ (D7) domains [ 28 ].

Hemoglobin (g/L):
The quantitative analysis of four studies ( n  = 4,227) demonstrated that dapagliflozin significantly increased hemoglobin levels compared with placebo (MD = 4.37 g/L; 95% CI 0.71–8.03 g/L;  p  = 0.02; I 2  = 100%) ( Figure 3 ). This finding remained significant in a sensitivity analysis ( n  = 4,123) after excluding one cross-sectional study (MD = 4.11 g/L; 95% CI 0.19–8.04 g/L;  p  = 0.04; I 2  = 100%), indicating the robustness of the overall result ( Figure 4 ).
Systematic review across other included studies consistently find that dapagliflozin treatment is associated with an increase in hemoglobin levels. The studies ranged from a large prospective cohort to smaller prospective, retrospective, and case-level reports, investigating populations with T2DM, CKD, or both. A large prospective cohort study by Elwaraky et al. (2025) in patients with T2DM and CKD demonstrated that dapagliflozin significantly increased the frequency of an hemoglobin rise 10 g/L (66.7% vs. 14.3%,  p  < 0.001) and the rate of anemia correction (53.6% vs. 4.8%,  p  < 0.001) compared to placebo [ 25 ]. This finding was supported by a smaller prospective study, which found that dapagliflozin 5 mg/day significantly increased hemoglobin 12–13 g/L from baseline over 12 weeks ( p  < 0.01) [ 25 , 27 ]. Similarly, a retrospective study by Shibata et al. (2023) observed an increase in hemoglobin levels in CKD patients without T2DM from 123 ± 16 g/L to 126 ± 16 g/L within the first month ( p  < 0.0001), a level that was sustained at 2 months (127 ± 17 g/L) [ 26 ]. Furthermore, a case report by Pasari et al. (2024) highlighted the potential magnitude of this effect, describing two patients whose hemoglobin levels rose from baselines of 158 g/L and 148 g/L to 188 g/L and 186 g/L 28 . Despite its benefits, one study showed that dapagliflozin use was associated with a significantly higher risk of iron deficiency anemia (IDA) (36.7% vs. 18.2%,  p  = 0.005), aligning with mechanistic data from another study which showed significant decreases in ferritin, hepcidin, and transferrin saturation [ 25 , 27 ].

Hematocrit (%):
A pooled analysis of three studies ( n  = 3,528) for hematocrit showed no statistically significant difference (MD = 1.44; 95% CI = −0.10–2.98;  p  = 0.07; I 2  = 100%) between the dapagliflozin and placebo groups ( Figure 5 ). However, after a sensitivity analysis excluded one cross-sectional study, the remaining two studies ( n  = 3,423) showed a highly significant increase in hematocrit (MD = 2.15; 95% CI = 1.86–2.44;  p  < 0.00001; I 2  = 100%) ( Figure 6 ).
One prospective study found a significant ( p  < 0.01) increase in mean hematocrit over 12 weeks in both the Full Analysis Set (FAS), from approximately 37.5% to 40.5%, and the Per-Protocol Set (PPS), from approximately 38.0% to 41.0% [ 27 ].

Findings on adverse events genitourinary, cardiovascular, and mortality:
The primary analysis of adverse events, which pooled three and two studies in three subgroup analyses, showed a non-significant reduction in the overall risk (RR = 0.78; 95% CI = 0.59–1.02;  p  = 0.07; I 2  = 49%). Within this analysis, dapagliflozin was associated with significantly lower mortality (RR = 0.67;  p  = 0.02) but showed no significant difference for cardiovascular (RR = 0.77;  p  = 0.15) or genitourinary events (RR = 0.98;  p  = 0.97) ( Figure 7 ). Following a sensitivity analysis that excluded the crossover study, the pooled analysis of the remaining two studies demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in the overall risk of adverse events (RR = 0.77; 95% CI = 0.59–0.99;  p  = 0.04; I 2  = 53%). In this secondary analysis, the significant mortality benefit was confirmed (RR = 0.67;  p  = 0.02), while the cardiovascular (RR = 0.79;  p  = 0.22) and genitourinary (RR = 0.86;  p  = 0.70) subgroups remained non-significant ( Figure 8 ).

Dapagliflozin and improvements in hemoglobin and hematocrit as indicators of erythropoietic activity in CKD patients:
This systematic review and meta-analysis found that dapagliflozin significantly increases hemoglobin levels. A similar effect on hematocrit was also noted, although its statistical significance in the pooled analysis was heavily influenced by a single study, as revealed during sensitivity analysis. The observed increase in hemoglobin and hematocrit following dapagliflozin administration appears to be a multifactorial phenomenon, involving both an initial acute phase of hemoconcentration due to mild diuretic-induced plasma volume contraction and a more durable stimulation of true erythropoiesis with metabolic erythropoiesis. In CKD, intrinsic production of EPO as a critical hormone that stimulates the proliferation and differentiation of erythroid progenitor cells in the bone marrow is frequently impaired. This irreplaceable renal function is compromised as fibrosis and pericyte-to-myofibroblast transition reduce the capacity of HIF to activate the EPO gene, leading to anemia [ 29 , 30 ]. The increase in hemoglobin and hematocrit follows a distinct biphasic pattern. The initial, acute phase is widely attributed to a modest diuretic-induced contraction of plasma volume and is evident within the first 2 to 4 weeks [ 23 ]. This hemoconcentration, resulting from the osmotic diuretic effect of dapagliflozin, leads to a rapid increase in red cell indices by reducing plasma volume rather than by increasing red cell mass [ 29 , 30 ].
However, the persistent elevation beyond this acute phase suggests a second, sustained phase involving the genuine stimulation of true red blood cell production. This later phase is supported by several proposed biological pathways, metabolic erythropoiesis. One key mechanism is the improvement of renal oxygenation and tubulointerstitial hypoxia, which facilitates partial recovery of the capacity of peritubular cells to produce EPO [ 21 , 31 ]. Furthermore, this sustained correction deeply involves the interplay of inflammation and iron metabolism. CKD is characterized by persistent low-grade inflammation, elevating cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) that upregulate hepatic hepcidin, which in turn sequesters iron in storage sites and creates functional iron deficiency even in the presence of normal or high ferritin. Dapagliflozin breaks this cycle through anti-inflammatory action, as shown by studies documenting reductions in urinary monocyte chemoattractant protein-1, IL-6, and other inflammatory mediators. Decreased inflammation leads to suppression of hepcidin and facilitates the mobilization of iron stores, increasing iron bioavailability for erythropoiesis. Clinically, this is mirrored by a consistent decrease in serum ferritin in parallel with rising hemoglobin, reflecting the mobilization of iron from storage. Other proposed mechanisms include a potential EPO-independent pathway mediated by arginine vasopressin [ 21 , 31 ]. This unique pathophysiology fundamentally distinguishes dapagliflozin from HIF-PHIs ( Supplementary Table 5 ), as both are oral agents stimulating endogenous erythropoiesis but through divergent pathways.
Nevertheless, the relative contribution of these proposed pathways remains incompletely elucidated. The transient nature of the EPO surge challenges its role in the long-term maintenance of elevated hemoglobin, and the reported inconsistencies between changes in hepcidin and hemoglobin in some analyses suggests the relationship is more complex than initially posited [ 27 ]. Therefore, while the clinical observation is robust, the underlying pathophysiology is intricate and not yet fully elucidated. Altogether, these mechanisms support the view that SGLT2i like dapagliflozin correct anemia in CKD patients through a synergy of hemodynamic, anti-inflammatory, and iron-mobilizing pathways, rather than simple volume shifts alone, warranting further mechanistic work to delineate these complex interactions [ 28 ].

Clinical implications of dapagliflozin use and safety findings related to genitourinary, cardiovascular, and mortality outcomes:
Regarding safety, the most robust finding of this meta-analysis was a significant mortality benefit associated with dapagliflozin, while the trend toward reduced overall adverse events only reached statistical significance after sensitivity analysis. The non-significant findings for the cardiovascular and genitourinary subgroups are likely attributable to insufficient statistical power due to the very limited number of pooled studies. Evaluation of genitourinary adverse events encompassed a broad spectrum from urinary tract infections to severe outcomes like estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) decline or end-stage kidney disease (ESKD), did not demonstrate a statistically significant difference between dapagliflozin and placebo. This result must be interpreted with considerable caution, however, as there was significant variability in the findings of the individual studies. This disparity likely reflects the dual nature of SGLT2i, which may increase the risk for localized infections due to glycosuria while simultaneously offering long-term protection against major adverse kidney events [ 27 ]. Similarly, the analysis of cardiovascular events, including cardiovascular death or hospitalization for heart failure, also failed to show a statistically significant benefit. Despite this, a clear and clinically relevant trend toward cardiovascular protection was observed, aligning with the drug’s known beneficial mechanisms such as reducing cardiac preload and improving myocardial energetics [ 28 ]. In contrast to these specific endpoints, the analysis of all-cause mortality revealed a significant and robust benefit, with dapagliflozin substantially reducing the risk of death. These consistent findings suggest that anemia correction is potentially a mediator of mortality reduction, providing hypothesis for future trials [ 21 , 23 , 24 ].

Strengths and limitation:
This meta-analysis provides a timely and comprehensive synthesis of the emerging role of SGLT2i in renal care. A key strength is the inclusion of multinational studies, which broadens the data pool and enhances the representativeness and generalizability of our findings to the large global CKD population. Furthermore, by focusing specifically on dapagliflozin, this review offers drug-specific estimates rather than a more heterogeneous (and potentially confounded) class effect. The methodology is also strengthened by providing both quantitative and qualitative analyses, which offers a more complete picture of the evidence. This combined approach allows for clinically applicable insights into this emerging background anemia modulator therapeutic area.
Key limitations include the small number of available studies and the high heterogeneity observed for the primary hematological outcomes (I 2 =100%), which complicates interpretation. This extreme variance is likely driven by the significant clinical and methodological diversity of the pooled studies, such as the inclusion of both parallel-group and crossover RCTs (Cherney 2020) [22], the use of post-hoc data from varying populations (e.g., T2DM-focused vs. mixed CKD), and the inclusion of data using ‘anemia’ as a proxy for CKD (Stefansson 2020) [23]. Therefore, the pooled mean differences reported for hemoglobin and hematocrit should be interpreted with extreme caution, as they represent an average of substantially different clinical effects rather than a single, precise effect estimate. Furthermore, the generalizability of our findings is limited due to the significant reliance on post-hoc analyses and non-dialysis subgroup data from studies not originally designed to investigate these specific endpoints in an advanced CKD cohort. The current evidence is limited by the lack of crucial comparative studies, with no head-to-head trials against ESAs and no available data on ESA minimization or cost-effectiveness.

Future direction:
Future studies should move beyond post-hoc and heterogeneous analyses to prospective, anemia-focused randomized trials. These trials must explicitly test dapagliflozin as an ESA-sparing or ESA-modifying strategy across all CKD stages, including advanced non-dialysis and dialysis populations, stratified by baseline iron status, inflammation, and diabetes phenotype. High-impact research should integrate mechanistic biomarker panels (serial erythropoietin, hepcidin, ferritin kinetics, inflammatory cytokines, and renal oxygenation surrogates) to disentangle true erythropoiesis from hemoconcentration, while embedding adaptive trial designs to capture biphasic hematologic responses. Equally critical are head-to-head comparative effectiveness and cost-effectiveness studies against ESAs and HIF-PHIs, incorporating patient-centered outcomes (transfusion avoidance, fatigue, quality of life) and long-term safety signals (thrombosis, malignancy). Finally, leveraging real-world data, causal inference, and AI-enabled phenotyping to identify responders and optimize sequencing with iron and novel agents will position SGLT2 inhibition within precision anemia management pathways, maximizing translational impact.

[SECTION: CONCLUSION]
Dapagliflozin is associated with clinically meaningful improvements in hemoglobin and anemia outcomes in CKD. These findings suggest a potential erythropoietic benefit beyond glycemic control, helping to alleviate the significant burden of anemia. Notably, these hematologic improvements, combined with potential reduction in all-cause mortality, are highly encouraging, although dedicated anemia-focused trials are needed to confirm clinical applicability.
