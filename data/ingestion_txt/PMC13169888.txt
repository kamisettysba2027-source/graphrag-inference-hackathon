Title: Optimizing type 2 diabetes management in older adults with cancer through comprehensive geriatric assessment-guided medication reviews: a monocentric retrospective study

[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
Background Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and cancer frequently coexist in older adults, posing complex therapeutic management challenges, particularly due to polypharmacy, frailty and the risk of hypoglycemia. Although individualized glycemic targets and a personalized therapeutic approach are recommended, real-world prescribing practices may deviate from these guidelines. Objective To assess the alignment of pharmacological T2DM management with French guidelines in older adults with cancer undergoing comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA)-guided medication review (MR) prior to systemic cancer treatment. Methods We conducted a retrospective monocentric observational study including older outpatients aged ≥ 65 years with cancer and T2DM referred for CGA-guided MR at the geriatric oncology day unit of Marseille University Hospital between 2022 and 2023. Diabetes-related therapeutic issues (DTI) were identified, and associated factors were analyzed. Results Among the 189 patients included (median age: 79.2 years (IQR 75.8–83.7, min. 66, max. 93), 91% were frail or dependent, and 89.4% received ≥ 5 medications daily. The mean HbA1c level was 6.89 ± 1.03%, and 64.0% had HbA1c values outside their individualized target. DTI were observed in 67.7% of patients, mainly due to overly strict glycemic control and the use of hypoglycemia-prone agents. Multivariate analysis showed that age ≥ 75 years ( p  < 0.05), HbA1c below target ( p  < 0.001) and the use of ≥ 3 antidiabetics ( p  < 0.05) were significantly associated with DTI. Conclusion This study highlights a tendency toward overtreatment of T2DM in frail older adults with cancer. CGA-guided MRs, conducted collaboratively between geriatricians and pharmacists, allow better alignment with guidelines, reduce hypoglycemia risk, and support individualized care. Integrating these reviews before cancer treatment may enhance safety and clinical outcomes in this high-risk population.

Background:
Cancer and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) frequently coexist, affecting an estimated 8–18% of oncology patients [ 1 ]. With population ageing, the prevalence of T2DM among older adults is rising, and nearly one-quarter of individuals with diabetes are aged 70 years or older [ 2 ]. Compared to younger adults, older adults present a more heterogeneous profile in terms of diabetes-related complications, comorbidities, associated polypharmacy (defined here as the use of at least five prescribed medications per day [ 3 ]) and functional status [ 4 ]. Impaired counter-regulatory mechanisms render older adults more vulnerable to adverse effects, particularly hypoglycemia [ 5 ]. Reducing the risk of hypoglycemia in older adults with T2DM is essential, as low glucose levels can lead to falls and fractures, worsen cognitive impairment and precipitate cardiovascular events [ 6 – 9 ]. Cancer and oncology treatments may further contribute to hypoglycemia through associated symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, weight loss, and malnutrition [ 10 ].
All these factors highlight the challenges clinicians face in providing adequate pharmacological management of T2DM in older adults with cancer. Although neither advanced age nor cancer should be a barrier to diabetes treatment, therapeutic regimens may require adjustment in frail older adults or those with multiple comorbidities for reasons of safety and tolerability [ 11 ]. Based on results from the French study GERODIAB, which demonstrated that glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) thresholds are one of the main factors influencing survival in the older population [ 12 ], the Francophone Diabetes Society (SFD) recommends less stringent glycemic control for frail and dependent older adults than for the general healthy population, along with a more flexible therapeutic approach [ 13 ]. The American Diabetes Association (ADA) and the European Association for the Study of Diabetes (EASD) also recommend a personalized approach [ 14 ]. Recently, the International Geriatric Diabetes Society has proposed recommendations to optimize diabetes treatment regimens and improve care in older adults with T2DM [ 15 ]. To our knowledge, there has been a lack of guidelines specifically addressing pharmacological T2DM management in oncology, although preliminary guidance has been proposed to standardize pharmaceutical management in this population [ 16 ].
Comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) is a validated approach for managing older adults with cancer [ 17 ]. CGA helps identify vulnerabilities that may affect patients’ ability to tolerate cancer treatments, thereby guiding clinical decision-making and improving treatment outcomes. According to the International Society of Geriatric Oncology (SIOG) [ 18 ,  19 ], close collaboration between geriatricians and clinical pharmacists during CGAs leads to high-quality medication reviews (MRs) and helps prevent treatment-related adverse effects [ 20 ,  21 ]. For several years, our geriatric oncology day hospital unit has conducted CGA-guided MRs for older adults with cancer referred by oncologists prior to the initiation of systemic cancer treatment. Over the past two years, these CGA-guided MRs have revealed considerable heterogeneity in the management of T2DM among outpatients. Achieving high-quality glycemic control and diabetes management is essential in older adults with cancer requiring systemic oncology treatment, particularly to reduce both short- and long-term complications and the incidence of treatment-related toxicities.
In this context, the present study aimed to: (i) compare real-world T2DM management in older adults with cancer with existing guidelines [ 13 ], (ii) provide an overview of the diabetes-related therapeutic issues (DTI) identified and the recommendations made in our MRs, and (iii) identify factors associated with the presence of at least one DTI.

Objective:
To assess the alignment of pharmacological T2DM management with French guidelines in older adults with cancer undergoing comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA)-guided medication review (MR) prior to systemic cancer treatment.

[SECTION: METHODS]
We conducted a retrospective monocentric observational study including older outpatients aged ≥ 65 years with cancer and T2DM referred for CGA-guided MR at the geriatric oncology day unit of Marseille University Hospital between 2022 and 2023. Diabetes-related therapeutic issues (DTI) were identified, and associated factors were analyzed.

[SECTION: RESULTS]
Among the 189 patients included (median age: 79.2 years (IQR 75.8–83.7, min. 66, max. 93), 91% were frail or dependent, and 89.4% received ≥ 5 medications daily. The mean HbA1c level was 6.89 ± 1.03%, and 64.0% had HbA1c values outside their individualized target. DTI were observed in 67.7% of patients, mainly due to overly strict glycemic control and the use of hypoglycemia-prone agents. Multivariate analysis showed that age ≥ 75 years ( p  < 0.05), HbA1c below target ( p  < 0.001) and the use of ≥ 3 antidiabetics ( p  < 0.05) were significantly associated with DTI.

[SECTION: CONCLUSION]
Through geriatrician-pharmacist collaboration, this study highlights a high prevalence of potential overtreatment in frail older adults with cancer and T2DM, particularly through overly strict glycemic control and the use of hypoglycemia-prone agents. CGA-guided medication reviews conducted by a geriatrician-pharmacist team improve alignment with guidelines and help reduce treatment-related risks before systemic cancer therapy. This is especially important given recent evidence suggesting that poor glycemic control is an independent predictor of worse outcomes in patients with cancer and diabetes. Moreover, active communication between geriatricians and GPs following a CGA may foster more broader implementation of T2DM recommendations in this population.

Study design and population:
This is a monocentric retrospective observational study. It included all outpatients aged ≥ 65 years with cancer and T2DM referred to the geriatric oncology day unit of the University Hospital of Marseille (AP-HM, France), for a CGA-guided MR from January 1, 2022 to December 31, 2023. Francophone guidelines stipulate that oncologists can refer patients to the geriatric oncology day unit prior to starting systemic oncological treatment, only if they have an impaired Geriatric 8 (G8) score (≤ 14 out of 17), or if their G8 score is normal but they have suspected frailties not identified by the G8 scale (e.g., depression or loss of autonomy) [ 22 ]. CGAs and MRs are conducted by, respectively, a geriatrician and a clinical pharmacist. At the end of each CGA, the geriatrician contacts the patient’s general practitioner (GP) and oncologist to communicate the various medication modifications that the unit has already made or believes should be made (i.e., recommendations). All patients included were considered eligible for systemic oncological treatment and underwent a single CGA-guided MR.
As this was a retrospective observational study based on the exhaustive inclusion of all eligible patients referred during the study period, no a priori sample size calculation was performed. The final sample size was therefore determined solely by the number of patients meeting the inclusion criteria between January 2022 and December 2023: diagnosis of cancer requiring systemic oncological treatment; diagnosis of T2DM; and, completion of a CGA-guided MR.
In accordance with French legislation, this retrospective observational study, based exclusively on the reuse of existing clinical or therapeutic data, did not require approval from a Research Ethics Committee and did not meet the definition of a clinical trial. Informed consent from patients was also not required, as the study involved the reuse of care-related data. In compliance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the study was classified as “internal” by the CNIL (French Data Protection Authority), reviewed by the institution’s Data Protection Officer, and approved by the institution’s local Ethics and Scientific Committee (PADS24-120). This study complies with the Declaration of Helsinki.

Data collection:
For the present study, demographic characteristics (age, sex, place of residence), medical information (having at least three comorbidities excluding cancer and diabetes, history of falls or fractures in the previous 6 months, cancer site and stage) and geriatric characteristics (Mini Mental State Examination score (MMSE, < 24 indicates impairment [ 23 ]), Activities of Daily Living score (ADL, < 6 indicates impairment [ 24 ]), Instrumental Activities Daily Living score (IADL, < 4 indicates impairment [ 25 ]) were all obtained from each patient’s CGA report. Nutritional status was also collected from the CGA report and defined using Body Mass Index (BMI). The French Department of Health’s recommendations were used to define malnutrition [ 26 ]. Each CGA indicated the patient’s frailty profile: “robust/good health” (i.e., functionally and cognitively intact, with a good life expectancy), “frail/intermediate health” (i.e., functionally dependent or presenting multiple coexisting chronic comorbidities or cognitive impairment) and “dependent/poor health” (i.e., receiving palliative oncology treatment with end-of-life care).
Biological data were collected from individual blood tests performed on the day of the CGA. Renal function was assessed using the Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance (mL/min) to determine the stage of chronic kidney disease (CKD, stage 1 to stage 5). The actual HbA1c level for each patient was collected, and the individualized target HbA1c range was determined for each patient based on the SFD 2021 guidelines and the patient’s profile according to their CGA (i.e., HbA1c target ≤ 7% for “robust/good health” patients; > 7% and ≤ 8% for “frail/intermediate health” patients; and, > 8% and < 9% for “dependent/poor health” patients) [ 13 ].
Medication data were collected from the individual MR, which was performed on the same day as the CGA, and included the number of medications, the number of antidiabetics, and the glucose-lowering agents prescribed.
All recommendations regarding a patient’s therapeutic management were highlighted in the MR and summarized in the final CGA report. From each patient’s MR, we collected the following data: number of therapeutic-related issues, number of diabetes-related therapeutic issues (DTI) (defined as a deviation from the SFD’s 2021 guidelines [ 13 ]), the type of DTI, the glucose-lowering agent involved, and the type of recommendation proposed by the clinical pharmacist. When HbA1c values were outside the individualized target range, a DTI was identified only when this finding led to a therapeutic recommendation according to SFD guidelines.
Two groups of patients were defined: patients with no DTI (DTI = 0) and patients with at least one DTI (DTI ≥ 1).

Statistical analysis:
Results are presented as numbers and percentages and compared between the two patient groups (i.e., DTI = 0, DTI ≥ 1) using a univariate analysis with Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test according to the conditions of application. A multivariate logistic regression analysis was performed using forward stepwise selection to identify factors associated with having at least one DTI. Variables with a p-value < 0.2 in the univariate analysis and/or those considered clinically and practically relevant were tested in the multivariate model to minimize the risk of prematurely excluding variables that might not appear significant on their own but could be important predictors in multivariate analysis. Variables that were not considered independent determinants or showed a high risk of collinearity with other covariates were excluded from the multivariable analysis. For the multivariable logistic regression model, a complete-case analysis was performed, including only patients with available data for all variables. The results are expressed as odds ratios (OR) and their confidence intervals (CI), with the significance threshold set at 95%. Statistical analyses were performed using R + + version 1.6.15 software (© 2019–2022 Zebrys). The statistical significance threshold was set at 5%.

Among the 737 outpatients aged ≥ 65 years who underwent a CGA-guided MR in our geriatric oncology unit between January 2022 and December 2023, 189 (25.6%) had T2DM and were included in the study.

Diabetes-related therapeutic issues and recommendations:
A total of 1,418 therapeutic-related issues were identified by our unit’s clinical pharmacist, averaging 7.5 ± 2.8 issues per patient (Table  4 ). Of these, 169 were DTI, with 67.7% of patients having at least one. Among the DTI, 44.4% were related to blood test analyses, specifically when the actual HbA1c was either below or above the recommended individualized target range, and 55.6% were identified as drug-related problems. Notably, 63.8% involved inappropriate prescriptions for older adults due to a high risk of hypoglycemia or malnutrition, mainly sulfonylureas (56.7%), glinides (28.3%) and glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) analogues (15.0%). Thirty DTI (31.9%) were associated with inappropriate doses or regimens; including 14 DTI for unadjusted metformin or DPP4i in renal impairment and 16 DTI for inappropriate metformin regimens (e.g., once-daily prescription instead of twice- or three-times-daily regimens). In total, 285 recommendations were made during MRs and communicated to the patients’ health practitioners by the unit’s geriatrician (Table  4 ). Recommendations regarding discontinuation (i.e., complete discontinuation or discontinuation during an antidiabetic treatment switch) principally involved sulfonylureas (29.5%), metformin (22.9%), and glinides (14.3%). Recommendations regarding initiation (i.e., antidiabetic agents newly prescribed or introduced during a switch) mainly concerned DPP4i (44.9%) and metformin (36.7%).

Factors associated with the presence of at least one diabetes-related therapeutic issue (Table 5):
The analysis revealed a significantly higher proportion of patients aged over 75 years in the group with at least one DTI (OR 2.88, 95% CI 1.27–6.53). With regard to the CGAs, cognitive impairment (MMSE < 24) was negatively associated with the presence of a DTI (OR 0.46, 95% CI 0.23–0.92). Similarly, patients with at least three comorbidities were significantly less likely to have a DTI (OR 0.51, 95% CI 0.27–0.99). Of the recorded comorbidities, none were significantly associated with having a DTI, including renal failure. Conversely, patients reporting falls or fractures in the previous 6 months were significantly more likely to have a DTI (OR 2.69, 95% CI 1.17–6.21).
Among the 137 patients whose actual HbA1c level was compared with the recommended individualized target range, a significant association was observed between having at least one DTI and having an HbA1c level below the individualized target (OR 11.13, 95% CI 4.20-29.46). Patients taking at least three antidiabetics were significantly more likely to have a DTI (OR 2.44, 95% CI 1.09–5.44). Regarding glucose-lowering agents, the proportion of patients receiving treatment with metformin, glinides, SGLT2 inhibitors and/or GLP-1 analogues was significantly higher in the DTI group compared with the no-DTI group. Conversely, insulin-treated patients were less likely - although not significantly - to have at least one DTI (OR 0.56, 95% CI 0.30–1.04). All patients treated with sulfonylureas had at least one DTI.
After logistic regression, age older than 75 years ( p-value  < 0.05), an HbA1c level below the individualized target range ( p-value  < 0.001), and the prescription of at least three antidiabetics ( p-value  < 0.05) remained significantly associated with having at least one DTI. Moreover, the presence of at least three comorbidities remained significantly and negatively associated with the presence of a DTI ( p-value  < 0.05).

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
Our study population, composed of older adults with cancer and T2DM, was characterized by significant frailties and geriatric syndromes (91% were defined as frail or dependent). This can be partly explained by the fact that only the most vulnerable older adults with cancer (i.e., those with a G8 score indicating impairment) were referred to our unit for CGA. Frailty and geriatric syndromes – including falls, cognitive impairment, sarcopenia, depression and polypharmacy [ 27 ] – are common in older adults undergoing cancer therapy. Handfort et al. reported a frailty rate of approximately 40–50% in this population [ 28 ]. Frailty is associated with an increased risk of hypoglycemia, particularly in patients with multiple comorbidities (61.9% had ≥ 3 comorbidities in our study). Moreover, co-existing cardiovascular diseases (present in 87.8% of our population) can worsen cognitive decline and increase frailty in diabetic patients [ 29 ]. Consequently, strict glycemic control may result in more harm than benefit in these patients.
Considering the frailty and geriatric syndromes in our population, the mean actual HbA1c was notably lower (6.89% ± 1.03%) than the individualized targets recommended by the SFD’s 2021 guidelines [ 13 ] or most clinical practice guidelines [ 30 ], and also lower than those reported in two other studies (HbA1c 7.56%): one involving a heterogeneous older diabetic population (GERODIAB [ 31 ]) and the other focusing on older adults with T2DM and cancer (ONCODIAB [ 32 ]). In our study, 64.0% of patients had HbA1c levels outside their individually target range, mostly (51.4%) below it. It is important to highlight that HbA1c may not be a reliable marker of glycemic control in several conditions frequently observed in older adults with cancer, including anemia, chronic kidney disease, recent blood loss or transfusion, iron or vitamin deficiencies, and anemia treatments, all of which influence hemoglobin level. Moreover, poor nutritional status – identified in 63.5% of patients – can also affect HbA1c levels. In such circumstances, glucose monitoring would have been a relevant and recommended complementary approach to guide therapeutic adjustments [ 33 ,  34 ], especially since using a higher HbA1c target al.one is often insufficient to reduce the risk of hypoglycemia [ 35 ]. However, in our study, only fasting blood glucose values were available, without confirmation of fasting status. Therefore, HbA1c remained the only parameter available to assess glycemic control and individualize therapeutic goals. Thus, our findings suggest a tendency for practitioners to select overly stringent HbA1c targets, consistent with a recent US survey showing that physicians often selected lower than recommended HbA1c targets for patients with intermediate and poor health [ 36 ]. In our study, patients with individualized HbA1c target less than 7% typically had localized cancer, preserved functional and cognitive status, and a favorable life expectancy.
It is also important to note that most patients (82.6%) had newly diagnosed cancer and had not yet initiated first-line systemic anticancer therapy at the time of CGA. This likely limited the potential confounding treatment-related effects on glycemic control (11.1% had received steroids in the previous 3 months, mainly for cancer-related management, and only one patient had an HbA1c level above his individualized target). Several systemic anticancer therapies (e.g., cisplatin, 5-fluorouracil, PI3K or mTOR inhibitors and immune checkpoint inhibitors), as well as corticosteroid use, are known to influence glucose metabolism with a high incidence of hyperglycemia [ 37 ]. A meta-analysis including 12 studies (9 872 T2DM patients with various cancer) showed that hyperglycemia was associated with worse overall survival (HR 2.05, 95% CI 1.67–2.51) and disease-free survival (HR 1.98 95% CI 1.20–3.27) [ 38 ]. Therefore, these interactions highlight the importance of ongoing individualized diabetes management throughout cancer care. Future studies should further investigate the impact of different lines of systemic anticancer therapy on glycemic control in this population.
Polypharmacy, identified in 89.4% of patients (36.0% receiving ≥ 10 medications), increases the risk of poor glycemic control and hypoglycemia. This is considerably higher than the pooled prevalence of 50% reported in a meta-analysis by Remelli et al. [ 39 ]. One-quarter of patients received three or more antidiabetics, whereas the SFD recommends monotherapy or dual therapy with metformin +/- a DPP4i, and insulin therapy in cases of persistent hyperglycemia. The three glucose-lowering agents most frequently observed (metformin, DPP4i, insulin) reflect SFD guidelines. More than half of patients were treated with metformin, which is unanimously recommended as first-line therapy. Sulfonylureas and glinides accounted for 18.5% and 11.1% of prescriptions, respectively. This aligns with prescribing patterns reported by Doucet et al. in 2014–2015 [ 40 ] and suggests inertia in prescribing practices over the last decade, particularly since these medications have not been included in diabetes management guidelines for years, and should be avoided in older adults due to their high risk of hypoglycemia [ 41 ].
To identify situations in which de-intensification of antihyperglycemic treatment may be appropriate prior to starting systemic oncology treatment, we examined the factors associated with having a DTI. Age over 75 years, HbA1c levels below the individualized target, and the prescription of three or more antidiabetics were all significantly associated with DTI. Interestingly, having three or more comorbidities was significantly associated with lower odds of having a DTI. This may seem counterintuitive, as a higher comorbidity burden could be expected to complicate diabetes management. However, Struijs et al. showed that a higher comorbidity burden is associated with increased healthcare utilization and more frequent medical consultations among patients with diabetes [ 42 ]. In our view, one possible explanation is that patients with multiple comorbidities may receive closer medical monitoring, sometimes involving several specialists, which could facilitate treatment adjustments when needed. Nonetheless, this interpretation should be considered cautiously. Similarly, in the univariate analysis, cognitive impairment was also significantly associated with lower odds of having a DTI. Patients with cognitive alterations are less likely to participate in diabetes self-care and are at higher risk of severe hypoglycemia due to difficulties recognizing or reporting symptoms [ 43 ], which may lead prescribers to simplify treatment regimens.
Most DTI were linked to age-related factors (risk of hypoglycemia, malnutrition, etc.) and mainly involved stopping or switching insulin secretagogues (sulfonylureas or glinides) and/or GLP-1 analogues in favor of metformin and/or DPP4i (after evaluating renal function and weight loss). In the univariate analysis, falls or fractures within the previous 6 months were associated with DTI, particularly among those treated with sulfonylureas, glinides, or insulin (66.7% of patients). These glucose-lowering agents, especially when combined with polypharmacy [ 44 ], increase the risk of falls due to recurrent hypoglycemia, which often goes unrecognized in older adults [ 45 ]. Recommendations for discontinuing sulfonylureas were consistent with ONCODIAB findings [ 32 ], but those for glinides, metformin and DPP4i were twice as frequent. More specifically, discontinuation of once-daily metformin (≤ 1000 mg prescribed 1x/day) and DPP4i was associated with actual HbA1c level below the individualized target range, explaining their significant association with DTI in the univariate analysis. Regarding GLP-1 analogues, although they may be beneficial for T2DM patients with overweight or cardiovascular risk, the risk of inducing or worsening malnutrition or sarcopenia is a significant concern, as studies indicate that up to 50% of weight loss may involve lean mass reduction [ 46 ]. In our view, this risk is too high to justify their use in older adults with cancer who are malnourished or at risk of malnutrition (63.5% of patients in our study), particularly in malignancies associated with high rates of cachexia or anorexia (e.g., hepatopancreatobiliary and gastric cancers).
According to the “4S Pathway” [ 15 ], CGA-guided MRs help determine the most appropriate strategies to realign the diabetes regimen in older adults before initiating oncology treatment, using a patient-centred approach. This includes reducing treatment burden (for diabetes, but also concomitant comorbidities), lowering the risk of hypoglycemia, and improving overall quality of life. Collaboration between a geriatrician and a pharmacist offers the opportunity to recommend therapeutic classes with safer profiles for older adults with cancer. The present study has limitations. First, regarding diabetes-related factors, data on the number of hypoglycemic episodes and the duration of T2DM were not available, which may have influenced HbA1c interpretation or treatment adjustments. Second, 28.0% of patients had no recent HbA1c measurement, complicating personalized T2DM management and representing a potential source of bias for the regression model. This may be due to the practice of not systematically measuring HbA1c during CGA, especially when recent blood tests have been performed, but without any HbA1c measurement. Moreover, HbA1c variability can be multifactorial (conditions affecting hemoglobin level or malnutrition). Third, the prescription data covered only the previous 3 months, with most sourced from GPs, consistent with the French DIABASIS survey (93% of patients followed by their GP for diabetes) [ 47 ]. However, it was not possible to determine whether GPs were the only prescribers or simply renewing diabetologist prescriptions. Finally, as a retrospective single-center study, our findings may be affected by several biases: selection bias (our study population consisted of highly frail older adults, limiting generalizability to all older adults with cancer), information bias (missing HbA1c data reduced the sample size and may have limited statistical power and accuracy of the regression model), and confounding due to patient heterogeneity. Consequently, the reproducibility and generalizability of these results should be further investigated.
