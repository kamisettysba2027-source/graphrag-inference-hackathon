[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
The relationship between cannabinoids and mental health has become a major focus of scientific inquiry and public discourse. Cannabinoids are diverse chemical compounds from the  Cannabis  species that have been explored for their therapeutic applications in treating chronic pain, psychiatric and neurological conditions, such as depression, schizophrenia, epilepsy and anxiety. Additionally, cannabinoids are frequently used for recreational purposes. The evolving legislation surrounding cannabis has added a layer of complexity to the understanding of its pharmacological effects. Societal attitudes shifting towards a more permissive acceptance of cannabis use make it essential to balance individual freedom with public health concerns. While the use of cannabinoids remains a subject of ongoing research, clinical data have shown promise in the treatment of mental health disorders. However, debates and concerns have been raised regarding the potential pharmacokinetic (PK) drug–drug interactions that may occur, involving changes in the absorption, distribution, metabolism and excretion (ADME) of the prescribed drugs. The future prospect of patients having unrestricted access to cannabis requires special attention in case of concurrent use with the common medical treatments they may be receiving for a cardiovascular or metabolic disease or with medications related to the treatment of psychiatric disorders. This comprehensive review aims to critically discuss the main PK interactions of major cannabinoids (tetrahydrocannabinol, cannabidiol, cannabinol, cannabichromene and cannabigerol) in relation to the drugs prescribed in neuropathic pain, depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, epilepsy and cancer, as well as with the most common cardiometabolic disease treatments such as statins, beta‐blockers, warfarin, insulin analogues and metformin.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
In recent years, the intersection of cannabinoids and mental health has become a topic of great interest and debate. Cannabinoids, as a diverse group of chemical compounds found in the cannabis species, have been used for their therapeutic potential in variety of conditions, including chronic cancer and non‐cancer pain, for the management of depression, schizophrenia, epilepsy, and/or anxiety, as well as for recreational purposes [ 1 ]. This category of natural products includes over 100 distinct compounds, with delta‐9‐tetrahydrocannabinol (THC), cannabidiol (CBD), cannabinol (CBN), cannabichromene (CBC) and cannabigerol (CBG) being the most widely studied.
Τhe primary psychoactive compound in cannabis is THC, which is known for its dose‐dependent effects, with higher doses inducing psychotic or affective symptoms, while lower doses have anxiolytic properties [ 2 ]. In addition to its psychoactivity, THC exhibits analgesic and antiemetic properties, making it useful for pain and nausea management [ 3 ]. CBD, the second most abundant cannabinoid and non‐psychoactive, has garnered attention for its effectiveness in the management of Dravet syndrome (DS) and Lennox–Gastaut syndrome (LGS) [ 4 ,  5 ], as well as for its antioxidant and anti‐inflammatory effects and ability to alleviate psychotic, anxiety and withdrawal symptoms [ 6 ,  7 ]. In addition, CBD has demonstrated potential for modulating immune responses [ 8 ]. CBN is a mildly psychoactive compound formed as THC degrades in older cannabis material or upon heat exposure [ 9 ]. It has been associated with vasorelaxant effects despite its weaker interaction with cannabinoid receptors [ 10 ,  11 ,  12 ]. Moreover, it has been explored for its sedative effects and potential use in sleep disorders, however without strong clinical evidence [ 10 ,  12 ]. In contrast, psychoactive effects have not been reported for CBC which primarily exhibits anti‐inflammatory, antimicrobial and analgesic properties, with additional promise in dermatological conditions, such as acne [ 13 ]. CBG, often referred to as the ‘mother’ cannabinoid, is the biosynthetic precursor from which other major cannabinoids, including THC and CBD, are synthesized. Despite its non‐psychoactive nature, it has been identified as a potential neuroprotectant and in treating conditions such as glaucoma and inflammatory bowel disease [ 14 ].
The changing legal landscape surrounding cannabis has introduced a degree of complexity to the dissemination of its therapeutic effects. Differences in national regulations often influence research accessibility, clinical trial design and public perception, making it challenging to establish consistent scientific and medical evidence. Recently, the Danish Medicines Agency (Lægemiddelstyrelsen) announced a significant development in the regulation of cannabis species quality properties within Europe. The European Pharmacopoeia Commission (EPC) has adopted the new Monograph 3028 (supplement 11.5) [ 15 ], establishing quality standards for cannabis flower as a pharmaceutical raw material. The Danish Medicines Agency has since formally adopted this monograph, replacing its national cannabis monograph to ensure harmonization in production, safety and standardization. As societal attitudes shift towards greater acceptance of cannabis use, it becomes essential to balance individual freedom and public health considerations. A national survey conducted between 2017 and 2021 revealed a concerning trend: daily cannabis consumption among U.S. adults increased, driven in part by the perception that cannabis is safer than tobacco. Over the study course, there was a significant shift towards a more favourable perception of cannabis safety, as more participants believed that cannabis was ‘somewhat safer’ or ‘much safer’ than tobacco in 2021, compared to 2017. Moreover, an increasing number of individuals endorsed the opinion that second‐hand smoking from cannabis is safer than tobacco, even in the case of children and pregnant women [ 16 ]. However, uncontrolled or frequent cannabis use may also worsen underlying psychiatric conditions and has been associated with an increased risk of symptom exacerbation, particularly in individuals with psychotic or mood disorders [ 17 ].
The perceptions described earlier raise concerns regarding public health and informed decision‐making. The potential risks are associated with the extensive cannabis consumption, especially in combination with central nervous system (CNS)‐affecting drugs or common medical treatments (statins, insulin, metformin, paracetamol, etc.) [ 18 ,  19 ,  20 ]. For instance, both THC and CBD, as well as CBN to a lesser extent, have been shown to interact with cytochrome P450 enzymes which are responsible for the metabolism of numerous drugs. Their activity is mostly inhibitory and is expressed in isoforms, including CYP3A4, CYP2D6 and CYP1A2. Furthermore, in terms of pharmacodynamics (PDs), there is evidence of synergy with drugs such as oxycodone [ 21 ] and clobazam [ 22 ], while lower efficacy has been observed with prescription medicines, as in the case of risperidone [ 23 ].
While further research is required to fully assess their efficacy, clinical data have demonstrated encouraging results in managing conditions and symptoms related to chronic pain, depression anxiety, schizophrenia and cancer pain. Nevertheless, debates and concerns have been raised regarding the potential for drug–drug interactions (DDIs) that may occur though pharmacokinetic (PK) and PD mechanisms, involving changes in ADME characteristics of the prescribed drugs, as well as synergistic or antagonistic effects on drug targets. The future prospect of patients having unrestricted access to cannabis needs special attention in case of concurrent use with the common medical treatments they may be receiving for a cardiovascular or metabolic disease (e.g. hypertension, stable angina pectoris, diabetes, etc), or with medications related to the treatment of psychiatric disorders. Focussing on the five most pharmacologically and clinically relevant cannabinoids (THC, CBD, CBN, CBC and CBG), this comprehensive review aims to critically evaluate their PK interactions with commonly prescribed treatments in neuropathic pain, psychiatric disorders, epilepsy, cancer and cardiometabolic diseases. Other naturally occurring cannabinoids, such as cannabielsoin (CBE), cannabicyclol (CBL) and cannabitriol (CBT), were not included in this review because of the limited evidence available regarding their PKs and potential for drug–drug interactions, despite sharing the common terpenophenolic structure characteristic of phytocannabinoids [ 24 ].

METHODS–LITERATURE SEARCH:
In order to establish a comprehensive understanding of the interactions between major cannabinoids (THC, CBD and CBN) and medications used in the treatment of various of mental disorders, extensive research was conducted in prominent electronic databases, including PubMed, Scopus and Google Scholar. This search encompassed English‐language publications of the last 5 years, from 2018 to 2024. The systematic investigation was primarily focussed on the examination of clinical data concerning the co‐administration of major cannabinoids with medications commonly prescribed for a wide range of conditions, including neuropathic pain, depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, cancer, epilepsy and cardiovascular diseases. To ensure the inclusion of the most relevant studies, a set of specific search terms was employed, including: ‘cannabinoids’, ‘tetrahydrocannabinol’, ‘cannabidiol’ and ‘cannabinol’, in conjunction with ‘interactions with neuropathic pain’, ‘depression’, ‘anxiety’, ‘schizophrenia’, ‘cancer’, ‘epilepsy’, ‘cardiovascular diseases’ and ‘diabetes medications’. The authors conducted a comprehensive literature search excluding the studies for which the full text was unavailable, and publication language was other than English.

Oral ingestion:
THC is a highly lipophilic molecule, characterized by extensive distribution in fat tissue. It exhibits low oral bioavailability that ranges from 4% to 12% [ 25 ], with the maximum blood concentration being reported 0.66 to 4 h after administration, depending on the oral formulation characteristics. For instance, cannabis oil has been found to increase the bioavailability of THC and its metabolites, while cannabis decoctions (cannabis tea) have been found to enhance the bioavailability of cannabidiolic acid [ 26 ]. The distribution of THC in high‐vascularised organs, such as brain, heart, lungs and liver, occurs promptly after administration, and the substance is rapidly transferred to adipose tissue. Fat tissue demonstrates a role of a THC depot, from which the substance is slowly released back into the bloodstream [ 25 ]. The reported volume of distribution (Vd) is found to be high, equal to 10 L/kg [ 27 ], while a range of 4 to 14 L/kg is also considered to be observed in the population [ 28 ]. Moreover, a lower Vd value is estimated following inhalation, around 3.4 L/kg [ 27 ]. THC undergoes extensive hepatic first‐pass metabolism by the CYP2C and CYP3A enzymes, being transformed into the active and psychoactive metabolite 11‐OH‐THC. The latter is further oxidized into 11‐COOH‐THC, which does not exert psychoactive effects [ 29 ].
THC is primarily eliminated through the faeces (65%) and urine (20%), in the form of hydroxylated and carboxylic metabolites. Because of its high accumulation in fat, cannabis has a prolonged terminal half‐life of 22 h [ 28 ]. Approximately 80–90% of cannabis is excreted within 5 days primarily as hydroxylated and carboxylated metabolites, with over 65% eliminated through faeces and around 20% through urine [ 30 ]. The THC half‐life can be altered by the frequency of intake, the amount of the dose, and the lipid depots of the individual. Specifically, in frequent users, a longer half‐life has been reported, with concentrations higher than 1 μg/L being detectable in the bloodstream for over 24 h, following the last intake [ 28 ,  29 ].

Inhalation:
THC plasma concentrations reported after ingestion are substantially lower than those achieved after inhalation. In general, cannabinoids inhalation results in rapid and extended systemic exposure presenting a PK profile equivalent to intravenous administration. THC maximum plasma concentration levels are detected within 3 to 10 min after a single inhalation, while the bioavailability of THC is characterized by high interindividual variability ranging from 2% to 56% [ 29 ], or 10% to 35% [ 25 ]. This substantial variability is attributed to both inter‐ and intra‐individual differences in inhalation practices, the type of device used, the size of the inhaled particles and the specific site of deposition within the respiratory tract [ 28 ]. The mean reported value for inhalation bioavailability percentage is estimated to be 25%, which is substantially higher than the oral one. Despite the medical recommendations, smoking remains the predominant route of cannabis intake, mainly for recreational purposes. In this case, the smoking frequency affects the area under the THC plasma concentration  vs . time curve (area under the curve, AUC) profile, with lower levels observed in occasional smokers, as anticipated [ 29 ]. It is noteworthy that among frequent smokers, the peak levels of THC and CBG in oral fluids following any inhalation (smoking or vaporization) administration were higher than those observed following oral cannabis administration [ 31 ].
The inhalation route allows for the avoidance of extensive hepatic metabolism [ 32 ], as well as lower exposure to oral cavity enzymes [ 31 ]. First pass metabolism is also circumvented by transdermal administration, however with dubious results in terms of skin permeation, because of the highly lipophilic nature of the compound. It has been reported that THC hydroxylation also occurs in the brain, where 11‐OH‐THC expresses significant psychoactive activity [ 29 ]. Metabolite accumulation levels in the brain following either oral administration or vaporization are estimated to be similar, at approximately 200 ng/g [ 33 ]. In the liver, THC has been demonstrated to reversibly inhibit several isoforms of CYP enzymes ( CYP1A2 ,  CYP2B6 ,  CYP2C9 , CYP2C19, and  CYP2D6 ) raising concerns about cannabinoid consumption under the treatment condition of drugs, such as bupropion, cyclophosphamide, diclofenac, warfarin, phenytoin, propofol, ketamine and selegiline [ 34 ]. Therefore, the two main advantages of inhaled cannabis are the quick achievement of high levels in brain and the reduced potency for drug–drug interactions via the metabolic pathway.

CBD:
CBD can be administered using the same methods as THC, and as expected, its bioavailability varies significantly depending on the route of administration. Specifically, inhalation via smoking has been associated with a mean bioavailability of 31%, ranging from 11% to 45% [ 25 ]. In contrast, oral administration has been demonstrated to yield a significantly low bioavailability of 6% [ 29 ]. Additionally, the low solubility of the substance can result in a variable PK profile when administered in capsules, whereas oral or sublingual oil products provide more consistency [ 25 ].
The systematic review conducted by Millar et al. [ 35 ], summarizing 24 studies on CBD PK parameters, revealed that both AUC and  C 
 max  exhibit dose‐dependent increases. However, at higher doses (400–800 mg), a saturation effect is observed. Furthermore, in the case of oral ingestion of CBD, the presence of food significantly affects the PK parameters, with higher AUC and  C 
 max  reported under fed conditions [ 36 ]. CBD shows minimal evidence of accumulation in plasma, while clinical data on its accumulation in adipose tissue are limited. Nevertheless, the high lipophilicity of the compound may provide an explanation for the findings of Batinic et al. [ 37 ], who suggest that a lower percentage of fat tissue results in lower CBD accumulation and, consequently, higher CBD levels in the bloodstream. Maximum concentration levels are typically observed 1 to 4 h after oral administration, and the half‐life varies from 1 h to 5 days depending on the dose and route of administration [ 37 ]. Within this range, the mean half‐life is estimated to be 18 to 32 h [ 25 ]. The estimated volume of distribution is significantly high, equal to 32 L/kg [ 38 ].
As anticipated, achieving higher CBD levels is faster through the inhalation route. The landmark development in CBD's medical application came with the approval of Epidiolex® by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2018. Epidiolex® (Jazz Pharmaceuticals) is an oral solution containing purified CBD, specifically indicated for the treatment of seizures associated with two rare and severe forms of epilepsy: DS and LGS. Both conditions are typically manifested during childhood and are characterized by frequent, severe seizures that are often resistant to conventional anti‐epileptic medications. A recent study was conducted among patients diagnosed with Dravet, as well as LGS and tuberous sclerosis complex, in which this oral formulation of CBD was compared with a dry powder inhaler (DPI). The CBD DPI has been developed using the excipient and the inhalation device of an FDA‐approved inhaled insulin product. The study revealed that a 23‐fold lower dose administered via inhalation resulted in threefold higher CBD levels in plasma, compared to the oral route administration, achieved in 3.8 ± 1.3 min [ 4 ].
Similar to THC, CBD undergoes extensive first‐pass hepatic metabolism via the CYP2C19 and CYP3A4 to yield the active 7‐hydroxycannabidiol (7‐OH‐CBD) and the inactive 7‐carboxy‐cannabidiol (7‐COOH‐CBD) metabolites [ 34 ]. The hydroxylated metabolite is further metabolized and eliminated though faecal excretion and, to a lesser extent, urinary excretion [ 29 ]. The inhalation route increases CBD bioavailability bypassing this metabolic route, as demonstrated by the concentration‐time profile for 7‐COOH‐CBD following oral CBD and DPI CBD administration [ 4 ]. CBD temporarily accumulates in the liver during the metabolic process. High CBD levels in this tissue can inhibit the metabolism of other drugs (e.g. citalopram and clopidogrel), altering their clearance profile [ 39 ,  40 ]. Additionally, CBD has been found to inhibit CYP450 enzymes, specifically CYP1A2, CYP3A4, CYP2B6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19 and CYP2D6 [ 34 ].

CBN:
CBN holds the distinction of being the first cannabinoid to be discovered and isolated from cannabis. It is primarily formed as a non‐enzymatic oxidation byproduct of THC and is typically encountered as an artefact of extended storage, particularly under elevated temperatures [ 41 ]. In a recent study, Maioli et al. [ 42 ] characterized CBN as the most significant ‘minor’ cannabinoid. The term ‘minor cannabinoids’ is used to describe more than 150 compounds of cannabis plants with significant structural differences [ 42 ]. CBN has often been overshadowed by THC and CBD analogues, which exhibit more pronounced and promising biological properties. It possesses approximately 10% of the psychoactive potency of THC and undergoes first‐pass metabolism when administered orally, through hydroxylation, resulting in its primary metabolite, 11‐OH‐CBN [ 11 ]. This metabolite exhibits a higher potency for  CB1  receptor, almost equivalent to that of THC, but it is a weak antagonist of  CB2  receptor [ 43 ,  44 ]. Notably, the structure of CBN, which comprises with an additional aromatic ring, results in a comparatively slower and less extensive metabolic process compared to THC. The bioavailability of the compound after administration through smoking is estimated to be around 41%. However, this value can vary significantly, ranging from as low as 8% to as high as 77%. This variability can be attributed to factors, such as the individual metabolic profile of each patient and the specific method of administration [ 11 ]. Extensive preclinical or clinical investigations into CBN are not available, especially in terms of human PK or metabolism data. Nevertheless, the existing published findings support the potential of CBN in the treatment of several conditions, attributing it to anti‐inflammatory, analgesic, antibacterial, orexigenic and anxiolytic properties [ 42 ].

CBC:
CBC is a highly lipophilic molecule, characterized by low oral bioavailability. Although specific oral bioavailability studies for CBC are limited, it ranges, similarly to other cannabinoids, between 6% and 12%, depending on the formulation and food intake [ 45 ]. The compound undergoes first‐pass hepatic metabolism, with CYP450 enzymes such as CYP2C9 and CYP3A4 demonstrating key roles in its biotransformation [ 34 ]. Following oral administration, CBC is rapidly distributed to highly vascularized tissues, including the brain, liver, and lungs, and eventually sequestered in fat tissues because of its lipophilic nature. The Vd of CBC has not been extensively studied, but it is presumed to be high, similarly to other cannabinoids. CBC metabolites are primarily excreted in faeces, with minimal urinary excretion [ 29 ]. The terminal half‐life estimates for CBC vary depending on the route of administration and individual metabolic profiles, with limited evidence suggesting a range between 10 and 24 h [ 45 ]. The inhalation route of CBC administration provides faster systemic absorption and bypasses first‐pass metabolism, achieving peak plasma concentrations within minutes. While specific bioavailability data for inhaled CBC are scarce, inhalation is expected to provide a significantly higher bioavailability than oral routes, with estimates ranging from 25% to 45%, influenced by factors such as inhalation technique and particle size distribution [ 41 ]. CBC inhalation may also lead to faster therapeutic onset, suggesting it a potential candidate for conditions requiring rapid relief.

CBG:
CBG is another non‐psychoactive cannabinoid that is characterized by low water solubility and limited oral bioavailability, estimated to range from 6% to 10% depending on the formulation [ 25 ]. Similar to other cannabinoids, CBG undergoes extensive first‐pass hepatic metabolism via the CYP450 enzyme system, primarily CYP2C9 and CYP3A4, resulting in hydroxylated and carboxylated metabolites [ 34 ]. Maximum plasma concentrations are typically observed 1–4 h post‐ingestion, with food intake significantly enhancing absorption because of its lipophilicity [ 36 ]. CBG is distributed extensively in the body, concentrating in fatty tissues and high‐vascularized organs such as the liver and lungs. Its Vd is estimated to be substantial, though specific values have not been explicitly determined. Excretion occurs predominantly via faeces, with minor urinary elimination, and its half‐life ranges from 18 to 24 h depending on the dose and frequency of administration [ 29 ]. As anticipated, the inhalation route for CBG provides rapid absorption avoiding first‐pass metabolism, leading to higher bioavailability compared to oral administration. Peak plasma concentrations are typically reached within 5 to 10 min after inhalation, with bioavailability estimates ranging between 25% and 35%, depending on inhalation technique and delivery method [ 25 ]. The potential benefits of inhaled CBG are particularly promising for conditions requiring fast‐acting relief, such as acute pain or inflammation. Preclinical studies suggest that CBG has potential therapeutic effects, including anti‐inflammatory, neuroprotective and antibacterial properties [ 42 ].

In vitro DDIs on transporters and enzymes:
Cannabinoids have attracted increasing interest because of their potential to induce DDIs through their effects on key drug‐metabolizing enzymes and transporters. CYP450 enzymes are responsible for the metabolism of approximately 75% of clinically relevant medications, being also susceptible to inhibition or induction by various substances. Similarly, ABC transporters, including ABCB1 (P‐glycoprotein) and ABCG2 (breast cancer resistance protein), are essential for the cellular efflux of drugs and xenobiotics. These transporters are predominantly expressed in tissues that form protective barriers, such as the liver, intestines, kidneys and brain, where they regulate drug absorption, distribution and excretion. Their modulation by substrates leads to alterations in drug transport dynamics and potential DDIs. Recent in vitro studies screening the interactions of major cannabinoids with these enzymes and transporters are summarized in Table  1 .
Almost all cannabinoids significantly inhibited CYP2C9, which is essential for metabolizing drugs with a narrow therapeutic index, such as warfarin and phenytoin. Additionally, most cannabinoids were found to inhibit CYP2C19 activity, with CBD demonstrating the highest potency. Minimal effects on CYP3A4, CYP2D6 and CYP2B6 were also noted [ 47 ]. Furthermore, CBD inhibited the metabolism of midazolam, bupropion, dextromethorphan and tramadol [ 34 ,  51 ], while CBN inhibited CYP2E1, as well as other isoforms (e.g. CYP2B6, CYP2C9 and CYP1A2) [ 34 ,  47 ] (Figure  1 ). The clinical relevance of these interactions is notable, particularly for drugs that rely on these pathways for bioactivation or clearance.
The interactions of cannabinoids with ATP‐binding cassette (ABC) transporters can result in significant DDIs, potentially affecting the PKs and PDs of competing drugs. In this context, the potential interactions of CBC with prazosin and digoxin substrates of ABCB1 and ABCG2 transporters were investigated [ 50 ], while the recent work of Anderson et al. [ 54 ] screened three commercial cannabis products (both recreational and medicinal) containing THC and CBD. Combinations of THC and CBD inhibited both ABCG2 and ABCB11, whereas THC alone demonstrated negligible [ 48 ] to modest [ 54 ] effect on ABCG2. In contrast, cannabinoids such as CBC and THC exhibited negligible interaction with ABCB1 and ABCG2 in specific models. The study by Bansal et al. [ 49 ] investigated the PK interactions of CBD and THC with CYP450 enzymes using human liver microsomes as the model system. The findings revealed that CBD and its metabolite, 7‐hydroxy CBD, are potent inhibitors of multiple P450 enzymes (CYP2C19, CYP2C9 and CYP3A), indicating a significant potential for drug interactions at clinically relevant doses. In contrast, THC exhibited no notable effect on CYP enzyme activity. In addition to these findings, four major cannabinoids—THC, CBD, CBG and CBN—have been shown to inhibit dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP‐IV), a key enzyme in glucose regulation, although with lower potency than standard antidiabetic drugs [ 53 ]. Overall, these findings underscore the broad inhibitory potential of cannabinoids, particularly CBD, and support the need for cautious consideration in polypharmacy contexts.

Preclinical studies on cannabinoids DDIs:
Preclinical studies reported in literature on DDIs of cannabinoids are mostly focussed on the major compounds THC and CBD (Table  2 ). To date, only one study has investigated the interactions between CBD and CBN, or CBN and CBC in muscle pain disorders [ 60 ]. This study demonstrated a dose‐dependent efficacy for both CBD and CBN, with their combinations providing prolonged therapeutic effects compared to individual cannabinoids. Notably, these analgesic benefits were achieved without impairing motor function, thereby underscoring their potential for localized pain management. Significant attention has also been given to the interactions between THC and CBD when co‐administered in vivo [ 55 ,  57 ]. In rats pretreated with CBD, delayed THC metabolism was observed, leading to elevated plasma and brain THC levels. This effect has been shown to exacerbate THC‐induced anxiety and social withdrawal [ 55 ]. Conversely, CBD has also been found to suppress THC‐induced brain activation when the two cannabinoids were administered together, highlighting the complexity of their interactions.
The psychotropic effects of THC are critical when evaluating its role in neurological disorders such as epilepsy, schizophrenia or depression. For instance, repeated THC exposure was shown to modulate the efficacy of risperidone by up‐regulating P‐glycoprotein (P‐gp) at the blood–brain barrier, reducing brain concentrations of risperidone and its active metabolite. This effect was not observed with clozapine, a non‐P‐gp substrate, indicating the selective nature of THC's influence on drug transport mechanisms [ 23 ]. Additionally, a study investigating the interaction between THC and gabapentin demonstrated synergistic analgesic effects, although both THC and combination elicited side effects such as motor incoordination and sedation [ 58 ].
Special caution should be exercised with acetaminophen, because of its widespread use and perception as harmless. Its toxic metabolite (NAPQI) poses a risk when combined with CBD, which shares metabolic pathways via glucuronidation enzymes [ 64 ]. A study in mice co‐administered high doses of CBD and acetaminophen observed significant liver injury and mortality, suggesting a potentially harmful interaction under certain conditions [ 59 ]. However, it is noteworthy that the acetaminophen dosage used in this study exceeds the conventional maximum of 300 mg/kg administered to mice, which raises questions about whether the findings may have been influenced by the high‐dosage selection.
CBD's high lipophilicity contributes to its preferential accumulation in brain tissue, a characteristic linked to its anticonvulsant effects in seizure rat models [ 62 ]. When formulated with absorption enhancers such as Piperine–SNEDDS, CBD exhibits improved bioavailability and extended half‐life. However, this enhancement was less pronounced under chronic dosing conditions [ 61 ]. Other studies indicated no significant interaction between CBD and phenobarbital, suggesting this combination may not require dose adjustment [ 63 ]. In contrast, the co‐administration of CBD and clobazam resulted in significant PK and PD interactions, with elevated clobazam and its active metabolite levels and potentiated anticonvulsant effects via enhanced GABA receptor activation [ 46 ].

Clinical studies and observations on cannabinoids DDIs:
Evidence‐based clinical research on cannabinoids PKs is considered limited. The study by Bansal et al. [ 49 ] explored the PK interactions between cannabinoids and CYP 450 enzymes in healthy adults as a continuation of their in vitro study [ 49 ]. CBD‐dominant and THC‐dominant cannabis extracts were administered in the form of brownies. The CBD administered dose was 640 mg, while the THC dose was 20 mg. The study revealed that CBD and THC together inhibited several CYP enzymes in the order of CYP2C19 > CYP2C9 > CYP3A > CYP1A2, with no significant effect on CYP2D6. Conversely, THC‐dominant brownies alone did not exhibit any inhibition of CYP enzymes. Specifically, CBD‐rich brownies increased the AUC of probe drugs metabolized by CYP enzymes, including a 207% increase for CYP2C19 (omeprazole), 77% for CYP2C9 (losartan), 56% for CYP3A (midazolam) and 39% for CYP1A2 (caffeine). The study attributed these interactions to CBD's inhibition of first‐pass metabolism in the liver and intestines, particularly for drugs, that are highly dependent on CYP metabolism. Additionally, CBD was found to inhibit CYP2C9‐mediated THC clearance, amplifying THC's AUC by 161% and intensifying its pharmacological effects.
Based on the above study, it becomes evident that the off‐label use of cannabis, whether for medical purposes or recreational purposes, carries significant risks in terms of potential PK interactions. These interactions can impact the effectiveness and safety of a patient's existing medication regimen and the action of commonly used non‐prescription drugs, such as paracetamol. The following sections will discuss the results of recent clinical studies investigating the DDIs of cannabinoids with CNS drugs and commonly prescribed cardiometabolic disease treatments.

Neuropathic pain:
Neuropathic or chronic pain constitutes a complex and challenging condition related to the self‐damage of the nervous system. Neurological dysfunction is characterized by spontaneous pain often accompanied by sensations such as shooting, burning, tingling or electric‐shock pain. The most common therapeutic classes used in its management include antiepileptics, tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), serotonin–norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) and opioids. Inhaled cannabis, in both smoked and vaporized forms, has consistently been found to reduce chronic non‐cancer pain. Several recent reviews have summarized the potential of cannabinoids in pain management [ 64 ,  65 ,  66 ]. However, the existing clinical evidence from controlled trials and meta‐analyses is not yet considered sufficient for including cannabis in standard therapeutic strategies. Recent concerns have also emerged over cannabis use disorder (CUD), with a high prevalence (29.9% of the patients) reported in some pain clinics [ 67 ].
Among opioid treatments commonly used for non‐cancer pain, cannabinoids share metabolic pathways with agents like oxycodone, methadone, fentanyl and tramadol, while approximately 50% of adults receiving opioid treatment for chronic pain also use cannabis regularly [ 68 ,  69 ]. CBD has been found to inhibit CYP3A4, potentially increasing plasma levels of co‐administered opioids and heightening the risk of adverse effects such as dizziness and nausea [ 64 ]. For tramadol, which is metabolized by CYP2D6, co‐administration with cannabinoids (THC, CBD and CBN) may reduce analgesic efficacy [ 70 ]. Interactions between CBD and immunosuppressants, such as cyclosporine A and mycophenolate mofetil, have also been reported, involving both CYP3A4 and carboxylesterase enzymes [ 71 ]. It is noteworthy that no improvement in chronic neuropathic pain was observed during CBD treatment. However, in terms of CBD PKs, oral administration with high‐fat meals reduced variability in CBD concentrations compared to fasting or sublingual administration [ 72 ].
Cannabinoids may also enhance opioid analgesia. In a human trial, the combination of low‐dose oxycodone and cannabis with 5.6% THC led to increased pain tolerance without enhancing abuse potential [ 21 ]. A case report involving hydrocodone–acetaminophen and inhaled cannabis noted higher hydrocodone levels and improved pain and anxiety scores during co‐administration [ 73 ]. However, as this was a single‐patient report, its clinical applicability is limited.

Depression and anxiety:
The treatment landscape for depression and anxiety has continuously evolved over the years, as both disorders remain prevalent and challenging in modern society. Common treatments include benzodiazepines, SSRIs, TCAs and, increasingly, cannabinoids [ 74 ]. Activation of the endocannabinoid system (ECS) has been associated with antidepressant and anxiolytic effects [ 75 ]. However, special caution is warranted when cannabinoids are combined with conventional antidepressants. Among younger patients, cannabis use has notably increased, raising concerns about its therapeutic role and safety [ 76 ]. A meta‐analysis has indicated potential stress‐reducing effects of cannabinoids, although these results may be overestimated because of publication bias [ 77 ]. Benzodiazepines, which act by enhancing GABA neurotransmission, are commonly used for short‐term anxiety relief. Observational data have shown a decrease in benzodiazepine prescriptions in patients initiating medical cannabis, although causality cannot be established [ 78 ]. CBD has been reported to increase clobazam plasma levels via CYP3A4 inhibition, and its co‐administration with chlorodiazepoxide, clonazepam or ethosuximide may reduce these drugs' antiseizure effects while increasing sedation risk [ 79 ]. Conversely, diazepam's effectiveness may be reduced with the co‐use of THC, CBD or CBN, because of altered hepatic metabolism. In such cases, dose adjustments might be needed, although individual responses vary [ 80 ].
Despite the lack of dedicated clinical trials, the combination of cannabinoids with SSRIs is generally discouraged. Preclinical evidence shows bidirectional modulation of serotonergic signalling by cannabinoids, especially via 5‐HT2A and 5‐HT1A receptors [ 76 ]. THC and CBD may reduce the clearance of SSRIs (approximately 25%) like sertraline and escitalopram by inhibiting CYP2C19, raising the potential for adverse effects or required dose adjustments [ 76 ,  81 ].

Schizophrenia:
There is growing interest in exploring the potential therapeutic effects of cannabinoids, particularly CBD, in the management of schizophrenia, although their efficacy remains unproven [ 82 ]. The relationship between cannabinoid uses and schizophrenia is complex and multifaceted. A long‐standing hypothesis suggests that cannabis use—especially involving THC—may trigger or exacerbate psychosis, often referred to as cannabis‐induced psychosis (CIP) [ 83 ,  84 ,  85 ]. This condition has been linked to THC's PKs and its active metabolites, which may elevate dopamine levels and mimic effects seen with stimulants [ 86 ]. The debate continues as to whether CIP is a distinct disorder or a manifestation of schizophrenia itself. It has been proposed that the disruption of neuronal connectivity in circuits involved in reward and cognition may underlie both the negative and positive symptoms of schizophrenia in cannabis users. In individuals predisposed to psychosis, cannabis use can exacerbate symptoms, leading to more frequent episodes and increased the risk of hospitalization [ 87 ].
Despite the focus on risk, clinical studies have also explored CBD's therapeutic potential. As a non‐psychotropic compound, CBD has been shown in preliminary evidence to improve the psychotic symptoms in patients with Parkinson's disease [ 88 ,  89 ]. In randomized trials, CBD added to antipsychotic treatment demonstrated promising improvements in positive symptoms, while maintaining tolerability [ 90 ]. However, in other studies, CBD had no significant effect on cognitive symptoms [ 91 ]. These findings suggest that clinical efficacy may depend on factors such as dose, illness stage and treatment duration. A case report study supported the possibility of hypothesis that CBD could improve treatment outcomes in resistant cases. One patient with longstanding schizophrenia showed improvements in both positive and negative symptoms when CBD was co‐administered with standard medication. This benefit may stem from CBD's distinct mechanism, which does not involve dopaminergic D2 receptor antagonism, allowing it to complement conventional antipsychotics [ 92 ].
Although CBD and many antipsychotic drugs share common metabolic pathways (notably CYP3A4 and CYP2D6), no clinically confirmed PK interactions between CBD and antipsychotics have been reported to date. Further PK and clinical studies are warranted to elucidate potential metabolic interactions and their therapeutic implications.

Epilepsy:
Since 2018, the FDA has approved Epidiolex® (CBD) for the treatment of seizures associated with LDS and DS in children 2 years of age and older. This marked a significant milestone in epilepsy management [ 93 ]. Although, the exact mechanism of CBD in epilepsy is not fully understood, it is thought to involve neurotransmitter modulation and anti‐inflammatory effects [ 94 ]. In contrast, THC, despite some anticonvulsant properties, is generally avoided because of its potential to trigger seizures, while CBN has shown mild anticonvulsant effects with minimal psychotropic activity [ 95 ].
Cannabinoids interactions with antiepileptic drugs are critical for ensuring treatment safety and efficacy. Specifically, CBD potently inhibits CYP2C19 and, to a lesser extent, CYP3A4, the enzymes mediating clobazam metabolism. Co‐administration can produce a threefold to fivefold rise in plasma levels of clobazam's active metabolite N‐desmethylclobazam accompanied by a modest elevation in CBD and 7‐OH‐CBD exposure because of reciprocal metabolic inhibition [ 96 ]. This bidirectional interaction amplifies both therapeutic and sedative responses. While efficacy outcomes were consistently better in CLB‐comedicated patients, statistically significant improvements were also observed in patients not receiving CLB, particularly in the 10 mg/kg/day CBD dose group in one of the LGS trials [ 97 ]. Meta‐analyses of four pivotal randomized controlled trials in LGS and DS showed that CBD reduced convulsive‐seizure frequency both in the presence and absence of clobazam, confirming intrinsic antiseizure activity, yet the magnitude of response was consistently higher with clobazam [ 98 ]. In patients off clobazam, pooled analyses still demonstrated a statistically significant seizure‐count reduction over placebo ( p  = .023) and higher responder rates ( p  ≤ .03) [ 96 ], establishing that CBD's efficacy is not entirely dependent on clobazam co‐therapy. Clinically, somnolence and sedation can occur in roughly 46% of CBD + clobazam users compared with 16% without clobazam, consistent with additive PD GABA‐A receptor modulation and benzodiazepine accumulation. Regulatory interpretations diverged accordingly: the EMA restricted Epidyolex® use in conjunction with CLB, whereas the FDA authorized CBD irrespective of clobazam status, acknowledging independent, though smaller, effects off‐CLB [ 96 ]. Collectively, current evidence indicates that CBD has genuine antiseizure efficacy, but its apparent potency in pivotal trials was quantitatively enhanced by a major CYP2C19‐mediated PK boost from clobazam [ 99 ], necessitating therapeutic‐drug monitoring of N‐CLB and possible clobazam‐dose adjustment during co‐administration.
Another investigation demonstrated that CBD increases the systemic exposure of several other co‐administered antiepileptic drugs, including topiramate, rufinamide, zonisamide and eslicarbazepine. Abnormal liver function tests have been observed in patients using CBD alongside valproate. Even when serum levels of clobazam decrease, increased sedation has been reported, likely because of enhanced metabolite activity. These effects are attributed to CBD's inhibition of CYP enzymes [ 100 ]. Preclinical studies have also identified THC and CBD as P‐glycoprotein (P‐gp) inhibitors, potentially altering the bioavailability of opioids such as oxycodone and methadone. In the context of a Phase 1 Healthy Volunteer Trial, CBD co‐administration with everolimus was found to increase blood concentrations of everolimus, likely because of P‐gp inhibition [ 101 ]. However, the robustness of these findings is limited.
PD interactions have also been reported. CBD combined with clobazam in paediatric populations has led to improved seizure control and also a higher risk of sedation [ 22 ]. Similar findings have been reported in broader patient populations with LGS and DS, where adjunctive CBD therapy reduced seizure frequency. Adverse events were common but generally manageable [ 102 ]. While it is a standard practice to monitor drug usage regularly, it is also essential the close hepatic monitoring, particularly when CBD is used with valproate, as this combination can elevate liver enzymes [ 100 ,  103 ]. Despite these effects, a safety study suggests that CBD does not significantly alter valproate plasma levels, indicating that the interaction is primarily PD [ 97 ].

Cancer:
Patients with cancer often turn to alternative therapies, such as medicinal cannabis or CBD products, to manage symptoms of the disease, its psychological effects or side effects from standard treatments [ 104 ]. Cannabis is commonly used for pain relief, and many patients report benefits. However, the potential for DDIs with anticancer agents remains a major concern. A high prevalence of potential DDIs has been reported among cancer patients, even without cannabinoid use [ 105 ], indicating that cannabis may further complicate drug safety profiles. A comprehensive survey of 612 breast cancer patients also revealed underreporting of cannabis use to health‐care providers and misconceptions about its curative potential, despite a lack of clinical trial evidence [ 106 ].
From a clinical perspective, THC has been studied for symptom relief—especially for pain, nausea, anxiety and insomnia. However, owing to its psychotropic effects, CBD or CBD‐THC combinations are often preferred. Comparative studies have not demonstrated significant differences in efficacy among CBD, THC and combination therapies across various symptom domains [ 107 ]. Preliminary studies have examined CBD co‐administration with radiochemotherapy and other agents in patients with brain tumours. Notably, tumour stabilization or regression and improved seizure control were observed when CBD was combined with standard therapies or agents like temozolomide, levetiracetam, dronabinol and lacosamide [ 108 ]. Furthermore, in a case study of two patients with high‐grade gliomas, those self‐administering CBD oil experienced tumour reduction or symptom improvement. Both patients remained without disease progression for at least 2 years, suggesting a potential role for CBD in enhancing treatment efficacy and improving survival rates [ 109 ].
Regarding cannabis administration, smoking is discouraged during active breast treatment because of potential pulmonary and immune system complications. Surprisingly, a case report of a patient with lung cancer was published in 2021, prompting consideration of CBD oil as a potential lung cancer therapy. This patient opted against conventional lung cancer therapy (surgery, chemotherapy and radiotherapy) and, unbeknownst to her medical team, chose to self‐administer oral CBD oil 2–3 times daily. Notably, serial imaging data revealed a progressive reduction in her lung tumour, shrinking from 41 to 10 mm over 2.5 years [ 110 ]. Similar findings have been reported in the study of Sulé‐Suso et al. [ 111 ] presenting the case of a patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and histologically proven adenocarcinoma. This patient self‐administered CBD oil without receiving any other treatment or dietary changes. After 2 months of treatment with two drops daily (0.06 mL, 1.32 mg CBD), the complete resolution of the tumour mass in the left lower lobe was observed, with only a minor area of persistent speculated soft tissue [ 111 ]. These case reports suggest potential benefits of CBD in lung cancer, but the evidence remains sparse and largely observational.

Cannabinoid interactions with cardiometabolic disease treatments:
The safety of individuals who choose to incorporate cannabis into their daily routine is questioned, particularly when used alongside cardiometabolic medications. For instance, combining cannabis with beta‐blockers may cause significant variations in heart rate and blood pressure [ 112 ]. Some preclinical and epidemiological studies suggest that cannabis may influence metabolic parameters such as body weight and insulin sensitivity, with some evidence pointing to lower obesity and diabetes prevalence among users [ 20 ]. Cannabinoids are known to inhibit various CYP450 enzymes, which may alter the metabolism of medications commonly prescribed for cardiometabolic diseases. THC, CBD and CBN inhibit CYP3A4, potentially increasing the exposure and duration of action of drugs such as antiarrhythmics, statins, calcium channel blockers and nitrates [ 18 ,  113 ]. Inhibition of CYP2C9 can similarly affect the metabolism of medications like warfarin, NSAIDs and additional statins. CYP2D6 inhibition by cannabinoids can elevate plasma levels of beta‐blockers such as metoprolol and carvedilol [ 18 ,  114 ]. Additionally, CBD inhibits CYP2C19 activity at clinically relevant concentrations, potentially leading to interactions with clopidogrel, as clopidogrel is a prodrug required activation by the liver enzyme CYP2C19 to exert its therapeutic effects [ 40 ]. Dose adjustments and close patient monitoring are recommended. For warfarin, INR monitoring is recommended in patients concurrently using cannabis [ 115 ]. Although case reports have documented stable INR levels in some patients using THC/CBD formulations, variability in dosage and administration route can influence outcomes [ 116 ].
Cannabinoids may also influence glucose metabolism. While some findings suggest improved insulin sensitivity in certain subgroups, particularly obese individuals, spontaneous cannabis use can stimulate appetite and increase carbohydrate cravings, potentially destabilizing glycaemic control [ 117 ]. Cannabinoids can increase appetite and the desire for carbohydrates consumption, which may lead to elevated blood glucose levels [ 118 ]. When administered with metformin, CBD may enhance hypoglycaemic effects, necessitating careful monitoring to prevent adverse reactions such as dizziness or fainting. As with other drug interactions, patients should not alter their treatment regimens without medical consultation [ 119 ].

Dose‐ and route‐dependent cannabinoids DDIs:
Among the cannabinoids, CBD exhibits the highest potential for clinically relevant DDIs, especially at therapeutic doses used in products such as Epidiolex® (up to 25 mg/kg/day). CBD has been shown to inhibit several key drug‐metabolizing enzymes, including CYP2C19, CYP2C9, CYP3A4, UGT1A9 and UGT2B7, which can lead to elevated plasma concentrations of co‐administered medications [ 22 ,  100 ]. It has also demonstrated interaction with valproate, with a 17–30% decrease in valproate and metabolite (4‐ene‐VPA) exposure noted in a Phase 2 trial (CBD 5–20 mg/kg), accompanied by elevated liver enzymes in several subjects [ 120 ]. However, a clinical study conducted in healthy volunteers assessing potential DDIs between CBD and clobazam, stiripentol, and valproate reported minimal to no clinically relevant changes in drug exposure [ 121 ]. In contrast, four clinical studies involving patients with LGS and DS observed an increased incidence of somnolence and sedation when CBD was used in combination with clobazam, consistent with both PK (via CYP2C19‐mediated increases in clobazam and N‐desmethylclobazam levels) and PD mechanisms [ 122 ]. Additionally, THC can also inhibit CYP1A2 and CYP2C9, particularly when inhaled, but clinical consequences appear more variable and dose dependent, with interactions most commonly observed in cases involving warfarin and opioids [ 21 ,  116 ]. Clinical evidence from a phase I crossover study has also demonstrated that the co‐administration of Sativex® (a 1:1 THC/CBD oromucosal spray) with the CYP3A4 inhibitor ketoconazole (400 mg) led to a marked increase in cannabinoid exposure. In particular, THC AUC increased by 82%, and CBD AUC by 84%, confirming the involvement of CYP3A4 in their metabolism. This supports the need for dose adjustment or monitoring when Sativex® is combined with potent CYP3A4 inhibitors [ 36 ]. CBN and CBG, though less studied, show moderate CYP inhibition in vitro and may affect the metabolism of drugs such as bupropion and beta‐blockers. Nevertheless, no clinical trials are currently available, with systematic reviews highlighting potential interactions with narrow therapeutic index drugs, especially anticonvulsants (e.g. carbamazepine and brivaracetam), warfarin and tacrolimus [ 123 ].

CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES ON CANNABIS LEGISLATION IN EUROPEAN UNION:
Cannabis legislation across European Union (EU) countries reflects a diverse and evolving legal landscape. Medical cannabis is legalized in several EU countries, with Germany, Italy, Austria and France providing regulated access to cannabis‐based treatments under strict controls. Germany allows prescriptions for serious conditions, with costs often covered by insurance, while Italy and Austria oversee production and distribution through pharmacies. France has initiated a pilot programme to evaluate cannabis‐based therapeutic applications. Conversely, recreational cannabis policies vary widely. The Netherlands employs a toleration policy permitting sales in licensed coffee shops under strict conditions, while Spain decriminalizes personal cultivation and private use, supported by Cannabis Social Clubs. Belgium allows limited personal possession but enforces restrictions in public places. Sweden has adopted a zero‐tolerance approach, strictly prohibiting all cannabis use. Additionally, industrial applications of cannabis are gaining ground in Greece and the United Kingdom, while the Nordic countries, such as Denmark and Norway, are navigating cannabis reforms through pilot programmes and public debates [ 124 ]. Overall, EU countries demonstrate efforts to balance medical innovation and public health, while addressing the complexities of drug policies and the risks of cannabis misuse.
In the context of this evolving landscape, the potential establishment of free‐access cannabis environments highlights the importance of addressing PK DDIs. Current research underscores cannabinoids' significant effects on CYP450 enzymes and ABC transporters, which critically influence the ADME profiles of numerous drugs. The review highlights several clinically relevant PK interactions involving cannabinoids, particularly CBD and THC. CBD has been shown to inhibit key cytochrome P450 enzymes, including CYP2C9, CYP2C19 and CYP3A4, leading to altered metabolism of co‐administered drugs such as clobazam, where increased levels of its active metabolite have been associated with enhanced sedation. PK interactions have been also observed with a range of therapeutics including warfarin, where CBD may increase anticoagulant effects by inhibiting CYP2C9, and chemotherapeutics like temozolomide. Similarly, CBD may impair glutathione‐mediated detoxification pathways, as evidenced by increased hepatotoxicity when combined with acetaminophen. THC, however, has demonstrated the ability to modulate the PKs of opioids such as hydrocodone by accelerating drug clearance, potentially diminishing analgesic efficacy. Both cannabinoids may also interact with efflux transporters like P‐glycoprotein, further influencing drug disposition, particularly in the central nervous system. These findings underscore the need for careful assessment of cannabinoid–drug interactions in clinical settings, especially in populations with complex medication regimens. These interactions require careful dose adjustments and patient monitoring, especially for medications with narrow therapeutic indices. While cannabinoids, such as CBD, exhibit promising therapeutic effects for conditions, such as epilepsy, chronic pain and potentially schizophrenia, their use remains complex when co‐administered with other medications. Psychotropic effects, risks of exacerbating psychiatric conditions and interactions with medications, such as antiepileptics, anticoagulants and cardiometabolic treatments, are the key challenges. Moreover, the variability in individual responses and the limited clinical data emphasize the need for cautious and informed integration into treatment protocols. Future research should prioritize long‐term studies, comprehensive PK profiling and broader clinical trials to fully exploit the therapeutic potential of cannabinoids while mitigating associated risks.
