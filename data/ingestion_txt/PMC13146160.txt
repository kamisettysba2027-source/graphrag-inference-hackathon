[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
ABSTRACT Background Although semaglutide 2.4 mg has demonstrated significant weight loss efficacy in clinical trials, real‐world data, particularly with regard to clinically complex and underrepresented populations, remain limited. Objectives The study aims to assess the real‐world effectiveness and patient‐reported outcomes associated with the use of semaglutide 2.4 mg in individuals with severe and complex obesity. The study also intends to characterize weight loss response in pre‐defined subgroups of patients and to identify predictors of weight loss using machine learning. Methods SEMASEARCH is a retrospective multicentric observational cohort embedded within the French early‐access program for semaglutide 2.4 mg. A total of 1100 patients with severe obesity (BMI ≥ 40 kg/m 2  with at least one treated obesity‐related complication) were retrospectively included from 11 expert obesity centers, based on prospectively collected data at baseline, 6 months, and 12 months. Subgroups include patients with a history of bariatric surgery, binge eating disorder, hypothalamic obesity, age ≥ 60 years or altered body composition, BMI ≥ 60 kg/m 2 , and those receiving psychotropic medications. Assessments include clinical, biological, and body composition data, as well as standardized questionnaires evaluating eating behaviour, physical activity, sleep, quality of life, digestive symptoms, and mental health. Primary Outcome Body weight change since treatment initiation, with assessment at 6 and 12 months. Secondary Outcomes Clinically meaningful weight loss (≥ 10%) at 12 months, metabolic improvements, patient‐reported outcomes, body composition changes, and tolerance. Expected Impact SEMASEARCH will provide real‐world evidence on Semaglutide 2.4 mg use in patients living with severe and complex obesity. It will also address major knowledge gaps in specific populations underrepresented in clinical trials and generate predictive models of weight loss response.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Semaglutide 2.4 mg, a glucagon‐like peptide‐1 (GLP‐1) receptor agonist (GLP‐1RA), has demonstrated significant efficacy in inducing weight loss and improving obesity‐related comorbidities in patients living with obesity (PLO), as shown in randomized controlled trials such as the STEP and the SELECT programs [ 1 ,  2 ]. However, these studies were conducted on highly selected populations undergoing intensive lifestyle interventions (in the case of STEP) and under close clinical supervision, which may limit their generalization to routine clinical practice. Recent data from real‐world cohorts of PLO suggest that Semaglutide 2.4 mg can achieve weight loss outcomes comparable to those observed in clinical trials [ 3 ,  4 ]. Nevertheless, there is a lack of real‐world evidence, and several important questions remain unanswered.
First, the effectiveness and safety of Semaglutide 2.4 mg in subgroups that are often excluded or underrepresented in clinical trials has not been widely studied. These subgroups include patients who have previously undergone unsuccessful bariatric surgery, individuals with binge eating disorder, those with hypothalamic obesity (HO), older adults (≥ 60 years) or individuals with altered body composition (reduced muscle mass), patients with extreme obesity (BMI ≥ 60 kg/m 2 ), and those treated with psychotropic medications.
Second, most clinical and observational studies to date have primarily focused on weight loss efficacy and safety outcomes. Changes in eating behaviour, including compulsive eating, binge eating, and hunger regulation, as well as physical activity levels, digestive comfort, chronotype, and quality of life are rarely documented, particularly in real‐world settings.
Third, although semaglutide‐induced weight loss is consistently associated with a reduction of fat mass, its effect on lean mass and body composition is still being debated [ 5 ].
Finally, while some predictors of weight loss response such as sex, baseline BMI, presence of type 2 diabetes (T2D), and prior GLP‐1RA treatment have been identified [ 2 ], other potentially relevant factors affecting treatment resistance or response have not yet been explored. These include predictors that can only be acquired through comprehensive phenotyping, which involves clinical, biological, and body composition assessments, as well as eating behaviour, physical activity, and psychology using standardized questionnaires. Integrating machine learning approaches could facilitate the identification of predictors, enabling more personalized management strategies for PLO.
SEMASEARCH is a multicentre, retrospective study that will combine real‐world data collection with extensive clinical phenotyping. The study will include subgroups of PLO that are underrepresented in clinical trials. Additionally, artificial intelligence will be used to develop predictive models of treatment response with the aim of proposing personalized management strategies for severe obesity.

Patients:
The SEMASEARCH study is a multicentre, retrospective, real‐world cohort conducted in France within the framework of the early‐access program for semaglutide 2.4 mg once weekly. Details of the program and baseline characteristics of the population have been previously reported [ 6 ]. Briefly, eligible patients had a BMI ≥ 40 kg/m 2  and at least one treated obesity‐related severe comorbidity among the following: hypertension, dyslipidemia, obstructive sleep apnea, or cardiovascular disease. Between March 2022 and October 2023, over 9000 patients were included from more than 200 centres. Compared with randomized clinical trials, this cohort, recruited from specialized obesity centres, included patients with much more severe obesity, higher comorbidity prevalence, and greater clinical complexity [ 6 ]. Semaglutide was initiated at 0.25 mg/week (or 1.7 mg/week in patients with T2D already treated with semaglutide 1 mg), with monthly titration according to tolerance, up to 2.4 mg or the maximum tolerated dose. Nutritional and physical activity counselling followed routine practice at each centre, consistent with recent French national recommendations emphasizing multidisciplinary management of severe obesity [ 7 ], combining lifestyle modification and psychological support.
To enable analyses requiring detailed patient‐reported outcomes and longitudinal phenotyping, a subcohort of 1100 patients was retrospectively extracted from 11 university hospital centres with expertise in severe obesity management. Due to financial constraints, inclusion of the entire national early‐access cohort (> 9000 patients) was not feasible. Each centre was asked to include a capped number of patients proportional to its total number of treated individuals in order to ensure balanced representation. The selection strategy prioritized (i) patients with HO, given the rarity and unmet clinical need in this population, and (ii) patients with available baseline and 12‐month follow‐up data, particularly completed questionnaires, to allow longitudinal analyses.

Baseline and Follow Up Prospective Assessment:
Although inclusion in the SEMASEARCH study was retrospective, data were prospectively collected at baseline (before semaglutide 2.4 mg initiation), 6 months, and 12 months within the early‐access program.

Medical History:
At baseline, demographic characteristics, lifestyle factors (alcohol consumption, smoking), personal and family medical history, obesity‐related complications, and treatments were recorded. The detailed obesity history included the following: age at onset, early‐adulthood weight, maximum lifetime weight, recent weight change, prior weight‐loss attempts, and previous use of GLP‐1RA or other anti‐obesity drugs.
At each visit, semaglutide dosing data were recorded, including current dose, maximum achieved dose, time to reach maximum dose, dose reductions, and treatment discontinuation when applicable.

Clinical, Body Composition and Biological Assessment:
At each visit, weight, height, blood pressure, and heart rate were measured. Body composition assessment data by bioelectrical impedance (BIA) or dual‐energy x‐ray absorptiometry (DXA) were collected at baseline and at 12 months when available. Functional tests included the timed chair stand and handgrip strength and were performed if available at baseline and 12 months. Biological assessments at each visit included renal function (creatinine, estimated glomerular filtration rate by CKD‐EPI formula), liver enzymes (ASAT, ALAT, GGT), hs‐CRP (systemic inflammation), albumin, and uric acid. Fasting lipid profile (total cholesterol, HDL, LDL, triglycerides), fasting plasma glucose, and HbA1c were also recorded.

Eating Behaviour, Physical Activity, Quality of Life and Psychological Assessment:
A series of validated patient‐reported outcome measures were collected at each visit. These included:
 – Eating behaviour and hyperphagia: Binge Eating Scale (BES), Dutch Eating Behaviour Questionnaire (DEBQ), Hunger score – Physical activity: International Physical Activity Questionnaire—short form (IPAQ‐SF) – Chronotype and sleep patterns: Munich Chronotype Questionnaire (MCTQ) – Quality of life: EuroQol 5‐Dimension 5‐Level questionnaire (EQ‐5D‐5L) – Digestive well‐being: Gastrointestinal Quality of Life Index (GIQLI) – Mental health: Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS)
These questionnaires were administered at baseline (treatment initiation), at 6 and 12 months. Completion rates varied by centre. Efforts were made to prioritize patients with comprehensive longitudinal data.

Subgroups Inclusion Criteria and Sample Size:
The definition and inclusion criteria of each subgroup are summarized in Table  1 . The presence of binge eating disorder was evaluated according to DSM‐5 criteria or according to the binge eating scale score (BES ≥ 18 was used to estimate the presence of a binge eating disorder). HO encompassed both genetic (monogenic or syndromic) and acquired hypothalamic lesions, with further details on lesion type, pathogenic variants, and associated neurodevelopmental features. History of bariatric surgery and postoperative weight trajectory were documented. The control group was not a separately recruited population but comprised SEMASEARCH participants who did not fulfil criteria for any predefined subgroup.
The sample size was calculated from raw data at the end of the study inclusions but before data curation. Therefore, it may differ slightly from definitive numbers.

Outcomes:
The primary outcome is the body weight change from treatment initiation, with assessment at 6 and 12 months. Secondary outcomes include the proportion of patients who achieve a clinically meaningful weight loss, defined as a reduction of ≥ 10% at 12 months. Additionally, changes in eating behaviour, physical activity, sleep patterns, quality of life, digestive well‐being, and symptoms of anxiety and depression are assessed from baseline to 6 and 12 months using the validated instruments described above. Metabolic outcomes are evaluated through fasting plasma glucose, liver enzymes, and fasting lipid profiles. Safety outcomes include clinically relevant adverse events as reported by treating physicians during routine care. No formal adverse event grading system or centralized adjudication was implemented. All outcomes will be examined overall and in each pre‐defined subpopulation of interest.

Statistical Analysis:
Assuming that 60% of patients respond to treatment at 1 year, a total of 1100 patients will provide a precision of ±3.5% (half‐width of the 95% confidence interval) in the percentage estimate. In the smallest subpopulations, a total of 50 patients will allow for a precision of ±13.6% (half‐width of the 95% confidence interval) in the percentage estimate.
Primary analyses estimate weight change at 6 and 12 months among participants with available follow‐up measurements, corresponding to an as‐observed (on‐treatment–like) estimand within the SEMASEARCH subcohort. Change in body weight at 6 and 12 months relative to baseline will be analysed using linear mixed‐effects models accounting for repeated measures. The models will take into account time as a fixed effect, as well as relevant prognostic factors of weight evolution, and will include random intercept and slope for each patient. Missing data will be assumed to be missing at random, conditional on previous weights for a given patient, and conditional on the prognostic factors included in the model, consistent with hypotheses of mixed effect models. Sensitivity analyses will be conducted using imputation of missing weight values based on the 5th percentile of observed weight change distribution prior to reanalysis corresponding to a maximum bias approach. The proportion of patients achieving clinically meaningful weight loss (≥ 10%) at 12 months will be estimated with 95% confidence intervals. In addition, multiple imputation may be performed as a sensitivity analysis if the extent of missing data exceeds expectations.
Primary analyses will assess weight trajectories irrespective of achieved dose. Sensitivity analyses will incorporate maximum achieved dose and time to maximum dose as covariates.
Exploratory analyses of treatment heterogeneity will use logistic regression to evaluate associations between candidate predictors and ≥ 10% weight loss, with adjustment for established predictors (age, sex, baseline BMI, type 2 diabetes, prior obesity treatment). Clustering by centre will be addressed by including centre as a random effect or adjustment variable. Subgroup‐specific models will compare each predefined subgroup with the control group. Where appropriate, adjustments for multiple comparisons will be applied within thematic predictor groups.
All analyses will be conducted using R and SAS software, with a two‐sided significance threshold set at 0.05. Machine learning analyses are detailed in the dedicated paragraph below.

Ethics Statement:
All participants were informed about the objectives of the SEMASEARCH study and provided non‐opposition in accordance with French regulations. The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the appropriate national ethics committee. Data were collected in compliance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and applicable local laws.

Bariatric Surgery:
Bariatric surgery (mainly Roux‐en‐Y gastric bypass and sleeve gastrectomy) provides the largest weight loss among available treatments, but outcomes remain highly variable, with a substantial proportion of patients experiencing insufficient weight loss or weight regain [ 9 ,  10 ].
GLP‐1 receptor agonists have emerged as a potential therapeutic option in this context. However, evidence specifically evaluating semaglutide after bariatric surgery remains limited and heterogeneous. Existing studies are based on small sample sizes and often use variable dosing regimens, which limits the ability to analyse outcomes according to surgical technique, time since surgery, or distinct weight‐trajectory profiles such as insufficient weight loss versus weight regain [ 11 ].
This work package aims to compare the weight loss outcomes of semaglutide in patients with prior bariatric surgery (successful or unsuccessful) with those in the control group and to further determine the impact of surgery technique and duration since surgery on weight trajectories.

Binge Eating Disorder:
Binge eating disorder (BED) is common among people living with obesity, with a prevalence reaching up to 9% and likely underestimated [ 12 ,  13 ].
Pharmacological treatments used in addition to behavioural approaches for BED are scarce and exhibit only modest weight‐efficacy [ 14 ].
GLP‐1 receptor agonists enhance satiety and may reduce food cravings [ 15 ], and preliminary studies suggest potential improvement in binge eating symptoms. However, available data are based on small cohorts and short follow‐up, and the durability of these effects remains uncertain [ 16 ,  17 ].
This work package aims to evaluate Semaglutide 2.4 mg effects on weight loss and BED symptoms over a 12 months period in PLO and BED.

Hypothalamic Obesity:
HO, whether genetic or acquired, results from disruption of hypothalamic pathways regulating energy balance and is characterized by severe, treatment‐resistant obesity with hyperphagia and metabolic complications [ 18 ].
So far, HO management has relied mainly on lifestyle intervention, including nutritional counselling and physical activity, and in some cases, setmelanotide. Because GLP‐1RAs promote satiety through both hypothalamic and extra‐hypothalamic pathways, they have emerged as a potential therapeutic option in HO. However, evidence in both acquired and genetic HO is scarce and based on small cohorts with variable designs and semaglutide dosage [ 19 ,  20 ,  21 ,  22 ,  23 ,  24 ,  25 ,  26 ].
This work package aims to evaluate the effects on weight loss, eating behaviour and quality of life in the subgroup of patients with HO. We will also compare outcomes across HO subtypes when statistical power allows the analysis.

Adults With Altered Body Composition and Older Individuals:
Sarcopenia is defined as a progressive decline in skeletal muscle mass and strength, is common in chronic disease and aging [ 27 ]. The coexistence of obesity and sarcopenia has recently been recognized [ 8 ]. Although GLP‐1 receptor agonists induce substantial weight loss, concerns remain regarding potential lean mass loss and its impact on muscle function and frailty, particularly in adults aged ≥ 60 years and in individuals with altered body composition. Clinical trials indicate that 20%–40% of GLP‐1RA–induced weight loss corresponds to reduction in lean mass [ 28 ,  29 ] raising concerns about potential muscle loss [ 30 ]. However, imaging data indicate that these changes may reflect physiological adaptation to weight loss rather than true deterioration of muscle quality, with possible improvements in lean mass–to–fat mass ratio and intramuscular fat [ 28 ,  29 ,  30 ,  31 ]. A recent study of semaglutide 2.4 mg reported preserved or even improved muscle function despite lean mass loss, although these findings require confirmation [ 32 ].
This work package aims to evaluate the effects of semaglutide 2.4 mg/week on body composition and muscle function, and to determine if these effects differ in patients with altered body composition or aged ≥ 60 years‐old and the control group.

Extreme Obesity (BMI ≥ 60 Kg/m2):
Individuals with extreme obesity (BMI ≥ 60 kg/m 2 ) present with more severe comorbidities, higher surgical risk, and poorer quality of life than those with lower BMI classes [ 33 ]. Given the elevated operative risk in this population, effective pharmacological options are particularly relevant, either as alternatives to surgery or as preoperative optimization strategies. However, data on pharmacological obesity treatments in this subgroup remain scarce, and no randomized trials have specifically targeted patients with extreme obesity.
Evidence from clinical trials suggests that baseline BMI influences weight‐loss response to semaglutide, with lower relative weight loss observed at higher baseline BMI levels [ 34 ,  35 ,  36 ,  37 ,  38 ], raising questions about its relevance or the adequate dose in populations with extreme obesity.
This work package aims to assess semaglutide outcomes in patients with extreme obesity.

Psychotropic Medications:
The relationship between psychiatric disorders and obesity is bidirectional [ 39 ,  40 ,  41 ]. Weight gain associated with psychotropic medication, particularly antidepressants, antipsychotics, and mood stabilizers, represents a major clinical challenge and may complicate obesity management [ 42 ,  43 ,  44 ,  45 ]. It can impair treatment adherence and worsen both metabolic and psychiatric outcomes [ 46 ].
On the other hand, severe psychiatric disorders may limit the effectiveness of lifestyle interventions [ 47 ], and may even contraindicate bariatric surgery [ 48 ]. However, data on the effectiveness of semaglutide in patients receiving psychotropic treatments remain limited.
This work package aims to assess how weight‐loss effectiveness of semaglutide 2.4 mg is influenced by different psychotropic drug classes (antidepressants, mood stabilizers, antipsychotics, stimulants, anxiolytics).

Machine Learning Prediction of Outcomes:
Given the high variability in individual responses to semaglutide 2.4 mg—ranging from minimal to double‐digit % weight loss [ 1 ]—there is a pressing need for tools that can anticipate treatment outcomes on a personalized level. Traditional statistical approaches have difficulty disentangling the complex interplay of numerous clinical, behavioural, and biological factors, many of which are inter‐correlated. Machine learning can address this challenge by uncovering nonlinear patterns and interactions that might not be apparent through conventional analyses.
Predictive analyses in SEMASEARCH are exploratory and hypothesis‐generating but follow a predefined analytical framework. Supervised algorithms will include penalized logistic regression, random forests, and gradient boosting models (e.g., XGBoost), applied in both regression (continuous weight loss) and classification (≥ 10% weight loss at 12 months) settings. Data preprocessing will include scaling of continuous variables and appropriate encoding of categorical predictors. Missing data will be handled using multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE), with simpler imputation methods (median or mode) used for variables with minimal missingness when appropriate. All preprocessing and imputation procedures will be conducted within cross‐validation folds to prevent data leakage. Feature selection will combine clinical preselection and penalized regression approaches. Validated global questionnaire scores will be included as primary predictors, while individual questionnaire items may be selected using LASSO regularization to reduce dimensionality and limit overfitting. Model development will rely on repeated k‐fold cross‐validation with hyperparameter tuning. For classification models, class imbalance will be addressed using SMOTE or class‐weighted algorithms. Performance will be evaluated using the area under the ROC curve (AUC) calculated on held‐out validation folds. Model robustness will be assessed through repeated resampling and sensitivity analyses, including exclusion of highly heterogeneous subgroups such as rare HO. The objectives are to explore potential predictors of treatment response and assess the feasibility of developing clinically informative prediction tools. No external validation dataset is currently available; therefore, findings should be interpreted as exploratory and will require independent validation before clinical implementation.

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
The SEMASEARCH study is the first large‐scale real‐world cohort to integrate detailed clinical, biological, behavioural, and body composition data in patients treated with semaglutide 2.4 mg in routine care. By focusing on clinically complex and underrepresented subgroups, the study aims to provide a more nuanced understanding of treatment response across heterogeneous obesity phenotypes. The integration of comprehensive phenotyping with predictive modelling may contribute to more individualized weight‐loss strategies.
The study has several strengths, including its large sample size, detailed longitudinal phenotyping, and inclusion of patients typically excluded from randomized trials. Including standardized, pre‐specified patient‐reported outcomes enables a more comprehensive evaluation of the patient experience, considering factors beyond weight loss. Several limitations must be acknowledged. Although data were prospectively collected, inclusion in the SEMASEARCH subcohort was retrospective and selective, potentially enriching the cohort with more adherent patients and limiting representativeness of the overall early‐access population. Accordingly, the primary analyses reflect an as‐observed estimand among participants with available follow‐up data and should not be interpreted as a treatment‐policy effect in the overall early‐access population. The absence of an untreated or alternative‐treatment comparator restricts causal inference, and findings should be interpreted as descriptive and hypothesis‐generating. The control group, defined by exclusion of predefined subgroups, represents a heterogeneous reference population. The multicentre real‐world design introduces heterogeneity in clinical practices, laboratory assays, and body composition assessments. Although centre effects are accounted for in statistical analyses and follow‐up measurements were generally performed using the same modality within each centre, residual variability cannot be excluded. Finally, safety data relied partly on physician‐reported adverse events without standardized grading. Although gastrointestinal symptoms were systematically assessed, other adverse events may be underreported.

[SECTION: CONCLUSION]
The SEMASEARCH study will provide real‐world data on weight outcomes and safety of semaglutide 2.4 mg for treating severe obesity, including in patients with complex clinical presentations. By characterizing weight loss trajectories and associated clinical changes across diverse subgroups, and by leveraging machine learning to predict individual responses to obesity treatment, the SEMASEARCH study has the potential to inform both current practice and future therapeutic development. Further longitudinal follow‐up beyond 12 months may be warranted to evaluate long‐term weight maintenance, safety, and metabolic durability.
