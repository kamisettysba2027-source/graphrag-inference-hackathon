Title: Association of GLP‐1 Receptor Agonists With Hepatic Decompensation in the All of Us Research Program

[SECTION: ABSTRACT]
ABSTRACT Aims To evaluate the association between glucagon‐like peptide‐1 receptor agonist (GLP‐1RA) initiation and the risk of hepatic decompensation compared with dipeptidyl peptidase 4 inhibitors (DPP4is) in a racially and ethnically diverse cohort of adults with Type 2 diabetes. Materials and Methods This retrospective active‐comparator new‐user cohort study utilised data from the National Institutes of Health's  All of Us  Research Program. Adult participants with Type 2 diabetes initiating GLP‐1RAs or DPP4is without prior hepatic decompensation or competing causes of chronic liver disease were identified. Stratified propensity score matching (1:1) was employed to balance baseline covariates. The primary outcome was a composite of hepatic decompensation events (oesophageal varices with bleeding, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, hepatic failure, or liver transplantation). Hazard ratios (HRs) were estimated using Cox proportional hazards regression. Results The matched cohort included 7854 participants (mean age 57.3 years; 58.8% female; 24.8% Black or African American). GLP‐1RA use was associated with a significantly reduced risk of hepatic decompensation compared with DPP4i use (HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.47–0.95;  p  = 0.026), corresponding to an absolute rate difference of −1.66 events per 1000 person‐years. Estimates were directionally similar in prespecified sensitivity analyses. Conclusions In this diverse nationwide cohort, initiation of GLP‐1RAs was associated with a significantly reduced risk of hepatic decompensation compared with DPP4is. These findings extend prior evidence by demonstrating this association in a cohort enriched for populations historically underrepresented in biomedical research.

While the efficacy of semaglutide in resolving MASH and improving fibrosis has been established in Phase 3 trials leading to regulatory approval, data on its ability to reduce the incidence of hard clinical endpoints remain forthcoming. Our findings add real‐world evidence that GLP‐1RA initiation is associated with a lower incidence of hepatic decompensation, complementing the histological improvements observed in clinical trials.

[SECTION: INTRODUCTION]
Metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) affects approximately 30% of the global adult population and can progress to metabolic dysfunction–associated steatohepatitis (MASH), cirrhosis, and eventually hepatic decompensation [ 1 ,  2 ,  3 ,  4 ]. Given the strong pathophysiologic link between MASH and metabolic comorbidities, glucagon‐like peptide‐1 receptor agonists (GLP‐1RAs) have emerged as a promising therapeutic class beyond their established roles in Type 2 diabetes and obesity [ 5 ,  6 ,  7 ]. This potential was recently underscored by the US Food and Drug Administration's accelerated approval of semaglutide for the treatment of MASH with moderate‐to‐advanced fibrosis, based on histological improvements observed in clinical trials [ 8 ,  9 ]. However, while the benefits of GLP‐1RAs on surrogate histological endpoints are increasingly recognised, evidence regarding their association with hard clinical outcomes such as hepatic decompensation in broad, real‐world populations remains limited.
Recent observational evidence, including a comprehensive meta‐analysis of over 1.4 million patients, indicates that GLP‐1RA use is associated with a reduced risk of major adverse liver outcomes (MALO) and hepatic decompensation compared with other glucose‐lowering medications [ 10 ,  11 ,  12 ,  13 ,  14 ,  15 ,  16 ]. However, primary data from these studies have been limited by a lack of diversity in sex and race or lack of granular demographic information [ 16 ]. Validating these observations in a diverse cohort prioritising populations underrepresented in biomedical research is critical to clarifying the association of these agents with a reduced risk of adverse liver outcomes and ensuring equitable clinical application.
To address this gap, we leveraged data from the National Institutes of Health's  All of Us  Research Program, a nationwide cohort explicitly designed to include those underrepresented populations [ 17 ,  18 ,  19 ]. While histologic improvement has driven regulatory approval, whether initiation of GLP‐1 receptor agonists is associated with a lower incidence of hepatic decompensation—the key inflection point toward liver‐related mortality—remains uncertain in routine clinical practice. Therefore, we aimed to evaluate the association between GLP‐1RA initiation and the risk of hepatic decompensation compared with DPP4i use in a diverse cohort of adults with Type 2 diabetes.

Study Design and Data Source:
This retrospective cohort study utilised data from the Curated Data Repository (CDR) version 8 of the  All of Us  Research Program, a large and diverse biomedical database established by the National Institutes of Health [ 20 ]. The  All of Us  dataset integrates electronic health records, physical measurements, and participant‐provided survey information [ 21 ]. We employed an active‐comparator new‐user cohort design (Figure  S1 ). The study protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Hanyang University Hospital, which waived the requirement for informed consent because the data were de‐identified (IRB No.: HYUH‐2025‐12‐006). This study followed the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) reporting guideline [ 22 ].

Study Population:
We identified adult individuals aged 18 to 75 years with a diagnosis of Type 2 diabetes who initiated treatment with either a GLP‐1RA or a DPP4i between January 1, 2006 and December 31, 2022. We restricted the upper age limit to 75 years to minimise confounding by frailty and the competing risk of mortality associated with advanced age.
The index date was defined as the date of the first prescription for either drug class. We included new users with at least 180 days of observable healthcare history and no study drug prescriptions during this period to ensure adequate baseline characterisation and confirm washout status. We excluded patients who initiated both drug classes on the index date to ensure distinct exposure groups. Further exclusions included patients with competing causes of chronic liver disease (e.g., viral hepatitis, alcohol‐associated liver disease), a history of hepatic decompensation events, or a diagnosis of hepatocellular carcinoma prior to the index date. The full list of concept IDs used to define study cohort is provided in Table  S1 . Flow of study participants is presented in Figure  1 .

Exposures and Outcomes:
The exposure was the initiation of either a GLP‐1RA or a DPP4i. We defined the follow‐up period starting 1 day after the index date. In the primary intent‐to‐treat (ITT) analysis, participants were followed until the occurrence of a study outcome, death, loss to follow‐up, or the end of the study period (December 31, 2022), whichever occurred first.
The outcome was hepatic decompensation, defined as a composite of oesophageal varices with bleeding, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, hepatic failure, or liver transplantation [ 14 ]. We ascertained these outcomes using standardised diagnostic concepts (Systematised Nomenclature of Medicine [SNOMED]) [ 23 ], mapped within the Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership (OMOP) Common Data Model (Table  S1 ).

Covariates:
We assessed baseline characteristics using electronic health records, physical measurements, and survey data available in the 365 days prior to the index date. Demographic variables included age, sex assigned at birth, and self‐reported race (categorised as Asian, Black or African American, White, or Other/Unknown).
Clinical characteristics were assessed through standardised physical measurements (weight, height, body mass index [BMI], systolic and diastolic blood pressure) and laboratory assessments, including haemoglobin A1c, glucose, lipid panel (total cholesterol, low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, triglycerides), and liver biochemistries (alanine aminotransferase, aspartate aminotransferase, alkaline phosphatase, total bilirubin). Estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) was calculated using the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration (CKD‐EPI) equation [ 24 ]. The Fibrosis‐4 (FIB‐4) index was calculated to assess the baseline severity of liver fibrosis.
We identified relevant comorbidities using SNOMED codes, including obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, heart failure, and MASH. Overall disease burden was summarised using the Charlson Comorbidity Index. Concomitant medication use was assessed for metformin, sodium‐glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors, sulfonylureas, thiazolidinediones, insulin, statins, and antihypertensive agents (ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers [ARBs], beta‐blockers, calcium channel blockers [CCBs], and diuretics). Healthcare utilisation was assessed as the number of outpatient visits in the year prior to the index date. Additionally, we utilised participant‐provided responses from surveys to determine smoking status and alcohol consumption.

Statistical Analysis:
To minimise confounding by indication, we employed propensity score matching. Missing baseline covariate data were handled using multiple imputation by chained equations generating 50 imputed datasets. We estimated the propensity score for initiating a GLP‐1RA versus a DPP4i using a multivariable logistic regression model within each imputed dataset. The resulting propensity scores were averaged across the datasets to obtain a single score for each participant. The model included all baseline covariates listed in Table  1  (demographics, vital signs, laboratory values, comorbidities, concomitant medications, and healthcare utilisation). We matched new users of GLP‐1RAs 1:1 with new users of DPP4is using a nearest‐neighbour greedy matching algorithm with a calliper width of 0.2 standard deviations of the logit of the propensity score. To ensure precise balance across key demographic subgroups, we employed stratified matching for race, smoking status, and alcohol consumption. Covariate balance was assessed using standardised mean differences (SMDs), with an absolute SMD of less than 0.1 considered indicative of negligible imbalance.
For our primary analysis, we employed an ITT approach within the propensity score‐matched cohort. We estimated hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the risk of hepatic decompensation using Cox proportional hazards regression models. Cumulative incidence curves were generated using the Kaplan–Meier method and compared using the log‐rank test.
Secondary outcomes included the individual components of the primary composite endpoint, all‐cause mortality, and changes in weight and BMI. Longitudinal trajectories of weight and BMI were assessed over a 5‐year follow‐up period using linear mixed‐effects models. These models included fixed effects for treatment group, time (categorical years), and their interaction, with a random intercept for each participant to account for within‐subject correlation.
We performed subgroup analyses to assess potential effect modification by age (< 65 vs. ≥ 65 years), sex, race and ethnicity, obesity status, duration of Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and specific GLP‐1RA agents (exenatide, liraglutide, dulaglutide, and semaglutide). Interactions were evaluated using likelihood ratio tests.
We conducted several sensitivity analyses to evaluate the robustness of our findings: (1) an as‐treated (AT) analysis, where follow‐up was censored at the earliest occurrence of index medication discontinuation (defined as a gap in supply > 180 days), switching to an alternative glucose‐lowering agent, death, loss to follow‐up, or study end; (2) an inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) analysis using the full, unmatched cohort to maximise generalizability; (3) a combined IPTW AT analysis; and (4) an analysis using a composite MALO endpoint that included hepatocellular carcinoma. Finally, we evaluated the risk of bone fracture as a negative control outcome to assess for unmeasured confounding.
All analyses were performed within the  All of Us  Researcher Workbench using Python programming language (version 3.10.16; Python Software Foundation), utilising pandas (version 2.0.3) for data management, lifelines (version 0.30.0) for survival analysis, scikit‐learn (version 1.0.2) for multiple imputation, and statsmodels (version 0.14.4) for regression modelling. All hypothesis tests were 2‐sided, and a  p  value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Study Population and Baseline Characteristics:
From an initial pool of 17 751 adults with Type 2 diabetes initiating either medication, 13 157 met the inclusion criteria. Propensity score matching resulted in a final analytic cohort of 7854 participants, with 3927 individuals in each treatment group (Figure  1 ). In the matched cohort, the mean (SD) age was 57.3 (10.4) years, and 4622 participants (58.8%) were female. The study population was racially diverse, comprising 1944 Black or African American participants (24.8%), 3640 White participants (46.3%), 110 Asian participants (1.4%), and 2160 participants (27.5%) classified as other or unknown race. The cohort exhibited a substantial burden of metabolic comorbidities; approximately 58% of participants had obesity, 82% had hypertension, and 77% had dyslipidemia. Baseline liver fibrosis severity, as measured by the FIB‐4 index, was well‐balanced between groups with a mean of 1.2. Prior to matching, participants initiating GLP‐1RAs were younger, had a higher BMI, and were more frequently treated with insulin compared with those initiating DPP4is (Table  S2 ). Following stratified propensity score matching, these differences were attenuated, and all measured sociodemographic, clinical, and laboratory characteristics were well balanced between the groups (standardised mean differences < 0.1) (Table  1 ). Missingness rate for all covariates is provided in Table  S3 .

Primary Outcome:
During the study follow‐up period, 152 hepatic decompensation events were identified in the matched cohort. The median duration of follow‐up was shorter in the GLP‐1RA group (2.62 years) compared with the DPP4i group (5.13 years) (Table  S4 ). In the primary ITT analysis, initiation of a GLP‐1RA was associated with a significant reduction in the risk of hepatic decompensation compared with DPP4i use (HR, 0.67; 95% CI, 0.47–0.95;  p  = 0.026). This corresponded to an absolute rate difference of −1.66 events per 1000 person‐years (95% CI, −2.99 to −0.33). Kaplan–Meier survival estimates showed that the cumulative incidence curves for the two treatment groups began to diverge approximately 1 year after index, with the GLP‐1RA group maintaining a lower cumulative incidence of hepatic decompensation throughout the 5‐year observation period (Figure  2 ).

Secondary Outcomes:
When analysing the individual components of the primary composite endpoint, GLP‐1RA use was associated with a lower risk of ascites (HR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.46–1.01) compared with DPP4i use, although statistical significance was marginal (Figure  3 ). The risk of hepatic failure was also lower in the GLP‐1RA group (HR, 0.60; 95% CI, 0.30–1.21), although the confidence interval crossed unity. Analyses for oesophageal variceal bleeding and hepatic encephalopathy were inconclusive due to the low number of observed events (< 10 events in GLP‐1RA group). The risk of all‐cause mortality was numerically higher among GLP‐1RA users (HR, 1.30; 95% CI, 0.78–2.18), but this association was not statistically significant.
In longitudinal analyses of metabolic parameters, both treatment groups exhibited significant reductions in body weight over the 5‐year follow‐up period. However, linear mixed‐effects models revealed a significant treatment‐by‐time interaction ( p  < 0.001), indicating distinct trajectories of change. While the DPP4i group demonstrated a progressive decrease in body weight from baseline, the GLP‐1RA group achieved a significantly greater magnitude of weight loss that was sustained throughout the observation period (Figure  4 ). Similar trajectories were observed for BMI (Figure  S2 ).

Subgroup Analyses:
The association between GLP‐1RA use and reduced risk of hepatic decompensation was generally consistent across prespecified subgroups defined by age, sex, race and ethnicity, and dyslipidemia ( p  > 0.05 for interaction) (Figure  S3 ). However, significant heterogeneity was observed by diabetes duration ( p  = 0.045 for interaction), with a lower risk observed in patients with a diabetes duration of ≥ 5 years. Significant heterogeneity was also observed by baseline BMI group ( p  = 0.01 for interaction), with the reduced risk being most pronounced in the BMI 30.0–34.9 kg/m 2  subgroup.
In analyses stratified by individual GLP‐1RA agents, semaglutide was associated with the most pronounced risk reduction (HR, 0.21; 95% CI, 0.05–0.92). Estimates for exenatide (HR, 0.35; 95% CI, 0.14–0.88) were also significant. However, estimates for dulaglutide (HR, 0.98; 95% CI, 0.49–1.96) and liraglutide (HR, 0.88; 95% CI, 0.50–1.53) were not statistically significant (Figure  S4 ).

Sensitivity Analyses:
Results from sensitivity analyses were consistent with the primary analysis (Figure  S5 ). The HR for hepatic decompensation was 0.61 (95% CI, 0.33–1.15) in the AT analysis. In this analysis, the median duration of drug exposure was 341 days (IQR 206–686) for the GLP‐1RA group and 364 days (IQR 182–801) for the DPP4i group (Table  S5 ). The HR was 0.77 (95% CI, 0.58–1.01) in the IPTW analysis involving the full cohort. The association also persisted when defining the outcome as a MALO, which included hepatocellular carcinoma (HR, 0.70; 95% CI, 0.50–0.99). In the negative control analysis, GLP‐1RA use was not associated with the risk of bone fracture (HR, 1.10; 95% CI, 0.97–1.24).

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
In this retrospective cohort study utilising the  All of Us  Research Program dataset, we found that initiation of GLP‐1 receptor agonists in adults with Type 2 diabetes was associated with a 33% reduction in the risk of hepatic decompensation compared with DPP‐4 inhibitors. This inverse association was generally consistent across demographic subgroups, although heterogeneity was observed by diabetes duration and baseline BMI and was accompanied by significantly greater and sustained weight loss. Our findings complement trial evidence by evaluating a clinically meaningful outcome in routine clinical practice.
Our findings extend the current evidence base by observing this reduction of hepatic decompensation risk in a cohort specifically enriched for populations historically underrepresented in biomedical research. While recent large‐scale observational studies have reported such reductions associated with GLP‐1RA use [ 11 ,  15 ,  16 ], these investigations were often limited by a lack of racial and ethnic diversity. For instance, Kanwal et al. reported similar benefits in a national cohort of Veterans Affairs patients, but their study population was predominantly male (89%–94%) and White (76%–83%) [ 11 ]. Similarly, other major US studies involved cohorts that were approximately 65% to 67% White, with limited granularity regarding other racial groups [ 15 ]. In contrast, our study is consistent with these findings in a demographically distinct population comprising 58.8% female participants and a substantial proportion of racial and ethnic minority groups, including 24.8% Black or African American participants. By extending these observations to a diverse setting, our results suggest that this association of GLP‐1RAs may be generalizable across a broader demographic spectrum.
The biological plausibility of our findings is supported by the substantial and sustained reductions in body weight and BMI observed in the GLP‐1RA group over the 5‐year follow‐up period. Obesity, Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and insulin resistance are central drivers of MASLD and MASH pathophysiology [ 25 ,  26 ,  27 ,  28 ]. Accordingly, calorie restriction and weight loss are recognised as the cornerstone of management [ 29 ], offering a direct therapeutic benefit by resolving hepatic steatosis and inflammation, reversing fibrosis, and ultimately preventing progression to decompensation [ 30 ,  31 ,  32 ,  33 ]. By achieving significant weight reduction compared with the DPP‐4 inhibitors, GLP‐1RAs may mitigate the metabolic insults that fuel liver injury [ 34 ]. Beyond weight loss, preclinical and clinical data suggest that GLP‐1RAs are associated with improved insulin sensitivity and anti‐inflammatory effects, potentially offering benefits that are complementary to their weight‐lowering properties [ 35 ,  36 ].
Our analyses revealed notable heterogeneity in the risk reduction based on specific agents and clinical characteristics. Regarding individual drugs, semaglutide was associated with the most pronounced inverse association (HR, 0.21). While this aligns with clinical trial data demonstrating its superior potency in inducing weight loss and resolving MASH, caution is warranted in interpreting this magnitude. Given the more recent market entry of semaglutide compared with DPP4is and earlier GLP‐1RAs, the median follow‐up time for this subgroup is inherently shorter. Consequently, this estimate may reflect an insufficient latency period to capture late‐onset decompensation events, in addition to potential unmeasured confounding such as socioeconomic factors. We also observed a significant association for exenatide (HR, 0.35), whereas estimates for dulaglutide and liraglutide were not statistically significant. Additionally, significant heterogeneity was observed by diabetes duration ( p  = 0.045 for interaction), with a more pronounced risk reduction in patients with established disease (≥ 5 years). We also observed a significant interaction by baseline BMI ( p  = 0.01 for interaction), with the inverse association being most consistent in the obesity class I category (BMI 30.0–34.9 kg/m 2 ). This finding implies that the reduced risk of hepatic decompensation associated with GLP‐1RAs may be dependent on the severity of metabolic burden, potentially showing a more pronounced inverse association when initiated before systemic metabolic dysregulation becomes refractory to standard‐dose therapy. Consequently, future research should prioritise stratified analyses to better define the optimal timing of GLP‐1RA initiation within the metabolic disease continuum.
To evaluate the robustness of our primary ITT findings, we conducted several prespecified sensitivity analyses. While these yielded directionally consistent estimates, statistical significance and effect sizes varied, reflecting inherent differences in analytical estimands and cohort characteristics. In the AT analysis, the HR for hepatic decompensation was 0.61; however, statistical significance was attenuated. This is primarily attributable to a substantial loss of statistical power, as the number of observed events decreased markedly upon censoring participants at the time of treatment discontinuation. Furthermore, the IPTW analysis in the full cohort yielded an attenuated HR of 0.77. This shift is attributable to the incorporation of the unmatched population and evaluation of the treatment association across a broader, more heterogeneous overall cohort, introducing potential residual confounding compared with our stringently matched primary sample. Importantly, the persistence of the inverse association when expanding the outcome to include hepatocellular carcinoma, alongside the null finding in our negative control analysis for bone fracture, bolsters the internal validity of the primary ITT estimate.

Limitations:
Limitations of the study must be acknowledged. First, as an observational study, we cannot establish causality, and residual confounding may persist despite propensity score matching and robust sensitivity analyses. Second, our definition of hepatic decompensation relied on diagnostic codes and electronic health record data rather than liver histology or elastography; while this approach is standard for large database research, it may result in misclassification or underascertainment of milder cases of liver disease. However, we aimed to mitigate this limitation by employing a rigorous active comparator design with DPP4is. This approach minimises detection bias and accounts for unmeasured confounding related to healthcare access and provider contact, thereby reinforcing the internal validity of our findings. Third, the median duration of follow‐up was unequal between groups (2.62 years for GLP‐1RAs and 5.13 years for DPP4is). This difference might be attributed to prescribing trends, as widespread adoption of GLP‐1RAs is a more recent phenomenon than that of DPP4is. Although censoring rates due to loss to follow‐up were comparable, the shorter observation window for GLP‐1RA users may limit the detection of late‐onset events. Fourth, the median duration of drug exposure (approximately 1 year) was shorter than the median follow‐up period. This discrepancy could have introduced exposure misclassification which likely biases the ITT estimate toward the null. However, the primary ITT analysis captures the net clinical benefit of the initial prescribing decision. The null statistical significance observed in the AT analysis (HR 0.61;  p  = 0.13) is likely attributable to reduced statistical power as event numbers decreased (from 152 to 45 events), rather than a lack of association. The persistence of the reduced risk in the ITT analysis despite treatment discontinuation suggests that the initial treatment decision may be associated with longer‐term differences in risk. Fifth, missing data for baseline covariates, particularly BMI (approximately 33% missing), is an inherent limitation of real‐world EHR data. We addressed this by utilising multiple imputation by chained equations to minimise selection bias, but the potential for data not missing at random remains. Finally, while we assessed all‐cause mortality, the current  All of Us  dataset lacks cause‐of‐death registry data, preventing a specific analysis of liver‐related mortality. Future studies with enhanced mortality linkages are needed to investigate this endpoint.
