[SECTION: METHODS]
We conducted a pragmatic, prospective, observational, real-world cohort study in a tertiary care—academic hospital to evaluate healthcare outcomes in PWT2D during their inpatient admission between June 2024 and February 2025, according to STROBE guidelines. This study began after the center started using the new ENDOCARE CPG for the comprehensive management of hospital hyperglycemia. In brief, the CPG was developed with input from the main medical departments involved in the care of PWT2D and cardio-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome [ 11 ]. It includes a detailed start-stop protocol for the proper in–hospital use of oral antidiabetic drugs (OADs), requiring reassessment of the clinical situation every 24 h. The ENDOCARE protocol thus incorporates a proactive safety framework for surgical patients; as illustrated in Fig.  1 , specific 'STOP' criteria mandate the timely discontinuation of SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 RAs before surgical procedures, to minimize risks such as euglycemic ketoacidosis or gastrointestinal dysmotility. The protocol was designed for implementation by any qualified medical and nursing professional within the center. Practical access to the CPG is ensured by its online availability via the institution’s platforms, physical distribution to nursing stations and medical offices, and direct digital access through smartphone-scannable QR codes. A detailed guide about the use of adjuvant insulin is also included (see Fig.  1 ). We consecutively sampled all PWT2D aged over 18 years admitted to medical and surgical hospital wards. Patients were categorized into two main groups based on their glucose-lowering therapy during hospitalization:
Group A included patients who were either maintaining pre-admission OADs (including SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 receptor agonists) or had these therapies initiated during their inpatient stay. This group could also include patients with or without baseline insulin or multiple insulin doses. Although the protocol for inpatient management prioritized the initiation of oral GLP-1 receptor agonists due to their pharmacokinetic advantages and simplified administration, the maintenance of pre-admission subcutaneous GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy was permitted. For the purpose of this study, and to streamline analyses, patients continuing subcutaneous GLP-1 receptor agonists were categorized within Group A;
Group B comprised patients on insulin-only therapy, defined as those with baseline insulin or multiple insulin doses without concomitant OADs, throughout their admission. Patients whose glycemic management during admission was limited to sliding scale insulin (variable-dose subcutaneous insulin adjusted by bedside capillary readings) were excluded.
We obtained data from electronic medical and pharmacy prescription records on a daily basis. Sociodemographic, clinical, and outpatient pharmacologic variables were collected, including an assessment of frailty by the Clinical Frailty Score in the subpopulation aged 65 years and older [ 12 ]. Related to non-insulin glucose-lowering therapies, we documented their initiation time and location (e.g., inpatient ward or emergency department), the specific type administered, and their ongoing management throughout hospitalization. This included recording whether treatment discontinuation was necessary based on reassessment and if it was subsequently restarted. Due to the study’s design and its real-world clinical practice setting, a sample size calculation was not performed. The study received approval from the Ethics Committee of the Dr Balmis General University Hospital, Alicante, Spain (2022–054).
The primary outcome was safety, which was defined as the occurrence of severe adverse events (AEs) (diabetic ketoacidosis or level 2–3 hypoglycemia) [ 13 ]. Level 2 hypoglycemia is defined as a blood glucose concentration below 3.0 mmol/L (54 mg/dL) and level 3 hypoglycemia is characterized by severe cognitive impairment and/or physical alteration that requires assistance from another person for recovery, regardless of a specific blood glucose measurement. We also reported on mild-to-moderate adverse events, including level 1 hypoglycemia (defined as a blood glucose concentration of less than 3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) but not less than 3.0 mmol/L (54 mg/dL)), oral intolerance, clinical hypotension (dizziness, presyncope, and syncope), ketotic hyperglycemia [ 13 ], a decrease in eGFR (CKD-EPI) > 30% and lactic acidosis. Secondary outcomes evaluated effectiveness in terms of metabolic control (median daily capillary blood glucose) and glycemic variability (median daily standard deviation). As an exploratory analysis, we also reported hospital stay, ICU requirement, and mortality.
Given the low proportion of missing data, analyses were conducted using a complete-case approach. Non-normal quantitative data were reported as medians (IQRs), qualitative data as frequencies. Comparative analyses between groups were performed using the Mann–Whitney test and the chi-squared test, as appropriate. Multiple logistic regression was used to evaluate the adjusted odds of outcomes according to the group (adjusted Odds Ratio [aOR], 95% confidence interval [CI]). Variables showing statistical significance in bivariate analyses were included as covariates, with additional adjustment for sex and age over 65 years, which were considered essential clinical confounders.
To address baseline imbalances and mitigate potential confounding by indication, propensity scores (PS) were calculated for each participant within the overall cohort using a multivariable logistic regression model. This model incorporated a comprehensive set of pre-treatment covariates known to influence treatment assignment and/or clinical outcomes, specifically: age, biological sex, diabetes progression time, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, chronic kidney disease, advanced cardio-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome, outpatient insulin or SGLT2 inhibitor use, HbA1c, and inflammatory burden (e.g., C-reactive protein). Subsequently, a subpopulation was identified by matching participants on their estimated propensity scores using a 1:1 nearest neighbour algorithm with a caliper of 0.1 standard deviations of the logit of the PS, without replacement. Covariate balance in this matched subpopulation was rigorously assessed using standardised mean differences. Then, a PS multivariable logistic regression was fitted for outcomes; odds ratios (OR) with 95% confidence intervals [95% CI] were estimated for the association between groups and outcomes. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05. The analyses were performed using SPSS Statistics 25 (IBM, Armonk, NY, USA).

[SECTION: RESULTS]
From the initial cohort of 1536 PWT2D admitted during the study period, 557 were under sliding scale insulin alone. A total of 979 (Group A 582 and Group B 397) were finally included in the study. The median age was 74 (65–81) years, 35.3% were women, with a median BMI of 27.8 (24.3–31.9) kg/m 2 , with 86.4% presenting with CKM syndrome in stage 3 or above and 39.2% frailty. Outpatient therapy included insulin in 44.6%, SGLT2 inhibitors in 63.7%, and GLP-1 RAs in 18.1%. There were no significant differences in age, gender, BMI or frailty between the groups. Group A showed more heart failure, cardiovascular disease, CKM4a, and higher FIB-4, plus less outpatient insulin use and greater SGLT2 inhibitor use. OADs were started on the hospital ward in 98.3% of patients, with 81% initiated within 48 h of admission. The drugs prescribed included 438 SGLT2 inhibitors and 66 GLP-1 RAs (of which 61 were oral semaglutide). Specifically, 403 patients received only SGLT2 inhibitors, 31 received only GLP-1 RAs, and 35 received both drug classes. Additionally, 119 patients received metformin and 87 received DPP4 inhibitors. Adjuvant insulin therapy was used in 38.4% of cases in Group A. Once initiated or maintained at hospitalization, OAD was stopped in 13.7% of subjects and restarted in 4.7% of them during follow-up.

[SECTION: CONCLUSIONS]
In real hospital settings, the use of non-insulin therapies, predominantly SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 RAs guided by a start-stop CPG, was associated with a safe and effective glycemic management profile.

Research insights:
What is currently known about this topic? 
 Insulin monotherapy is standard for most hospitalized T2D patients. SGLT2i and GLP-1 RAs are routinely discouraged due to safety concerns. Real-world inpatient outcome data remain scarce.
What is the key research question?
Are hospital SGLT2i/GLP-1 RAs safe and effective when used with guidance and start-stop criteria?
What is new? 
 In hospital non-insulin glucose-lowering therapies reduce hypoglycaemia risk. Achieve superior glycemic control with reduced variability. Shorten hospital stay without increased ICU admission or mortality.
How might this study influence clinical practice?
Our proof of concept suggests reevaluating standard practices for early in-hospital SGLT2i/GLP-1RA use.

Primary outcome—safety:
A total of 73 severe AEs were reported, 72 level 2–3 hypoglycemia and 1 diabetic ketoacidosis. Group A was found to be independently associated with a lower risk of severe AEs (aOR 0.53 [95%CI 0.29–0.97]), primarily due to the lower incidence of hypoglycemia. Sensitivity analyses using eGFR cut-off points of < 45 or < 30 mL/min/1.73m 2  did not alter the main findings. This remained true in the subpopulation aged 65 and over, adding the adjustment for frailty (aOR 0.44 [95%CI 0.24–0.80]) (see Fig.  2 ). To address potential heterogeneity of exposure within Group A, a sensitivity analysis compared patients who continued pre-admission non-insulin therapies (N = 462) with those who initiated them de novo during hospitalization (N = 120). Patients in the de novo group experienced a significantly lower risk of level 1 hypoglycemic episodes (OR 0.32 [95%CI 0.13–0.83]), with no significant differences observed in level 2 or 3 episodes (OR 0.32 [95%CI 0.09–1.05]). Notably, a single event of diabetic ketoacidosis was recorded in Group A. This case occurred in a patient with metastatic cancer and sepsis, representing a major deviation from the “STOP-START” protocol, which mandates the temporary discontinuation of SGLT2 inhibitors during acute illness or septic states. Excluding this protocol violation, no other cases of DKA were observed. With regard to mild-to-moderate AEs, there were only differences in level 1 hypoglycemic episodes. Secondary outcome—effectiveness:
Figure  3  illustrates the differences in glycemic control and variability between the groups. The median of daily capillary blood glucose level was lower in Group A (153 (IQR 128–187) vs. 179 (IQR 148–225.5) mg/dl,  p  < 0.001) (see Fig.  3  and Table  2 ), and this difference persisted throughout all time periods and in the subpopulation with eGFR < 60 ml/min/m 2 . There was also lower glycemic variability in Group A (27.9 (IQR 10.4–49.7) vs. 43.1 (IQR 12.7–68.5) mg/dl,  p  < 0.001). To address potential heterogeneity of exposure within Group A, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to compare patients who continued pre-admission non-insulin therapies with those who initiated treatment de novo during hospitalization. No significant differences were observed between these two subgroups. Furthermore, both Group A subgroups demonstrated superior glycemic control relative to Group B, characterized by significantly lower median capillary blood glucose levels (continued 150 (127–180), de novo 162 (134–206), Group B 179 (148–225) mg/dl) and reduced glycemic variability (continued 36 (22–55), de novo 42 (29–60), Group B 55 (41–72) mg/dl) ( p  < 0.03, for all comparisons against Group B). Exploratory outcomes:
Patients in group A had shorter hospital stays (5 vs. 7 days,  p  < 0.001) without differences in deferred admission to the ICU or mortality (see Table  2 ).

Sensitivity analysis—propensity score matching:
Following PS matching, 223 participants from Group A (Group A-PS) were successfully matched with 223 participants from Group B, resulting in a matched cohort of 446 individuals. A total of 47 severe AEs were reported, 46 level 2–3 hypoglycemia (11 Group A-PS vs. 35 Group B-PS) and 1 diabetic ketoacidosis. Group A-PS was found to be independently associated with a lower risk of severe AEs (OR 0.31 [95%CI 0.15 to 0.61]), primarily due to the lower incidence of hypoglycemia. With regard to mild-to-moderate AEs, there were only differences in level 1 hypoglycemic episodes.
The inclusion of admission reasons in the propensity score matching to address potential confounding by indication did not alter the significant reduction in severe AEs associated with Group A-PS (OR 0.36 [95% CI 0.18–0.72]).
The median of daily capillary blood glucose level was lower in Group A-PS (167 (IQR 134–217) vs. 179 (IQR 146–229) mg/dl,  p  = 0.03). There was also lower glycemic variability in Group A-PS (46.5 (IQR 26.7–64.5) vs. 57.1 (IQR 41.2–75.3) mg/dl,  p  < 0.001).
Patients in group A-PS had shorter hospital stays (6 vs. 7 days,  p  < 0.001), without differences in deferred admission to the ICU (eight in each group) or mortality (four in each group).

[SECTION: DISCUSSION]
In real hospital settings, the use of non-insulin therapies, including SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 RAs, guided by CPG frameworks and start-stop criteria, appears to be safe and effective. These therapies are associated with a lower risk of severe AEs, better capillary glucose control, and reduced variability than monotherapy insulin. This approach is also associated with shorter hospital stays.
Inpatient hyperglycemia is a common challenge, affecting approximately one-third of hospitalized patients [ 14 ]. Traditionally, OADs are held during hospitalization, with insulin therapy becoming the standard of care. However, recent evidence questioning this practice, coupled with the expanding understanding of OAD safety and efficacy [ 5 – 7 ,  10 ], underscores the need for alternative approaches. While some retrospective studies have shown the safety and effectiveness of OADs in hospitalized PWT2D with fewer comorbidities, these often predated or did not include SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 RAs [ 15 ].
Data regarding the safe and effective use of SGLT2 inhibitors in the inpatient setting, beyond their demonstrated benefits in heart failure patients (with or without diabetes), remain limited for general hyperglycemia management [ 5 ,  8 ]. Although one nationwide cohort study linked continued SGLT2 inhibitor use during hospitalization to lower mortality, no increased acute kidney injury, and shorter length of stay, it did not report on glycemic control outcomes [ 16 ]. Consequently, current guidelines predominantly recommend basal-bolus insulin for most hospitalized PWT2D without heart failure, or DPP4 inhibitors for mild hyperglycemia, often discouraging SGLT2 inhibitors [ 17 – 20 ]. The American Diabetes Association suggests using SGLT2 inhibitors in PWT2D with heart failure only after recovery from acute illness and in the absence of contraindications [ 9 ]. Specific recommendations for GLP-1 RAs or metformin in this setting are generally lacking.
A primary concern regarding inpatient SGLT2 inhibitor use is the risk of diabetic ketoacidosis, which clinical trials may underestimate in real-world scenarios [ 21 ]. Our findings do not support the main concerns about the risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (including euglycemic), reduced glycemic efficacy with worsening renal function, or hypovolemia [ 19 ]. The implementation of guiding tools for proper use, alongside appropriate adjuvant insulin therapy when necessary, appears to mitigate these risks; notably, a single diabetic ketoacidosis event occurred in a cancer patient with sepsis, representing a deviation from our protocol. No other cases of diabetic ketoacidosis were observed, suggesting that the risk of this complication is minimal when the clinical practice guideline is strictly followed.
A critical therapeutic goal in inpatient care is reducing hyperglycemia while preventing iatrogenic hypoglycemia [ 9 ]. The OADs use inside the ENDOCARE framework is associated with a nearly 50% reduction in level 2–3 hypoglycemia, improved metabolic control, and decreased glucose dispersion compared to insulin monotherapy. To our knowledge, this is the first real-world, large-inpatient cohort study showing improved glucose control and reduced clinically relevant hypoglycemia with the use of several OADs.
If we focus on risk associated factors, outpatient insulin use and frailty (in older adults) were independently associated with higher odds of severe AEs. The imbalanced outpatient insulin use between groups might suggest reduced beta-cell function in those individuals, a notion supported by the lower C-peptide values observed in Group B subjects where this data was available. The association between low fasting C-peptide levels and clinically relevant hypoglycemia, along with increased glucose variability in both type 1 and type 2 diabetes, is well-documented in the literature [ 22 ]. Frailty, typically characterized by multimorbidity, poor physiological reserve, and limited resistance to physiological insults [ 23 ,  24 ], acts also as a strong risk factor. Consequently, adjustment for these robust risk factors further strengthened the association between Group A and severe AEs.
Our study has several limitations inherent to its observational design. Despite accounting for numerous measured confounders, we acknowledge that unmeasured confounding, selection bias, and limited external validity due to the single-centre setting could not be entirely mitigated. Furthermore, while adjusted logistic regression models consistently demonstrated statistically significant between-group differences, the non-randomized nature of the study means that residual confounding by indication remains a concern. Nevertheless, the concordant findings from both the propensity score-matched subpopulation and the multivariable-adjusted models in the global cohort substantially reinforce the robustness of our results and considerably reduce the risk of selection bias. In addition, the sequential implementation of the ENDOCARE guideline without a contemporaneous control group raises the possibility of temporal and protocol-related effects. However, the consistency of the results after additional adjustment for calendar time suggests that secular trends or progressive implementation effects are unlikely to fully explain the observed associations. Nonetheless, increased clinician engagement or protocol-driven changes in care, including a Hawthorne-type effect, cannot be completely excluded. Another limitation of our study is the inability to assess adherence to the ENDOCARE protocol, which could introduce unmeasured confounding related to variations in clinical practice. Also, our study was limited by the absence of routine serial ketonemia measurements in patients on SGLT2 inhibitors, as these are typically performed only with suggestive clinical symptoms or frank hyperglycemia. A potential limitation is the absence of documented minor gastrointestinal symptoms. While no formal GI adverse events were recorded, we cannot entirely exclude subclinical or mild symptoms that did not warrant clinical intervention. Nonetheless, the overall tolerability suggests that inpatient initiation of these therapies does not significantly compromise oral intake.
Taken together, these findings should therefore be interpreted as real-world proof of concept. Definitive causal inference and comparative effectiveness assessments will require confirmation in prospective randomized clinical trials.
This study offers several notable strengths. It represents a pioneering investigation in real-world clinical settings to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of OADs, including those with established cardio-nephroprotective benefits, in inpatients. Our study design was meticulously chosen to isolate the effects of OADs by adjusting for key confounding factors. Furthermore, the findings offer preliminary clinical support for the ENDOCARE framework’s start-stop criteria, applicable to other hospitals and detailed in Fig.  1 . A critical aspect is that this hospital CPG is utilized by various medical specialties and nursing teams involved in the hospital management PWT2D, extending beyond specialized diabetes teams. This broad implementation significantly enhances its potential for widespread use in diverse hospital environments. Our sample size, including 438 PWT2D using SGLT2 inhibitors, nearly triples the number of patients typically enrolled in clinical trials evaluating these drugs at the hospital level for heart failure, which often lack metabolic control data. Finally, the inclusion of oral semaglutide as a first-line GLP-1 receptor agonist, leveraging its shorter half-life, further enhances the clinical translatability of our results and expands its potential for inpatient use.

[SECTION: CONCLUSION]
This study provides hypothesis-generating evidence supporting a transition from the traditional practice of therapeutic deferral to a more proactive inpatient management strategy. Our observations suggest that hospitalization represents a strategic opportunity to initiate SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1RAs, moving beyond acute stabilization toward the optimization of long-term cardiometabolic care. Such an approach may help address therapeutic inertia, facilitate patient education, and improve long-term medication adherence.
While the ENDOCARE ‘start-stop’ protocol serves as a feasible proof of concept for integrating non-insulin therapies into hospital settings, definitive validation through randomized controlled trials remains a necessary step before these findings can be fully translated into clinical implementation.
